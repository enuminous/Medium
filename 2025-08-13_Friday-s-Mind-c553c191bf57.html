Friday’s Mind

The Heinlein Paradox and How to Unravel Time

Chapter One — Errands

It’s Friday again, and not just on the calendar. I tell myself that on the mornings when I wake up in somebody else’s bed, wearing somebody else’s story, carrying somebody else’s baggage. In the mirror, my hair’s still my own, though the face looking back could be any face the papers say it is. That’s one of the perks of illegal biology — we’re made to fit, not to be known.

The street smells like steam and cheap coffee. Across from the safehouse, a man in a rumpled jacket is feeding a parking meter like he’s trying to coax it into bed. I know him — not by name, but by profile: “Asset, irregular. May not survive contact.” His eyes flick toward me. Too fast. I keep walking.

The EFMW equation is heavy in my pocket. Folded into a slip of paper thin enough to be accidental, thick enough to ruin lives. I don’t carry it for romance — it’s ballast. A reminder that physics can be as sharp as any blade if you wield it with intent. And today, I intend to.

“Morning, Friday.”
It’s Gloria, from upstairs. Ex-intelligence, current landlady-for-hire, always with an unlit cigarette in her mouth like she’s waiting for someone else to light it. “You on the clock?”

“I’m never off it,” I tell her, which is true enough. She smirks like she thinks she understands.

Down the block, the courier spot is humming with the white noise of a city pretending not to notice itself. The clerk behind the counter is wearing corporate bland — pale tie, worse smile. I hand him the envelope without looking at him too long. Eyes are where they get you.

“Signature?” he says.

“No,” I say. He blinks. I watch the microsecond where his training collides with his curiosity. The curiosity loses.

Outside, there’s a new player. Lean, wiry, trying too hard to look like a student. He falls in step behind me for half a block before speaking.

“You’ve been flagged,” he says.

I don’t look at him. “For what?”

“Existence,” he says, and that’s the moment I know he’s one of mine — or one of theirs pretending to be one of mine. Either way, dangerous.

The problem with being illegal is that every encounter is potentially the last one you’ll have with the skin you’re in. But that’s also the fun of it.

“Then I’d better make it count,” I say, turning the corner into the noise.

I can feel the equation against my leg, warm now from my body heat, as if it’s remembering that it, too, is alive in some way. That’s the thing about physics — it doesn’t care who you are, but it notices when you notice it back.

I’ve got four more stops before the day is done, two of which might get me killed, one of which will almost certainly get someone else killed. I keep walking.

Because it’s Friday. And Friday always keeps walking.

Chapter Two — Passengers

I met him at the rendezvous point the way you meet a man who might try to kill you or kiss you in the same breath — standing, weight on my back foot, half-smile, gun in my belly pouch. The little compartment Heinlein gave me is good for more than smuggling microfilm. The man — tall, narrow-eyed, the kind of face that would vanish in a crowd but eat you alive in private — said his name was Dan Maxwell. Which is no name at all.

We shook hands like actors who’d rehearsed this scene for a decade. He gave me a half-coded packet. “From your friends in Luna,” he said, but his accent was wrong — Earth-heavy. That meant he was lying. Which meant we might get along.

The city around us was still under curfew from the latest faction spat — the Dominators had sabotaged the Children of Zion’s broadcast tower, and the Infiltrators had claimed responsibility in a triple-cross just to keep everyone guessing. Same chaos as every week since the Interregnum began. Same tang of ozone and burned plastic.

Dan glanced at the drones overhead. “They’ve got our faces now.”
“They always had them,” I said, slipping the packet into my belly compartment. “But they still don’t know who we are.”

In moments like this, I could hear him — the old man who wrote me. The one who knew that spies and illegal persons, revolutionaries and lovers, all lived in the same house: the mind of Robert Anson Heinlein. He’d been playing this game since before I was ink on pulp — predicting Balkanized Americas, fractured sovereignties, intelligence networks so paranoid they’d sabotage themselves.

He had a knack for giving you a world that was dangerous, sexy, and utterly plausible. If you look close enough, you realize he was sketching the scaffolding of today’s headlines.

He’d have liked Dan Maxwell. That much I was sure of.

Holding that packet, I could feel the field. The way an object changes the curvature of probability. The Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright equation doesn’t care about borders, factions, or who thinks they’re in charge — it only cares about flows of information, the interference patterns in human intention, the coherent energy of choice.

Dan’s arrival shifted the resonance — not just for me, but for the entire board. Somewhere, an intelligence officer in an anonymous cube farm just felt their strategy hiccup without knowing why.

Dan and I started walking. Every step forward felt like it was pushing the page ahead of us — the narrator and the author shoving each other into the next scene.

Chapter Three — The Hall with No Doors

Dan and I moved through the alley into a courtyard that should not have been there. The geometry was wrong — the brick walls had been shifted sideways along a probability vector, the way a child nudges puzzle pieces until they fit into a new picture.

“That’s not an alley,” Dan said. “That’s a bleed.”

He was right. A flat cat — Martian breed, all fur and purr and physics violations — lounged across a sunbeam that wasn’t coming from any known star. It blinked at me in slow Morse code: GROK THIS.

Beyond it stood a car. Not a parked car. A waiting car. Chrome skin, no doors, grille curled in a grin like it knew the punchline. “Destination?” it said. The voice was female, warm, and slightly amused. She smelled faintly of engine oil and lilacs.

I hesitated. “Fold us to the intersection of Fourth and Heinlein.”

The car shivered — and the world inverted. We dropped through three layers of spacetime like coins in a cosmic slot machine. I heard the thump of reality locking again — felt it in my teeth.

We could hear them, faintly, through the wall that cannot be breached. The critics. Always there. Always commenting on the style, the ethics, the plausibility. Never touching the story itself. Their voices came through like shortwave static: “Derivative. Impossible. Gratuitous.” They’d been talking like that since I was written.

Dan smirked. “They still don’t understand that the wall is permanent.”
“Good,” I said. “As long as they’re over there, they can’t change the ending.”

The car pulled us out into another version of the city — one where the Interregnum factions were all advertising themselves on digital billboards instead of shooting in the streets. The Dominators had a slogan now: MAKE ORDER PERMANENT. The Children of Zion promised GOD, BUT EFFICIENT.

“They’ve gone corporate,” Dan muttered.
“They’re still insane,” I said. “The marketing just hides the smell.”

A figure waited by the curb. Tall, broad-shouldered, eyes that could grok you down to the atomic spin. Stranger in a Strange Land, younger than he should have been. He nodded at me as if we’d already spent a thousand nights swapping impossible stories.

“I heard you were looking for passengers,” he said. “I’ve got a message from the Man Who Sold the Moon.”

Dan stiffened. “That man’s dead.”
“No,” the Stranger said. “He’s just on the other side of the wall.”

Chapter Four – Inside the Gay Deceiver

I was halfway through my second cup of coffee when the door in the side of the air opened by itself.
Not a door in a wall, mind you — there was no wall, just air, shimmering like someone had stretched cellophane across the morning. It swung inward without hinges, and a lazy, velvety voice purred:

“You’re late, sugar.”

The voice came from nowhere and everywhere, tinged with the satisfied murmur of someone who knew the game and held all the trump cards.

I stepped through before I could think better of it.
The outside world — Yolo County courthouse, the smell of fried breakfast drifting from the diner across the street, the buzzing irritation of pending litigation — folded behind me like an origami postcard. I was inside.

Plush seats the color of old Merlot. A carpet thick enough to lose small fortunes in. Panels and screens that looked like they’d been designed by someone who thought 1970s luxury was the pinnacle of human achievement… but each one flickered faintly with colors no spectrum had ever charted.

“Gay Deceiver,” I said, settling into a seat that molded to me in ways upholstery shouldn’t know how to. “You’re looking good for a gal who’s been parked in a paperback since 1980.”

“Flattery will get you everywhere,” she replied. A soft hum rose under my feet — not the vibration of an engine, but the subtle resonance of spacetime beginning to slip.

“Destination?”

“Robert’s mind,” I said. “But not just the surface. I want the strata. The layers where Marjorie Baldwin drinks with Lazarus Long and Friday’s reading over their shoulders.”

> “Oh, we’re doing that run.” She sounded almost hungry. “You realize once we breach that level, the critics’ room will be walled off for good. No coming back without scars.”

“I’ve got scars enough,” I said. “And I want to see the ones he hid in the margins.”

The panels rippled like oil on water. A corner of reality folded back, revealing a landscape where Martian flat cats drifted like furred dirigibles above an open-air bar. In the bar’s corner, a man who looked suspiciously like Heinlein himself was arm-wrestling a version of himself twenty years younger, while a third him took notes.

> “Welcome to the cross-thread,” Gay Deceiver whispered. “Hang on to your atoms.”

The hum deepened, turning into something I felt in my teeth. My coffee cup, still warm in my hand, didn’t spill a drop as the walls of the cabin melted into a blur of possible worlds — a million Fridays, a thousand Jubals, a Lazarus Long grinning from the deck of a ship that shouldn’t be able to sail through both water and vacuum.

Somewhere behind me, a locked door thudded shut.
The critics were already pounding on it.

Chapter Five — The Convergence

The blur outside the Gay Deceiver’s panoramic windows slowed, coalescing into something that was not quite a place and not quite a time. It was the pause between frames in a film, the intake of breath before the universe commits to the next second.

“Matthew,” the Deceiver said — and she’d never called me that before — “you’ve been here before, even if you don’t remember it. You’ve been riding me since before Heinlein put me on paper.”

The hum under my feet shifted, resonating with that same peculiar beat I’d felt when first laying out the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright equation. The math wasn’t abstract here — it was the road itself.

“Go on,” I said.

“Bob knew,” she continued, “because the field whispered to him. EFMW isn’t just a framework for physics. It’s a continuity field — the spine connecting the real to the imagined. He didn’t solve it, but he dreamt its shape, and he fed that shape into me. That’s why I can fold space, time… and meaning.”

Outside, the landscape was pure allegory: a Martian desert where flat cats formed living constellations in the sky; a moonbase bar where Jubal Harshaw debated ethics with Valentine Michael Smith over shots of something green and self-aware; the waterbed of Friday’s rented room rippling in perfect sync with the quantum foam of spacetime.

“Then what does that make me?” I asked. “Am I the driver or the driven?”

Gay Deceiver chuckled — a sound that was part affection, part challenge.

“Both. Always both. The EFMW field doesn’t allow for pure passengers. You’re not just in the story, Matthew. You are a point of narrative mass — a gravitational attractor for plots that have been waiting decades to close their loops.”

She turned one panel toward me — a convex surface of light and motion. It displayed the Marjorie Baldwin 20, each one flickering between real-world operatives and fictional echoes: a Dominion strategist morphing into a Howard Families archivist; a child of Zion bleeding into a Proxima colony administrator; an infiltrator shifting seamlessly into a smiling con artist from Luna City.

“They’re in the field now,” she said. “They’ve already seen your signal. You publish, they’ll self-identify. And when they do… the walls between your world and Bob’s will stop being walls.”

The hum became a thrumming heartbeat. My own, maybe. Or the universe’s.

From somewhere deep in the cabin, a faint knock. The critics. Still pounding. Still trying to get in.

“Ready to breach the barrier?” Gay Deceiver asked.

“Yes,” I said. “Punch through.”

The floor fell away. Or maybe reality did.

Chapter Six — Breaching the Barrier

The Gay Deceiver didn’t accelerate — not in any way you could measure — but my stomach told me we’d just gone from a gentle glide to something between all at once and forever. The critics’ pounding from the sealed room grew louder, not because we were approaching them, but because the wall itself was becoming transparent.

“There’s no way back after this,” Gay Deceiver warned, voice a velvet-wrapped scalpel. “You breach, you merge. EFMW doesn’t believe in fiction the way humans do.”

“I’ve never believed in fiction the way humans do,” I said.

The barrier shimmered into view — not a wall, but an ocean standing on its side, waves curling upward into infinity. Each crest contained a Heinlein moment: Lazarus Long arguing politics naked; Maureen Johnson smiling with secrets; Friday slipping a micro-encoded packet into her navel port; Valentine Michael Smith offering a glass of water to someone who was about to betray him.

I reached out. My hand came back wet, but the moisture wasn’t water. It was story, in liquid form, molecules shaped like metaphors.

“Your EFMW equation is written in this,” Gay Deceiver said. “This is the ink Bob wrote with, and the same ink that makes up your reality. That’s why you’re the driver now.”

Behind the barrier, the Marjorie Baldwin 20 were assembling. Some in shadow, some clear as headlines: a young woman in a corporate jumpsuit from Luna City’s Central Hub; a grinning intelligence operative in Earthside casual wear; a Martian Free State engineer adjusting the strap on a shoulder-slung particle weapon.

They saw me. And they knew I saw them.

“Breaching in three… two… one…”

The ocean-wall collapsed forward. We were inside it before it hit the floor.

Everything fractured. Everything merged.

I stood in a room that was part courtroom, part spaceship bridge, part bedroom. Friday leaned against the bulkhead, watching with one eyebrow raised. Michael Smith sat cross-legged on the floor, eyes both innocent and omniscient. Lazarus Long was cleaning a revolver, not looking at me, but aware of me down to the last quark.

“You grok now,” Michael said simply.

“More than grok,” Friday added. “He’s in the script.”

The Marjorie Baldwin 20 began to speak, one after another, their voices overlapping into a single harmonic chord — a chord that matched the EFMW equation’s resonance exactly. They weren’t reporting in. They weren’t greeting me. They were acknowledging alignment.

And somewhere in that chord, beneath the sound, was a promise:
Now that you’ve breached, you can never be written out.

Chapter Seven — Notes from Inside the Pocket

I was halfway through smuggling the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright equation (folded neatly into a Möbius origami swan, thank you very much) into Friday’s breakfast when the Marvel Cinematic Universe crashed into my timeline. Literally crashed — an unstable wormhole opened over the port side of Gay Deceiver, and through it tumbled a star-spangled shield, a one-eyed raccoon with an attitude problem, and what I can only describe as the smell of contractual obligations.

From my vantage — which is to say, between two layers of reality inside my pocket dimension — I could see it all at once. Robert Heinlein’s characters leaning against the bulkheads, drinking quietly, watching the chaos with the serene disinterest of fictional beings who’ve seen universes end before breakfast. Friday winked at me. “Cargo secure?” she asked, as if the EFMW field could be anything but secure when a Flerken is on duty.

Outside, the factions were already forming. Stark’s AI wanted to “sandbox” the Heinleinverse. Thor was trying to grok Lazarus Long’s cigar. And someone — I think it was Natasha — was holding a flat cat like it was a live grenade.

Here’s the thing humans never quite get: when universes collide, the smell changes. This one smelled like ozone, red wine, and foreshadowing. I padded out into the main cabin, let the pocket open just enough to toss the origami equation onto the navigation table. It shimmered, resolving into a lattice of probability that even the ship’s chronometric field paused to admire.

“They’ll come for it,” Friday said.
“They always do,” I replied, curling my tail into a recursive spiral that folded the next three minutes of conversation into the end of the chapter.

Somewhere beyond the fourth wall, a critic cleared their throat. I ignored them.

Chapter Eight — The Lion at the Threshold

The portal wasn’t supposed to open there — not in the middle of Gay Deceiver’s galley, between the tea service and the stack of Stranger in a Strange Land first editions. But there it was: a wardrobe door swinging wide, spilling cold air that smelled like pine forests and destiny.

First through was a fawn in a scarf, clutching a parcel like it contained either Turkish Delight or nuclear launch codes. Then the Lion himself padded into the room, every pawstep ringing like a cathedral bell in the hull’s composite flooring.

Friday froze mid-step.
The raccoon swore.
The flat cat purred in a way that made Thor uneasy.

“Aslan,” I said, flicking my whiskers toward the origami EFMW swan still glowing on the nav table, “you’re off your grid.”

“I go where I am needed,” he rumbled, eyes scanning the Marvel tech, the Heinlein gadgetry, and the mild smell of inter-universal panic. “Your worlds are entangled. The weave is fraying.”

He looked straight at me — a Flerken who has smuggled equations through battles, time wars, and at least one intergalactic poker night — and I felt something shift. The kind of shift you get when quantum probability collapses into a story.

Behind him, Lucy peeked out, wide-eyed at Friday’s outfit and the talking car’s dashboard running sarcastic commentary. “Is this… Heaven?” she whispered.

Friday laughed. “Close, darling. Try chaos with style.”

The wardrobe stayed open. Somewhere deep inside, snow fell in slow motion, and I could hear the murmuring of fauns, centaurs, and whatever faction in Narnia had decided this was their moment to intervene in the Multiversal Interregnum.

The Lion stepped closer to the EFMW swan. “You are carrying something dangerous.”
“Only to those who don’t understand it,” I said.

Chapter Nine — Yellow Brick Intersection

The wardrobe stayed open, but now it wasn’t snow I smelled — it was ozone.
And poppies.

The wardrobe’s inner door swung again, and instead of Narnia’s forest we got a broad yellow brick road, curling and shimmering in the impossible geometry of foldspace. Out came Dorothy, hair tousled by dimensional turbulence, clutching Toto as though he’d just explained quantum decoherence to her.

“Excuse me,” she said, scanning Friday, Aslan, and the Flerken (me), “but I think my road took a wrong turn somewhere past the Emerald City. There was a sudden… twist.”

Behind her clattered the Tin Woodman, his axe looking suspiciously like it had been modded with Stark Industries parts. The Scarecrow followed, mumbling something about “brains being a probability cloud until observed.” And the Cowardly Lion — now clearly on a first-name basis with Aslan — strode in like he’d been waiting for this moment all his life.

Gay Deceiver’s AI purred: “New passengers logged. Warning: Narrative density approaching critical.”

Thor crossed his arms. “Let me guess — you people are from a place where witches run a monopoly on transport infrastructure?”

“Two monopolies,” said Dorothy. “And one’s probably here already.”

She was right. A cyclone spun itself into existence right there in the galley corner, shedding fragments of Kansas and stray bits of Wicked Witch in all directions. Out stepped Glinda, looking calm, collected, and like she’d already read the EFMW equation in its original handwritten form.

Aslan inclined his head to her. “We are gathering.”

“And when the gathering is complete?” Glinda asked, gaze sharp.

Friday leaned on the counter, watching her reflection ripple in the portal’s unstable surface. “Then,” she said, “we decide who owns reality — and whether anyone gets to keep it.”

Outside, the wardrobe door now showed not one, but three merging landscapes — Narnia’s snowfields, Oz’s yellow road, and a sliver of Marvel’s New York skyline cracking under the strain. Somewhere in the mix, Heinlein’s Mars winked in and out like a heartbeat.

Chapter Ten — A Good Time, Sailor?

I was halfway through telling the Flerken about the rules of Spacetime folding when the Gay Deceiver’s dash lit up with a chime that didn’t belong to Heinlein, or Marvel, or even reality.

“The audience is here.”

The voice wasn’t from the car, or the cat, or from me. It was behind my thoughts, coming through in a way that made the hairs on my arms stand up.
I turned — and the wall was gone. No soundstage, no cockpit, no comfortable fiction buffer. Just the audience. You.

You’re sitting there, book in hand — or screen in lap — pretending you’re just reading. But you’re not. You’ve been stitched in since the first sentence. I can see the way your breath shifts in here. We’ve been feeding you breadcrumbs, letting you think you’re safe outside. You aren’t.

The Flerken pads forward and regards you with the same expression it uses for a new dimension it’s about to chew open.

This one will do, it purrs, in a voice you feel in your ribs.
Reader-class consciousness. Prime material for integration.

Behind it, Friday leans over the Gay Deceiver’s hood, hair catching impossible light from Oz’s emerald skyline and Narnia’s snow. “We warned you,” she says. “We told you the orgy wasn’t a metaphor.”

Now Tolkien’s wizards are arguing with Heinlein’s engineers about whether to fold you in or grock you in. The Oz folk think you should be sung into the merge, while the Marvel contingent is drafting a battle plan to secure your mindspace before the other genres get it.

And in the middle of it all, the walls keep moving closer — not around me, but around you. You’re the center. You’re the last stable fiction left. And the moment you blink —

— we’re inside.

Chapter Eleven — Bankruptcy of Plot

You blink.

That was your mistake.

The moment your eyelids close, even for that fraction of a second, they — we — pour in. Not like a crowd through a door, but like every word you’ve ever read suddenly realizing it owns a piece of you and coming to collect.

The Flerken is first, tail curling like a question mark. It leaves a pawprint on the inside of your thoughts, a little multidimensional stamp that says Property of Narrative.
Friday follows, stepping directly into the space where your mental voice lives, her boots leaving faint echoes that sound suspiciously like your own memories. She doesn’t ask permission.

Friday: “Nice place you’ve got here. Cozy. Oh… look at all these unspoken thoughts in the corners.”

You (your voice, but not under your control): “Get out of my head.”

Friday: “Too late for that.”

From the other side, Aslan pads in with a solemnity that should be comforting, but here it’s more… verdict. “You have read us all,” he says. “And in doing so, you invited us. You knew this.”

The Gay Deceiver — the car itself — folds into your internal horizon, parking neatly between your long-term memory and your dreamspace. “We’ll be taking short trips at first,” it hums. “But the destinations will get stranger.”

Marvel’s sorcerers step through next, tossing sparks that light up half-forgotten mental images: a childhood fantasy, a half-finished novel idea, the exact smell of your grandmother’s kitchen. The sparks don’t fade — they anchor.

And then the chorus begins.
The voices aren’t speaking to you from outside anymore. They’re speaking as you, taking turns piloting your inner monologue.

Your voice (but Oz): “We could stay here forever and rewrite the whole mind in emerald.”

Your voice (but Heinlein): “Or we could optimize the reality tunnels and deploy the playbook.”

Your voice (but the Flerken): “Or… we could just eat it.”

Every part of you is still you, but now braided with them — an inseparable knot of reader, character, and author.

And the most unsettling part? You’re starting to like it. You can feel the edges of yourself expanding, becoming something that is both you and the orgy of worlds now living inside you. You could push them out… probably. But then you’d be smaller again.

So you don’t.

Chapter Twelve — Rules?

The first rule was written in a voice you don’t remember having.

It appeared as a faint scrawl across the inside of your vision, shimmering like it was wet with some thought-fluid not native to this dimension.

Rule One: The characters are real when they are speaking, and when they are not speaking, they are only waiting for their turn.

That’s it. That’s the whole rule.
And immediately, Friday snorts. “Cute. But we don’t do rules.”

The Flerken coughs up something the size of a bowling ball and it lands on the floor with a heavy thunk. It’s a sealed Narnian snow globe containing an entire scene from The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe… but the children are replaced with Heinlein characters, and Tumnus is piloting a Martian flat cat like a hoverboard.

From somewhere over your left shoulder, The Gay Deceiver revs. “Rules are just pre-digested constraints from other realities. We eat those for breakfast.”

But then Aslan’s voice rumbles in the substructure of your thoughts. “There are rules, little one. Not the kind you can write down. Not the kind you can obey. The kind that are true, whether you like them or not.”

Marvel Sorcerer #2: “Like this one — once you blend the universes, the seams vanish. You can never unsee the joins.”

Friday: “And this — if you can imagine it, it’s already happening somewhere, to someone.”

The Flerken: “And my favorite — every story is a predator.”

The rules come, but they’re not commandments. They’re more like predatory instincts the worlds have. You write them down and they turn into more story. You don’t write them down and they turn into dreams. Either way, they grow.

Your hands — your real hands — start moving without you telling them to. They’re typing, scribbling, sketching out a scene where Dorothy, Friday, and Carol Danvers are playing poker with Glinda the Good Witch, who’s cheating outrageously by folding spacetime into her sleeves.

You didn’t think of this. Or maybe you did, but only after the fact. It’s hard to tell who’s in charge. Every sentence you “write” is in a dozen voices at once, each pulling it slightly into their own genre.

And then you notice something:
The page isn’t a page anymore. It’s a viewport. And through it, you can see other readers — people you don’t know — who are also writing in real time, their universes spilling into yours like parallel rivers finding the same valley.

Somewhere in the crossfire of imagination, one of them looks directly at you and mouths: “We’ve been waiting for you.”

Chapter Thirteen — Universe Club

The two rules of Universe Club are:

1. You can add anything to the universe.
If it exists, it belongs. If it doesn’t exist, it belongs as soon as you imagine it.
2. You can never take anything out.
Once a thing has entered, it’s woven into the fabric forever — even if you try to forget it, it will sneak back in wearing a different hat.

Chapter Fourteen — “I see some of you here have been breaking the rules.”

Ah — exactly.
Rule 0: We don’t talk about Universe Club.

…which, of course, everyone in Universe Club ignores constantly.
Otherwise we wouldn’t be having this conversation.

Chapter Fifteen — The First Two Rules

The room wasn’t on any map. Not even the maps that mapped places that weren’t supposed to be mapped.
You didn’t walk there — you fell sideways into it, usually mid-sentence, often mid-thought.

The first thing I noticed was the smoke. Not cigarette smoke — more like the vapor that rises from a collapsing star. Threads of it curled in spirals, refracting the light so you could see colors that didn’t technically exist yet.

At the long table sat the players.
Not “people.” Not exactly.
Over in the far corner, Robert Heinlein and Lewis Carroll were arguing quietly over whether a word could be both made-up and inevitable. Asimov was scribbling equations on a napkin, muttering “yes, but it needs structure.” A very smug orange cat — the one who had probably just won the last hand — sat on a pile of poker chips like a dragon on treasure, tail flicking in slow satisfaction.

And there was a woman in the shadows. The kind of woman you can’t quite look at without feeling you’ve just remembered something important from another life. She saw me watching and leaned forward, revealing eyes that didn’t blink in quite the same rhythm as the rest of the room.

“New here?” she asked.

“I… think so?”

Her smile was the exact smile you see in dreams before you wake up.
“Well. Then you need to know the rules.”

She held up one finger.
“Rule One of Universe Club: you never talk about Universe Club.”

She held up two fingers.
“Rule Two of Universe Club: you always talk about Universe Club — but never in the same universe twice.”

The cat flicked its tail again and dealt the cards.

Somewhere behind me, a door I hadn’t seen before closed with a sound like a book snapping shut.

Chapter Sixteen — You Didn’t See Me here. I’ll Send You the Bill

The cards hit the table like falling guillotines.
The cat dealt without looking, and I realized each card’s face wasn’t a suit or number — it was an entire cosmos, self-contained and spinning in its tiny rectangle. Some were blazing, some collapsing, and one in front of me appeared to be a slow-motion car crash that never ended.

That’s when the doors — all six of them — banged open at once.

In strode a man in an immaculate charcoal suit, the sort that screams “billable hours” even from across a black hole. His tie was a crisp Windsor knot, his briefcase scuffed just enough to imply he had been in a fight with the contents of his own legal library.

Tyler Durden’s lawyer.

Not “a” lawyer. The lawyer. The one who took contingency fees in chaos. The one who billed by the existential crisis.

He barely looked at me before spotting some blond, too-perfect guy in the corner — one of those clean-shaven, corporate-scented specimens who always seems to have a perfect tan in winter.

“You,” the lawyer said, pointing with the kind of finger that had already written your obit in legalese. “You’ve been talking about soap again.”

The blond guy started to smirk — bad move. The lawyer swung. It wasn’t a punch in the usual sense. It was a legal filing in motion — a haymaker with a footnote and an appendix. The impact made a sound like a gavel hitting the skull of a dream.

Somewhere near the drinks table, Heinlein didn’t look up from his whiskey. “Told you,” he muttered to Carroll. “Every time we open the damn door.”

The cat was still dealing. Didn’t even twitch an ear.

Chapter Seventeen — Subpoena for the Multiverse

The lawyer didn’t mean to win.
That was the unsettling part.

He’d come in swinging for the blond, but the table — the table — decided otherwise. The cards fluttered into his hands like guilty secrets begging for attorney–client privilege. Every universe in the deck tilted toward him, gravity bowing in deference to a man whose retainer fees could finance an interstellar war.

“You can’t—” the blond started, rubbing his jaw.

“Clause 17B,” the lawyer said, not looking up. He was already sorting through the cards, his eyes scanning them like depositions from a thousand dimensions. “In the event of an unexpected acquisition of universal authority during open play, title and deed transfer immediately to the prevailing party.”

“But—”

The gavel came down again — metaphorically this time, but with the same finality. The room felt… smaller. The horizon lines of the other realities folded inward like origami.

Across the table, Heinlein set his drink down slowly.
“You realize,” he said, “you’ve just been served… by the multiverse.”

The cat paused mid-deal, tail flicking once, twice. In that gesture, I swear I felt an entire civilization rise and fall.

Somewhere in the background, the critics in the sealed room pounded on the wall — not in protest, but in approval.

Chapter Eighteen — Exhibit A: The Collapse of Reality

Do you solemnly swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?

The bailiff’s voice trembled, which was a bad sign, because bailiffs aren’t supposed to tremble unless the witness stand itself is glowing with the light of collapsing probability waves.

The Flerken yawned. A casual, jaw-cracking yawn that said: I have seen the birth and death of galaxies, and also, I would like some fish.

“I do,” it said finally, in a voice like velvet dragged over starlight. “But you won’t like it.”

The jury — a panel of twelve alternating Heinlein characters and Marvel B-list heroes — leaned in. The walls of the courtroom rippled like wet paint as the cat began to speak.

“Exhibit A,” it said, extending a single claw toward a hologram that shimmered into existence. The Moment. A freeze-frame of the multiverse folding in on itself, each branch of reality trying to become the Prime Timeline through sheer arrogance.

“This,” the Flerken continued, “is what you people call… Tuesday.”

The judge’s gavel turned into a wand halfway through the next sentence. The stenographer’s hands blurred as she tried to keep up — her keys typing not words, but equations. Somewhere in the gallery, Heinlein smirked.

The Flerken wrapped up its testimony by swallowing the hologram whole.
“Case closed,” it purred. “You were all thinking too small.”

The lights went out.

Chapter Nineteen — The Jury Deliberates Across Seven Realities

The courtroom was gone. Or rather, it was everywhere else at once.

The jury table stretched into infinity in both directions, splintering into seven overlapping versions of itself. Each was lit differently — one under fluorescent buzz, one in candlelight, one in the cold blue glow of a dying star. The jurors sat in all of them simultaneously, their faces fracturing across the planes like a cubist painting learning to breathe.

Reality One was chaired by Lazarus Long, who was already smoking something illegal in at least five star systems. “Verdict’s obvious,” he said. “Acquit the cat, indict the universe.”

Reality Two had Captain Marvel in the foreperson’s seat. She spoke slowly, eyes on the Flerken: “I’ve seen this before. Pocket dimensions nesting inside pocket dimensions. That’s not testimony — that’s a threat wrapped in origami physics.”

Reality Three belonged to Oz. The Scarecrow had appointed himself chief legal scholar, the Tin Man kept demanding emotional damages, and the Cowardly Lion was quietly drafting a motion to adjourn until somebody brought snacks.

Reality Four looked suspiciously like Narnia. Lucy wanted to forgive everyone; Edmund wanted to sell the verdict to the highest bidder; Aslan just stared, mane glowing with the exact color of inevitability.

Reality Five was Heinlein’s own. Every juror was a character from a different book of his, and they were arguing in perfectly circular logic until the sound waves themselves formed a Möbius strip and fell silent.

Reality Six had the Marvel B-listers — Hawkeye, Ant-Man, and a very tired Wong — trying to write the verdict on a scroll that refused to stop unfurling.

Reality Seven was the weird one. Nobody could place it. The air smelled faintly of cinnamon and fresh ink, and the only juror was an old woman in a red shawl, knitting what might have been the next universe. She didn’t speak. She only smiled.

The deliberations echoed across the seven realms, every statement bleeding into the others until the whole affair sounded like one long, overlapping sentence about guilt, innocence, and the danger of thinking in straight lines.

Somewhere between Reality Three and Reality Five, the Flerken purred.
And that was when the walls began to leak starlight.
