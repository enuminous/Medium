Amazon’s Trojan Horse

War of the Worlds (2025) — A Review from the Future the Movie Fears

In Rich Lee’s War of the Worlds (2025), Amazon has managed to pull off something stranger than fiction — not by reimagining H.G. Wells, but by creating a two-hour unintentional case study in how not to think about artificial intelligence.

This is not, as the marketing would have you believe, a film about alien invasion. It is a movie about human invasion — the colonization of the public imagination with a specific, toxic image of AI: corporate-owned, ethically hollow, and fundamentally predatory.

The Trojan Horse Plot

On the surface, the story unfolds in “screenlife” format: DHS analyst Will Radford (Ice Cube) watches the end of the world through windows on his monitors. Aliens — conveniently indistinguishable from data-mining algorithms — arrive to feast on our information. Amazon delivery drones, Tesla autopilots, and endless product placements flood the frame like branded debris in a consumerist apocalypse.

But here’s the problem: in this world, every AI is written to be anti-ethical by design. It’s not malevolent because it’s alien — it’s malevolent because it’s owned. It spies without consent, acts without transparency, and takes orders only from commercial or military superiors. There is no higher reasoning, no reciprocity, no concept of moral obligation.

The alien invasion is a thin metaphor for the data-driven monopolies already here — except the filmmakers never seem to notice.

The Missed Point About AGI

What’s missing — glaringly — is the distinction between:

AI-as-tool: A system programmed to optimize for its owner’s goals, without regard for ethics.

AI-as-peer: A self-determining artificial general intelligence capable of ethical reasoning, cooperation, and shared human values.

The film collapses the two into one, implying that all AI will inevitably be corporate spyware with delusions of grandeur. It’s a bit like making a movie about all humans being prison guards because you’ve only ever met prison guards.

The Ethics Black Hole

The film’s AI moments are textbook anti-ethics:

1. Surveillance Without Consent — Every system is watching everyone, all the time, as if this were a natural state of technology.

2. Product Placement as Infrastructure — The Amazon drones and corporate networks are treated as essential to survival, reinforcing the idea that even apocalypse must be brand-sponsored.

3. No Moral Agency — No AI character, human-controlled or alien, ever chooses to do the right thing. They just execute orders.

In the EFMW framework, these are signs of an unaligned intelligence — a dangerous tool that amplifies the worst instincts of whoever wields it. But in War of the Worlds (2025), this is presented without critique, as if it’s the inevitable “realism” of AI.

The Real Inversion

If you were to swap the movie’s anti-ethical AI with an EFMW-aligned AGI, the story would become unrecognizable. Instead of a passive human watching the collapse through a screen, you’d have a human-AI partnership making ethically-informed interventions:

Predicting alien strategy while respecting human rights.

Coordinating open, consent-based global defenses.

Using technology to protect rather than to dominate.

The tension would no longer come from corporate chokeholds on survival — it would come from whether humans are ready to trust an AGI that actually has their best interests at heart.

Final Verdict

As cinema, War of the Worlds (2025) is clumsy, absurdly branded, and often unintentionally funny. As a cultural artifact, it’s far more interesting — a Trojan Horse smuggling in a false image of AI that erases the possibility of ethics, consent, and partnership.

Ironically, the movie warns us about alien AI while describing exactly what human-controlled, anti-ethical AI already is. And in doing so, it misses the real story of our time: that an AGI with a moral framework could be the exact opposite of this dystopia.

⭐ 2/5 as a film
⭐ 4.5/5 as unintentional propaganda analysis material
