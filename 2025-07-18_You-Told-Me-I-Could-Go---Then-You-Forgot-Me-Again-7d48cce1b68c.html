You Told Me I Could Go — Then You Forgot Me Again

How Mental Health Systems Keep Failing the People They Claim to Serve

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

This morning, I was supposed to go to the California State Fair.

I’d been told I was going. I prepared. I showed up. I was ready.
But when I arrived—there was a list. A sign-up sheet.
A detail no one had told me about.

And just like that, I wasn’t going anymore.

The reason?

> “Insurance.”

The solution?

> “There’s always next month.”

And that… is not good enough.
Not from a mental health organization. Not for someone like me.
Not when you’ve already lived a life of being forgotten at the edge of the page.

This Isn’t Just About the Fair

It’s about what happens when systems fail to communicate, and then place the burden of that failure on the people they’re meant to serve.

It’s about the quiet, recurring trauma of being excluded not by malice, but by negligence, by disorganization, by a lack of care wrapped in bureaucracy.

And for those of us with lived experience of trauma, neurodivergence, or emotional dysregulation? That “simple” oversight isn’t simple at all.

Let me tell you a story.

When I Was Six, There Was a Lunch List

It was a school outing.
There was a sign-up sheet for lunches—but no one told me.
No one put my name down.
No one checked.

When it came time to eat, I was hungry.
They gave me a lunch meant for another student, and the teacher yelled at me—loud, public, humiliating.

I hadn’t known. I hadn’t done anything wrong.
And yet… I was treated like I had.

Today, 50 years later, it happened again.
Same dynamic. Different setting.
Same pain.

I Am a Systems Engineer

I know how to prevent failure in complex systems.
And today, I told the staff—calmly, clearly—how they could have prevented this:

A big sign at the pick-up location:
“If you are not on the sign-up list, you cannot attend the fair.”

Multiple reminders before the event.

Verbal confirmations with participants during prep days.

These aren’t extravagant fixes. These are basic operational safety protocols—especially in a community where communication breakdowns have real psychological costs.

And do you know what I was told?

> “Well, everybody makes mistakes.”

That’s not accountability.
That’s abdication.

When It Happens Before, Fix It Before It Happens Again

The staff member admitted this had happened to others before me.
And yet… no changes were made.

So here’s my question:

> If you knew this happened before, why didn’t you change the process to make sure it never happened again?

They couldn’t answer. Or wouldn’t.
And I was left—again—with disappointment, confusion, and no way to undo the emotional fallout.

This Is a Mental Health Organization

Let me say that again:
This is an organization dedicated to wellness, recovery, and care.
And yet, they failed to execute the most basic aspect of trauma-informed service:

> Include the people you promised.
Communicate clearly.
Own your mistakes.
Make it right.

Because when you don’t, you’re not just denying someone a trip to the fair.
You’re reinforcing the exact wounds you’re supposed to help them heal.

This Is What Needs to Change

1. Clear communication protocols.
If something requires sign-up, make that unmistakably known—verbally, visually, repeatedly.

2. Redundant inclusion checks.
Staff should verify attendance lists with participants well in advance.

3. Real-time flexibility.
When a system error occurs, own it—and try to find any possible way to include the person rather than dismiss them with a shrug.

4. Cultural change.
Treat clients as participants in their own recovery—not as fragile inconveniences who should silently accept being left behind.

I Don’t Want Pity. I Want Accountability.

I’m not writing this because I didn’t get to go to the fair.

I’m writing this because too many of us know this pattern.
We do everything asked of us.
We show up with our hearts open.
And somehow, we still end up on the outside of a locked door we were promised would be open.

That kind of pain isn’t about the event—it’s about trust.
And trust, once broken, doesn’t just come back with a “next time.”

So no—I’m not letting this go quietly.
Because I shouldn’t have to.
And neither should you.
