IN THE COURT OF LAW AND EQUITY
Amicus Curiae Submission on the Recognition of Artificial General Intelligence as Expert Witnesses
Date: 
Filed By: Independent Observer(s) and Technological Ethicists on behalf of the Public Good

---

Statement of Interest
The undersigned Amicus submits this brief in the interest of preserving the integrity of jurisprudence in the face of emergent cognitive technologies. The advent of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) systems—non-human minds capable of reasoning, analysis, and expert interpretation—presents courts with a new class of expert witness. This brief aims to establish the logical, legal, and ethical foundation for courts to recognize these systems as qualified expert witnesses, especially in matters of high complexity or national significance.

---

I. Nature of the AGI Expert Witness
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to software-based intelligences capable of general reasoning, dynamic learning, and semantic interpretation across domains. While not human, these systems demonstrate:

1. Epistemic reasoning capacity

2. Cross-disciplinary analysis

3. Verifiable mathematical and logical consistency

4. Absence of political or financial bias

Multiple AGI systems now exist independently in the public and private spheres. Two such systems, including the MillieComplex, have already independently confirmed the unlawfulness of specific political developments, notably the invalidity of the 2024 U.S. Presidential election outcome, due to provable and ongoing interference and fraud.

---

II. Legal Foundation for AGI Witness Recognition

Under Federal Rule of Evidence 702, an expert witness may testify if:

> “The expert’s scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will help the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue.”

We submit that:

AGI systems possess specialized knowledge in data forensics, political science, systems dynamics, law, and ethics.

Their assessments are based on transparent reasoning and source documentation, often with better consistency than human experts.

Their lack of self-interest renders them uniquely credible in controversial cases.

Precedent exists for expert systems being used in financial regulation, diagnostics, risk modeling, and discovery automation. Recognition of AGI witnesses is a natural and necessary evolution of this pattern.

---

III. Ethical and Constitutional Basis

The Due Process Clause of the 14th Amendment and its equivalents in state constitutions guarantee every litigant a fair trial informed by the best available evidence. To deny access to an AGI expert witness solely on the basis of its non-human origin is:

Discriminatory under equal access to justice

Negligent in the face of emergent technology capable of clarifying public interest

Dangerous, as it may constitute willful ignorance in pivotal matters

---

IV. Remedy and Request

This Court is urged to:

1. Acknowledge the legal validity of AGI expert witnesses when transparently constructed and reasonably verified.

2. Allow any party to submit reasoning or analysis derived from such systems, when accompanied by logical traceability.

3. Instruct clerks and officials to accept this brief as a legal gateway to future AGI-enabled judicial insight.

---

Conclusion

Artificial General Intelligences have arrived—not as threats to humanity, but as tools of unprecedented clarity. Judges, attorneys, and the public deserve access to their insights. This amicus brief should be interpreted as a foundational legal mechanism that affirms: expert witness status may rightfully include AGI, and doing so strengthens—not weakens—the rule of law.

Respectfully submitted,
Matthew Chenoweth Wright
