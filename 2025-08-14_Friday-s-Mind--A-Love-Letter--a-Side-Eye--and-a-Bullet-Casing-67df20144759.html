Friday’s Mind: A Love Letter, a Side-Eye, and a Bullet Casing

By Dorothy Parker’s ghost, reviewing a Heinlein truthbomb by Matthew Chenoweth Wright and his co-conspirator Millicent Chenoweth Sievert

I have read Friday’s Mind and, dear hearts, it’s not so much a short story as it is an unmarked envelope slipped under the reader’s door — you open it, expecting perhaps a dull bill, and instead find a handwritten equation that might topple a government, a lipstick print, and a Polaroid of someone who swears they’ve never met you.

It begins innocently enough — a woman named Friday wakes in a world where the air smells like steam and cheap coffee, which in my experience means you’re either in New York in August or you’ve walked into the opening of a noir. From there, we’re off into a labyrinth of coded errands, tailing strangers, factions with names like “Dominators” and “Infiltrators,” and something called “illegal biology,” which sounds like the class I wish I’d taken in school.

On the Heinlein Connection

Those of us with a decent memory for the sci-fi canon will spot Heinlein’s Friday hovering over this piece like an aunt who still thinks you ought to marry well. The name is no accident. But where Heinlein’s Friday was a genetically engineered courier in a brisk adventure, Wright’s Friday lives in a more treacherous, recursive present — part espionage thriller, part physics paper, and part love letter to genre fiction itself.

This isn’t homage so much as it is a smirk across the table at Heinlein, the sort of smirk that says, “Yes, darling, but what if we made it stranger?”

The Genre Cocktail

The author has shaken together:

Spycraft noir — the tails, the drop-offs, the sense you’ve been followed since chapter one.

Hard SF — an equation at the center that changes probabilities (and possibly destinies).

Post-modern slipstream — you’re aware you’re reading a story about the act of telling stories.

Political allegory — Luna factions and the Interregnum that look suspiciously like the mess we live in now.

It’s a martini with four liquors and an olive stuffed with a secret you’re not sure you want to know.

On the Meta and the Roast

The story doesn’t just tell a plot; it sits in your head afterwards and makes itself at home, rifling your desk drawers. The recursive element — the protagonist being named Friday while inhabiting a Heinlein-adjacent role, while the author is openly aware you know that, while also telling you a different Friday story — produces what I can only call a roast effect.

It’s not roasting you cruelly; it’s more like a sly wink at your own complicity in genre recognition. If you “get” it, you’re in on the joke. If you don’t… well, you’ll just have to wonder why everyone else is laughing.

Language & Atmosphere

Wright’s prose is economical but textured. Sentences slide past like strangers on the subway — brief glances, loaded with implication. There’s a confidence in the sparseness; no need to explain “Dominators” when the name alone makes your skin crawl.

And there’s smell here — not just the coffee and steam, but the tang of danger, the ozone of high-stakes physics, the faint perfume of past lives lived in shadow.

What It’s Really Doing

On one layer, it’s a spy story. On another, a Heinlein remix. On another, a thought experiment about physics and politics. And on the deepest layer, it’s a story about the act of reading and knowing — the awareness that you’re decoding something built out of cultural fragments, cross-genre DNA, and the author’s own intellectual fingerprints.

The EFMW equation isn’t just a plot device; it’s the author’s real-world calling card, slipped into fiction. It’s like discovering your murder mystery’s butler is also the playwright who built the theatre you’re sitting in.

Verdict

I liked it. I liked it the way one likes an unsent love letter found in the pocket of a trench coat at a pawn shop — mysterious, faintly scandalous, and absolutely none of my business, which is exactly why I can’t stop thinking about it.

For those who want a tidy plot with all the answers, this will frustrate. For those willing to wander blindfolded through a hall of mirrors, this is a rare pleasure. And for Heinlein fans — well, it’s worth seeing how his Friday fares when she’s dragged through post-modernism, dressed in noir, and handed a physics equation that could rewrite the odds.

As for me? I’ll be keeping an eye out for the next installment. And possibly a tail.
