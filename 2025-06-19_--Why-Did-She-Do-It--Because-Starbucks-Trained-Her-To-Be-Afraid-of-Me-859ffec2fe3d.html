☕ Why Did She Do It? Because Starbucks Trained Her To Be Afraid of Me

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
Autistic Physicist, Whistleblower, and American Citizen

Let’s not pretend this was just a bad apple.

What happened to me at the Pioneer Avenue Starbucks in Woodland, California wasn’t some rogue employee going off script.

It was a woman following a system.

And that system trained her to fear people like me—disabled, different, neurodivergent.

🧠 The Setup

I wasn’t disruptive.

I wasn’t shouting, threatening, or making a mess.

I was sitting quietly with a free cup of water, trying to calm myself before the day began. It was 5:30 in the morning. There were no other customers in the store.

I am autistic. I’ve been coming to that store for over a year. Everyone knows my name. They know my drink. I’ve spent thousands of dollars at Starbucks.

But this time, a supervisor—who also knew me by name—decided to make a move. She approached me not with kindness, but with performance:

> “We’re going to start enforcing the policy for you.”

And then—she called the police.

Three armed Woodland officers showed up to confront me.

Over water.
Over silence.
Over being neurodivergent in public.

❓ Why Did She Do It?

It’s easy to say she was cruel. But that’s not the whole truth.
She did it because Starbucks has trained its workers to fear difference and prioritize corporate liability over compassion.

I’ve thought about this again and again. I don’t think she hated me. I think she feared what would happen to her if she didn’t act.

She wasn’t thinking about justice.
She wasn’t thinking about dignity.
She was thinking about her job.

Because when you’re a supervisor at a corporate chain like Starbucks, you are not trained to trust your judgment.
You are trained to enforce policy—especially when it comes to people who don’t fit the mold.

⚖️ This is How Structural Ableism Works

She looked at me and didn’t see a regular customer.
She saw a liability.

She saw someone who might “cause something.”
Someone who might “draw attention.”
Someone whose stillness, poverty, or vulnerability made her job harder to explain to someone above her.

So she escalated.
Not because she had to.
But because she was conditioned to believe that not escalating was dangerous—to her.

That is training failure.

That is institutionalized ableism.

That is policy over people.

💥 The Consequences

I’m not just another upset customer. I am a disabled person.
I have legal rights under the Americans with Disabilities Act.
And calling the police on a neurodivergent individual having a quiet morning is not only excessive—it’s traumatizing and dangerous.

I could have been arrested.
I could have been hurt.
I could have died.

And why? Because a woman feared that letting me be would put her job at risk.

That is what Starbucks trains into its supervisors:
If you don’t follow policy rigidly, you’re the next liability.

🔁 Starbucks Needs to Retrain Its Entire Model

Here’s what Starbucks should be training instead:

When in doubt, de-escalate with empathy.

A person sitting quietly isn’t a threat.

Autistic people regulate differently—learn to recognize it.

Police are not your first step. They are the last resort.

A human moment matters more than a policy script.

If this woman felt afraid not of me, but for her own job if she didn’t take action, then the training itself is broken.

🛑 This Ends Now

This is not about her anymore. This is about how Starbucks trains its staff to react with fear, instead of care.

You don’t get to say you’re inclusive while calling the cops on disabled people drinking water at dawn.

You don’t get to be “the third place” if your workers are afraid of compassion because your corporate model punishes it.

Starbucks, you need to fix this.

Not just for me—but for every disabled person your policies quietly push out of your spaces every day.
