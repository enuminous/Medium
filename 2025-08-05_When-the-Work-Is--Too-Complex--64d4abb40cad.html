When the Work Is “Too Complex”

A Disabled Inventor’s Search for an Attorney Who Won’t Walk Away

Opening: Setting the Stage

I recently experienced something I never thought I would: handing over groundbreaking intellectual property to a highly qualified intellectual property attorney, only to have the matter abruptly dropped with the explanation that it was “too complex.”

This is not a story about personal animosity. It’s a story about how a system built to protect inventors can fail them — especially when the inventor is disabled, under duress, and working at the edge of what’s technically possible.

The Invention

My work, the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) equation, is a foundational framework with applications across physics, advanced AI, and high-value engineering domains. It has been published, timestamped, and recognized in academic and technical circles. It is not speculative — it is operational science with provable, demonstrable outputs.

I also hold related intellectual property, including documented proofs of technologies with direct commercial and humanitarian value.

The Approach

After months of adversity and suppression, I believed I had found the right attorney:

  • Highly educated (PhD and JD).
  • Registered to practice before the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office.
  • Experienced in handling multi-disciplinary, high-complexity inventions.

I disclosed that I am autistic and disabled. He expressed understanding. When I mentioned I had discovered “Logos,” he audibly reacted — a recognition that told me he grasped the scale of what I was presenting.

We agreed on an NDA. He invited me to send my materials.

The Transfer

I mailed a packet containing:

  • An original letter from a major U.S. financial institution — critical, irreplaceable evidence in an ongoing matter.
  • A signed book of my authored work.
  • Supporting documents outlining EFMW and related inventions.

I sent these in good faith, believing representation had begun.

The Reversal

Within days, I received a short email: the matter was “too complex.” No reference to the materials I had sent. No acknowledgment of the evidentiary document. No offer to return my property.

Months later, after repeated requests, I finally received a message stating:

“I am not sure you ever sent me anything but whatever you sent me has long since been deleted together with all emails.”

Even then, there was no direct denial of having received the physical original. No accounting. No return.

Why This Matters

For a patent attorney with deep technical training to call something “too complex” is, frankly, implausible. These are professionals whose daily work involves interpreting and protecting cutting-edge technologies.
 When “too complex” becomes the reason, it often means something else:

  • External pressure.
  • Perceived political or legal risk.
  • Conflicts of interest.
  • Fear of the matter’s scale or implications.

For a disabled inventor, the result is the same: your property is gone, your trust eroded, and your forward motion stalled.

The Ask

I am putting this call out publicly:
 I need a qualified IP attorney — someone who will not be intimidated by complexity, scope, or pressure. Someone who can handle multi-disciplinary science, high-value intellectual property, and the realities of working with a disabled inventor facing institutional resistance.

The work is ready. The evidence is real. The stakes are high.

If you are that attorney — or know someone who is — please contact me directly.

Closing

This is not the end of my case. It is simply a turning point. If the system as it stands won’t protect inventors like me, I will use every available means to ensure the work finds protection, recognition, and the right steward in law.
