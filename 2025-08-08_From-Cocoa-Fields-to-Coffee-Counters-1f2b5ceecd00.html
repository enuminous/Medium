A recent Medium article by Ali, The Dark Secret Behind the Chocolate Industry, takes readers on a vivid journey from the glossy Swiss chocolate factories to the brutal reality of child labor in West African cocoa fields. It’s an uncomfortable truth: behind the sweetness lies a system that thrives on poverty, exploitation, and the deliberate undervaluing of human dignity.

At first glance, you might think my own ongoing battle with Starbucks over ADA rights is a completely different story. My case began when I walked into a Starbucks during a panic attack — a place I considered a “safe space” — asked for water, and instead found myself threatened with police intervention, denied accommodation, misnamed by corporate, and repeatedly shut out of any good-faith interactive process.

Two stories. Two industries. Two continents. But here’s the inevitable crossover: Starbucks is one of the largest corporate buyers of chocolate in the world.

Every mocha, every hot chocolate, every chocolate croissant, every seasonal beverage with cocoa powder — all of it ties Starbucks directly into the same global supply chain Ali described. That means the same corporate apparatus deciding whether to enforce ADA compliance at the counter is also deciding how much it will pay for cocoa, which farms it will source from, and what human rights risks it’s willing to ignore along the way.

The Common Pattern

Ali’s article lays bare how the chocolate industry keeps costs low by pushing the burden down to the farmers — often resulting in child labor, dangerous working conditions, and a cycle of poverty. The companies at the top avoid accountability by outsourcing responsibility, hiding behind layers of intermediaries, and maintaining the public image of ethical, quality-focused brands.

The Starbucks ADA story follows the same pattern, only the harm happens here, in their own stores:

  • A clear legal obligation (ADA Title III) is treated as negotiable.
  • The burden is shifted onto the vulnerable party — in my case, forcing me to “have my lawyer call” instead of simply honoring my rights.
  • The public-facing brand promises warmth, comfort, and inclusivity, while the internal operational reality is obstruction, avoidance, and retaliation.

Whether the harm is to a Ghanaian cocoa farmer’s child wielding a machete in the fields or to a disabled customer being threatened with police for sitting quietly with water, the logic is the same: people’s dignity and rights are subordinate to corporate convenience.

Why This Crossover Matters

It would be easy for Starbucks to say these are “separate issues” — supply chain ethics over here, store-level ADA compliance over there. But a company’s core values are not compartmentalized. An organization that tolerates rights violations in one part of its operations will tolerate them elsewhere, because the underlying decision-making framework is the same.

If Starbucks was committed to ethical responsibility, you would see it in both places:

  • In the upstream sourcing of cocoa, ensuring no child labor and paying farmers fairly.
  • In the downstream customer experience, ensuring disabled patrons are accommodated and respected.

When both ends show evidence of disregard, you are looking at systemic culture, not isolated incidents.

Closing the Loop

Ali’s work exposes the hidden harm behind something as ordinary as a chocolate bar. My work documents the visible harm behind something as ordinary as ordering a drink at Starbucks. Both are windows into how global brands manage the tension between their public image and their private actions.

If Starbucks cannot be trusted to honor a glass of water under the ADA, why should we trust them to police their cocoa supply chain? And if they can ignore human rights risks halfway across the world, why should we expect them to care about human rights risks sitting right across the counter?

The truth is, it’s one story — a single thread of corporate decision-making that runs from the cocoa fields to the coffee counters. The only difference is where you’re standing when you see it unravel.
