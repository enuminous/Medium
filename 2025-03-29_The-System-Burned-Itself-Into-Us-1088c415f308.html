Section 1: What Is EFMW?

EFMW stands for Emergent Force-Memory-Wave. It is a framework that models systemic motion across cognition, trust, and volatility using principles derived from physics, recursive dynamics, and human-centered complexity theory. In short: it's a map for why things change, not just how.

Unlike classical models, which treat events as isolated and linear, EFMW recognizes that feedback loops create recursive alignment. When a system is broken, the echoes of its breakdown reinforce the initial rupture until the break becomes the norm.

This is how venture-backed therapy platforms break people. And this is how.

Section 2: Capital as Intent Vector

VC-backed therapy platforms (like BetterHelp, Headway, etc.) are not care institutions. They are optimization engines. Their primary goal is to maximize user throughput, not user healing. This reorients every structural node (therapists, software, client access) around a single emergent force: scalability over sustainability.

From an EFMW standpoint, capital acts as an intent vector. Every therapist interaction becomes a data point not for insight, but for metric reinforcement.

You aren't being healed. You're being harvested.

Section 3: Feedback Loops as Burnout Engines

EFMW tracks how systems evolve through recursive feedback. Here’s what that looks like in VC therapy:

Therapists are over-scheduled.

Clients feel rushed.

Trust erodes.

Drop-off rates spike.

Platform sees drop-off.

Platform enforces more oversight.

Therapists have less autonomy.

Burnout increases.

This loop is called a cognitive dissonance inflation spiral.

EFMW predicts that once this loop passes a certain threshold, nothing can stabilize it internally. And the loop becomes visible to clients as existential discomfort.

That’s when people quit.
