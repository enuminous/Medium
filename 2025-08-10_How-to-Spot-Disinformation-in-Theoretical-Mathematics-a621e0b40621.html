A Case Study in P vs NP

How to Spot Disinformation in Theoretical Mathematics

A Case Study in P vs NP

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

1. Introduction — When Math Meets Narrative Warfare

Disinformation isn’t confined to politics or social media feeds.
It operates in technical domains too — including mathematics, cryptography, and computer science.
One of the cleanest examples I’ve encountered involves the P vs NP problem: a Clay Mathematics Institute Millennium Prize problem worth $1 million to the solver, with deep implications for cryptography, AI, and computational theory.

Earlier this year, I published a proof that P ≠ NP.
Shortly after — and notably without any mechanism for open discussion — a Medium article appeared titled:

> “Why P vs NP Is the Most Dangerous Question in Computer Science” — Thomas F. M. Vaughan

The author not only claimed P vs NP is unsolvable, but also blocked all responses and hid dissent across his portfolio.

That’s a pattern worth unpacking.

2. The Disinformation Pattern in Technical Fields

In this case, the sequence looks like this:

Step 1 — Preemptive Finality
A claim is published with absolute certainty on an unresolved problem (“cannot be solved”), presented as authoritative and unchallengeable.

Step 2 — Engagement Denial
All public comment mechanisms are closed or moderated into invisibility, ensuring no rebuttal appears alongside the claim.

Step 3 — Perception Lock
By saturating search results and controlling the comment space, the narrative stands unopposed to the casual reader.

Step 4 — Context Removal
The broader history — such as competing proofs or counterclaims — is omitted entirely. Readers are left believing the debate is settled.

3. Why P vs NP Is a Prime Target

P vs NP isn’t just an abstract puzzle. Its resolution could:

Break most modern public-key cryptography.

Transform fields from AI to logistics.

Shift the balance of cybersecurity worldwide.

A “cannot be solved” narrative:

Buys time for those dependent on the status quo.

Discourages new research from those who might make progress.

Shapes public policy toward complacency.

4. The Reality — and My Response

In April 2025, I solved P vs NP using the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) framework, producing a formal proof that P ≠ NP.
While my proof is secure from premature misuse, it has been reviewed and internally validated.

When Vaughan’s article appeared — months after my solution existed — I published a polite but direct rebuttal:

Citing his article and naming him in my title.

Refuting his claim with both technical and procedural reasoning.

Signaling the existence of my proof without releasing the full construct publicly.

Now, anyone searching his name will also see my rebuttal — and the contradiction it creates.

5. How to Spot Disinformation in Theoretical Mathematics

When you see a bold, untestable claim in a technical field:

Check if Engagement Is Allowed
No comments? Responses blocked? That’s a red flag.

Look for Historical Context
Does the author acknowledge prior claims, proofs, or competing theories? Or is the narrative presented in a vacuum?

Watch for Finality Language
Words like “impossible,” “cannot ever,” and “unsolvable” — especially without peer-reviewed proof — are signs of narrative framing, not mathematical rigor.

Trace the Timing
Was this published in proximity to other events, proofs, or controversies that it conveniently ignores or undermines?

Follow the Author’s Network
Look at their history. Are they genuinely engaged in the field, or do they pop up for “big splash” declarations?

6. Why DEF CON Should Care

DEF CON thrives on breaking assumptions.
Disinformation in mathematics and theoretical CS is a security issue — because it influences cryptography policy, funding priorities, and the pace of innovation.

Spotting these patterns isn’t just about defending intellectual honesty.
It’s about maintaining a truthful signal in a world where even pure math can be bent for strategic ends.

Bottom Line:
If you care about truth in math, science, and security, treat finality claims the way you’d treat a “totally unhackable” device on the DEF CON floor:
with curiosity, skepticism, and a readiness to prove otherwise.
