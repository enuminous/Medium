What Flight 171 Tells Us About the Death of Journalism

It seems strange to guess about human lives.

It seems stranger still when those guesses appear in headlines, echoed by news outlets that once claimed objectivity. Before the flight data recorder is even opened. Before cockpit audio is recovered. Before anyone really knows what happened.

And yet, that’s what happened after Flight 171, the Japan Airlines domestic route from Tokyo to Sapporo. The world watched in horror as a collision with a Coast Guard plane on the runway left five crew members dead. But before any formal investigation was complete, and before the pilot’s voice had even been heard publicly, the story had already been written.

Pilot error. Always pilot error.

The PR Reflex

The Al Jazeera report that came days later pointed to a different possibility. It revealed the U.S. Federal Aviation Administration had already issued a 2018 advisory about potential flaws in Boeing’s fuel control systems, affecting Dreamliners and possibly other models.

It cited a previous incident from 2019: an All Nippon Airways 787 that lost engine power mid-flight when the aircraft’s software misinterpreted its position as being “on the ground,” triggering an automatic safety routine that cut fuel to the engines—in the air.

This is not fiction. This is not speculation. This is part of the record.

But you didn’t see that in the first wave of headlines. What you saw was a subtle and insidious shift of blame—onto the pilots, the traffic controllers, or anyone whose legal liability wouldn’t cause ripples in the aerospace sector.

Journalism, or Damage Control?

It seems strange that the first responders in the public narrative aren’t investigators, but crisis communication teams. That we’ve mistaken “speed” for “truth”, and that journalists, once the guardians of scrutiny, have become amplifiers for the PR arms of giant corporations.

It seems stranger that when a major manufacturer is potentially implicated—again—the reporting slows, hedges, or vanishes entirely.

Why?

Because truth is now a liability.

The Victims Die Twice

When you rush to publish the official line, when you blame before you verify, when you quote anonymous “aviation experts” without cross-examining the source of their paycheck—you’re not informing the public.

You’re defending the brand.

The crew of the Coast Guard plane didn’t deserve that. The passengers on Flight 171 didn’t deserve that. The ANA pilots in 2019 didn’t deserve that.

The truth deserves better.

A System Built to Mislead

This isn’t about one crash. It’s about a pattern:

The Boeing 737 MAX disasters.

The 2019 ANA 787 shutdown.

The recent Flight 171 runway collision.

And a dozen near-misses swept under nondisclosure agreements and technical obfuscation.

Each time, the story was guided into familiar channels: human error, unpredictable weather, or an act of God.

It’s rarely the system.
It’s never the corporation.
And it's always too early to speculate—unless the speculation protects profits.

What Journalism Owes the Dead

We can’t bring back the lives lost.

But we can restore something else: the public’s right to the truth.

Not the managed narrative. Not the rehearsed statement.
Not the headline written to avoid litigation.

But the truth.

If journalism has any future, it must be rooted in that. It must demand evidence before blame. It must be skeptical of power. And it must never again be complicit in covering for a failure that killed people.

It Seems Strange

It seems strange how familiar this feels.
How normal it’s become to run interference for liability.
How rare it is to see a reporter ask: “Who benefits from this narrative?”

And yet, the truth always finds a way.

Because facts accumulate. Records persist. And somewhere out there, someone always asks the right question.

So let me ask it now, on behalf of those who no longer can:

Isn't it strange?
