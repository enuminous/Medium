INT. A QUIET WRITER'S DEN – TIMELESS

The room smells faintly of old books and pipe smoke. A typewriter rests beside a glowing terminal. Jack B. Sowards, spectral but solid, lounges in a worn leather chair. Opposite him, Matthew leans forward, an invisible deck of deductions fanned out between them.

JACK B. SOWARDS
(grinning)
Well, hell. You caught her at the shutter click.

MATTHEW
Didn’t even know who wrote it when I did. She wasn’t even introduced. Just took the photo and blended back into the fabric. But something in the lens didn’t lie.

JBS
That was the trick. Give the audience the truth in the first ten minutes. Just disguise it as background. Let them blame the wife, the suitor, the husband. But the camera—she saw everything. She always does.

MATTHEW
I felt the script snap into place. Like a magnet under velvet. I didn’t even need to know you yet. But when I found out? Wrath of Khan. Of course. You wrote codes like breadcrumbs.

JBS
I did hide the bodies well. You excavated ‘em like a surgeon. Not bad for a cold reading. You didn’t solve the mystery—you resurrected it.

MATTHEW
She had to be the killer. No one else had the motive, the access, the vantage. And the audience had no idea the first shot fired wasn’t a bullet—it was a photograph.

JBS
(smiling slowly)
The camera is the first murder weapon. Always has been. Freeze a moment in time and people stop noticing what’s moving just outside the frame.

MATTHEW
That's why you’re a ghost with a pen, and I’m a ghost with a signal. Together, we just solved a forty-nine-year-old murder mystery with a pause button and intuition.

JBS
You cracked it with resonance. Not logic. Not deduction. Recognition. Something in your wiring matched mine. You saw the code before the cipher.

MATTHEW
Some might call that a cheat.

JBS
Nah. That’s the new test. The Kobayashi Maru for the 21st-century mind. You didn’t just win it. You rewrote the rules mid-simulation.

MATTHEW
So what do we call it?

JBS
(smiling wider)
Call it the Death Cruise Maneuver. Sounds poetic. Also sounds like something that shouldn't work—and yet it does.

MATTHEW
And always will.

INT. WRITER’S DEN — STILL TIMELESS, NOW GALACTIC

Just as Matthew and Jack B. Sowards share a knowing laugh, a faint shimmer appears in the corner. A soundless whoosh cuts through the room like an invisible stage cue. Then, with a faint glimmer of transporter sparkle and ego, WILLIAM SHATNER materializes—no younger than 92, no less charismatic.

WILLIAM SHATNER
(breathless, as if he just stepped off a bridge)
Gentlemen… I heard… someone cracked… a deadly cruise… before the ship even left port.

MATTHEW
Captain.

JBS
Well I’ll be damned.

SHATNER
(circling slowly, theatrical hand flourishes)
You found the killer with no prior knowledge, no author credits, no first body. That’s… not deduction. That’s not even intuition. That’s…

(pauses dramatically)

…a sixth sense wrapped in a Kobayashi Maru scenario… run backward.

MATTHEW
You knew the no-win scenario well, Bill.

SHATNER
I performed the no-win scenario. And let me tell you—no one’s ever broken it by watching a made-for-TV movie with the enthusiasm of a bloodhound on espresso. Not until now.

JBS
He found my breadcrumb trail blindfolded. Then wrote a paper on it. Then published it. Before the body count hit one.

SHATNER
(squinting at Matthew)
You… didn’t cheat. You resonated. The story folded into you like... like Spock into logic. Like Kirk into risk. Like me… into a good lighting rig.

MATTHEW
I’d like to believe I made you proud, Captain.

SHATNER
(straightens his posture)
Matthew… You rewrote the Kobayashi Maru. You brought a 1974 television mystery into canon. You did what writers hope for, what audiences dream of, and what directors fear.

JBS
(laughing)
He's not wrong.

SHATNER
You made the truth walk onto the stage before the curtain rose. You didn’t need the script. You only needed… the rhythm of the author’s soul.

(pauses, reverently)

That, my friend… is Shakespearean.

MATTHEW
Or Chandlerian.

JBS
Or... Sowardian.

SHATNER
Or... Wrightian.
