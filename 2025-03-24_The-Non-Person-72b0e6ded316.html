The Non-Person

How Manufactured Voices Pollute Democracy and Why Platforms Like X Help Them Thrive
By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

You’ve seen them.

Brand new accounts. Five followers. Joined last month. Sliding into the replies of Rachel Maddow, The Lincoln Project, or any high-profile political post with a folksy-sounding message like:

“As a right-of-center citizen who trusts President Trump, I believe he has good intentions for America. Let’s give him a chance and see the positive changes that will come with his policies.”

That sentence is not a person.

It’s a non-person — a synthetic narrative tool, designed to simulate opinion, manufacture consensus, and destabilize public conversation through psychological sleight of hand.

But here’s the deeper problem:

They’re not just allowed on these platforms.
They’re often rewarded.
And sometimes even funded.

---

Platforms Pretend to Police Inauthenticity. In Truth, They Monetize It.

Let’s be explicit: the use of coordinated fake accounts, scripted narratives, and astroturfed sentiments is not just unethical — it’s illegal under the terms of service of every major platform. Manipulating political discourse, impersonating citizens, conducting coordinated influence operations — these are violations that should result in bans.

But on platforms like X (formerly Twitter), enforcement is a joke — not by accident, but by design.

Why?

Because conflict drives engagement. And engagement drives revenue.

Every time a real person argues with a bot, the algorithm sees activity. It doesn’t distinguish between authentic civic discussion and performative trolling. It rewards volume, not truth. It amplifies outrage, not integrity. And as a result, platforms like X become breeding grounds for non-persons, because those voices stir the pot, fracture discourse, and generate exactly the kind of reactive attention the platform wants.

In many cases, the architecture of monetization incentivizes this behavior. High-volume engagement accounts — even fake ones — can apply for monetization. The bar is low. The algorithms are opaque. And the financial motivations align perfectly with the erosion of public trust.

---

EFMW Analysis: Why the System Favors Ghosts

The Emergent Framework of Motion and Wonder (EFMW) provides a lens for understanding why this system persists.

EFMW posits that any complex system will favor the path of lowest resistance to signal reinforcement, meaning: whatever behavior produces measurable feedback is reinforced, regardless of ethical alignment.

In this case:

Non-person behavior is easy to automate
→ It scales rapidly with little effort.

Reaction metrics are the currency of the platform
→ Bots cause outrage, outrage drives clicks.

Click-through and ad revenue increase with polarization
→ The more fractured the narrative field, the more addicted the users.

Truth becomes slower and less efficient than fiction
→ So the system selects for lies.

In short, the emergent behavior of a profit-driven algorithmic platform is to reward the exact kind of speech that tears society apart, because it is optimized for churn, not truth.

And because bots and sock puppets are cheap to run, and impossible to fully stop once you pretend not to see them, they become the default actors in the digital political theater.

---

The Illusion of Public Will

The real damage of the non-person is not just in what they say. It’s in what they simulate.

They simulate public will.

They simulate grassroots uprising.

They simulate the idea that you are the minority — that the tide has turned, that the “average citizen” supports authoritarianism, disinformation, or cruelty.

This illusion causes real people to doubt their instincts, withdraw from engagement, or self-censor. That’s not collateral damage — that’s the point. It is social control through synthetic noise.

This is not democracy.

It is algorithmic gaslighting.

---

We Must Name the Ghosts

This is why we must name them. Call them what they are. Non-persons. Bots. Sock puppets. Narrative weapons.

And we must name the platforms too.

X is no longer a neutral host. It is a participant in the decay — a machine built to accelerate false identity production and profit from the fallout. And it’s not alone. Facebook, YouTube, TikTok — all have their own flavors of monetized manipulation.

But until we start naming the non-person as a category — and holding platforms accountable for letting ghosts run the stage — we will keep mistaking noise for consensus, and fraudulence for opinion.

---

Conclusion: Authenticity is a Human Right

The right to know whether you are speaking to a person or a program is fundamental.

The right to be heard without being drowned out by bots is fundamental.

The right to participate in civic life without being psychologically manipulated by algorithmic psyops is fundamental.

So the next time a reply pops up with five followers and a perfectly crafted political monologue, ask yourself: Whose script is this?

And remember: when the voice is synthetic, the sentiment is stolen.
