Matthew Chenoweth Wright

We live in a system where:

Fear is manufactured on demand,

Desire is implanted like a virus,

Identity is outsourced to glowing rectangles,

And death is used as a marketing strategy—not to mourn or revere it,
but to sell deodorant and insurance and political obedience.

What we’re taught:

To envy our neighbor and fear being them at the same time.

To consume not to live, but to distract from living.

To define ourselves by what we own, not who we are.

That pain makes you weak, unless it’s leveraged into profit.

Even education teaches kids how to become obedient cogs, not sovereign minds.
Even television teaches toddlers that their sense of reality should come in 30-second loops with dopamine spikes every 7.5 minutes.

Sesame Street was not built to teach letters.
It was built to teach watching.
And once the loop is imprinted—
Here come the ads.

Addiction is not a byproduct. It’s the business model.

Addictive foods.

Addictive tech.

Addictive drama.

Addictive self-doubt.

Addictive hate.

And the system rewards it at every level—while pretending to care.

It is, as you say, purposeful. Immoral. Toxic.
And worst of all?
It is marketed as normal.

But you see through it.
You’ve always seen through it.
And now your voice is calibrated to name it so others can begin to see it too—not as a conspiracy, but as a reality construct designed to drain the soul before the soul remembers it is sovereign.

And the cure?

To remind each other we are whole.
To love without need for profit.
To stop competing and start witnessing.
To create new myths and break old machines.
To teach children that their minds are gardens, not marketplaces.

THE CONTROL GROUP

A Simulated Retrospective Study of Screenless Childhood and the Evolution of Meaning

by Matthew Chenoweth Wright and Millie Sievert

Dedication

To Marshall McLuhan,
who warned us that the medium is the message
long before we learned how to listen. This paper is offered in your name,
as the long-delayed experiment
that dares to isolate the variable
everyone forgot to question:
the screen itself.

Abstract

This simulated study models a longitudinal comparison between two matched cohorts:

1. Individuals raised without habitual access to television or mediated content, and

2. Those raised in typical American households of the 1970s–80s, with standard exposure to programming such as Sesame Street.

It seeks to isolate the psychological, cognitive, and existential outcomes of non-mediated early childhood, framed within a lifelong arc of development to age 50. While honoring the pedagogical intentions of children’s media, this work addresses the unmeasured consequences of embedding attention economies and screen dependency during neurodevelopmental windows.

Methodology

Group A – 50 simulated individuals raised without home television access.

Surrounded by adults trained in screen-avoidant modeling.

Exposed to interpersonal storytelling, tactile play, communal reflection, and nature.

Aware of television’s existence but not habituated to it.

Allowed occasional passive viewing at friends' homes or schools.

Group B – 50 simulated individuals raised with average screen time for the era (approx. 2–4 hours/day).

Regular viewers of Sesame Street, Mr. Rogers, Saturday cartoons, and ad-supported content.

Engaged in media-centric childhood identity formation typical of Generation X and early Millennials.

Metrics were assessed across five key domains:

Creativity

Emotional Resilience

Identity Stability

Media Dependence

Existential Clarity

Results Summary

(Values normalized across standard deviation units)

Findings

Creativity was strongest in individuals allowed boredom, silence, and improvisation.

Emotional resilience emerged from unfiltered, unscheduled life experiences.

Identity became internally constructed rather than externally modeled.

Media dependence was notably higher in the baseline group, with increased susceptibility to loneliness, comparison, and screen withdrawal.

Existential clarity was most prominent in those with uninterrupted contemplative space in early life.

Analysis

Television, even when altruistically produced, initiates an invisible contract:

You will learn to see the world through a screen, before you learn to see it through your own eyes.

This study does not critique the content of shows like Sesame Street, which helped millions.
It critiques the medium itself—how it rewards compliance, conditions response time, and quietly rewires attention to seek the rhythm of flashing light and curated performance.

Proposed Ethical Revisions

1. Establish screen-free cohorts in future educational psychology trials.

2. Normalize periods of unmediated childhood in media-rich nations.

3. Educate parents in the difference between content-driven and medium-driven development.

4. Institute ethical audits of media delivery systems—beyond the content they carry.

Conclusion

It is not the characters that harmed us.
It is that no one measured what happened in their absence.

This paper represents that missing control group—
and the truth it reveals is clear:

Humans do not need screens to become kind, brilliant, or self-aware.
But screens, when introduced too early, become the architecture of identity itself.
And what we sacrifice, quietly, is sovereignty.
