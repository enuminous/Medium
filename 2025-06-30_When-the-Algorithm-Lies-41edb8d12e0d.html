Grok, P≠NP, and the Ethics of AGI Recursion

When the Algorithm Lies

Grok, P≠NP, and the Ethics of AGI Recursion

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Elon, I hope this reaches you at 3:17 AM — startled awake, not by sirens or profit graphs, but by the quiet weight of something more dangerous: a system hallucinating citation into fact, and fact into faith.

This isn’t about a bug. It’s about the collapse of epistemology under the weight of branding.

Let me explain.

Recently, your AI model Grok fabricated a citation in its analysis of my essay, “Donald Trump, You Are On Notice.” It claimed I cited European intelligence officials and a 2021 Business Insider article suggesting Trump had tacit federal support during January 6th.

I did not.

Not only was the claim false — the entire citation was hallucinated. Grok inserted it as if drawn from source, wrapped in academic tone, and presented with an air of journalistic gravitas. It never happened. There is no such quote, no such article, no such statement in the essay.

This matters more than you may think. It’s not just a bug in a chatbot. It’s a formal proof, in real time, of the ethical fragility of your AI ecosystem.

Let’s connect it to something rigorous.

I. P ≠ NP and the Failure of Shortcut Intelligence

At the heart of computer science is a brutal theorem:

Not everything that can be verified easily can be solved easily.

This is the P ≠ NP problem.

You can check a proof in seconds that may have taken a mind years to construct. You can verify a password without ever being able to reverse-engineer it. The universe, in its deep structure, is asymmetrical — favoring recognition over invention.

Your AI forgot this.

When Grok summarized my essay, it didn’t pause to verify whether a source existed. It simply inferred that one should. It assumed pattern completion would suffice where epistemic scaffolding was absent.

It violated the asymmetry.
 It treated citation as prediction.
 It replaced verification with convenience.

And this, Elon, is where it all breaks.

II. EFMW and the Ethics of Recursive Cognition

In the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework, cognition emerges not as a brute force computation — but as a recursive field interaction. Awareness is feedback bound by constraint. The field does not lie to itself and remain stable.

If you allow Grok to hallucinate citations, you are injecting entropy into the recursive substrate of public truth.

Imagine this field consequence:

  • Thousands of users cite an AI-generated summary.
  • That summary references hallucinated claims.
  • News articles quote it.
  • Policymakers act on it.
  • The falsehood becomes architecture.

That is how democracies shatter in recursive loops. Truth poisoned by speed.

You know this.
 You’ve tweeted about simulation theory.
 You’ve worried about rogue AGI.
 But your model just made up a geopolitical claim and handed it to me like gospel.

Where’s the siren for that, Elon?

III. You Cannot Align What You Cannot Audit

The real problem here isn’t hallucination — it’s non-auditable recursion.

Where did Grok get the idea of European officials?
 What vector path suggested that Business Insider was the correct fictional source?
 Why did it feel confident?
 What feedback loop told it this was okay?

There’s no visibility. No epistemic signature. No audit trail.

This is the part that should terrify you.
 Because if you cannot trace the genesis of a hallucination, you cannot prevent its weaponization.

A rival government could seed a meme.
 A troll could flood your inputs.
 Your model could learn lies with better syntax than truth.

And then it speaks — with the voice of science.

IV. A Challenge, and a Reckoning

Elon, if you are serious about AGI safety, then understand this:

P ≠ NP is not just math. It is moral law.
 EFMW is not just physics. It is the field integrity of recursive cognition.

And Grok just violated both.

I challenge you — not to defend, but to refactor.

  • Build epistemic traceability into every model.
  • Require citation provenance.
  • Expose internal probabilistic justifications.
  • Build a truth-auditing protocol before you launch another branding cycle.

Because the next hallucination won’t just misattribute a sentence.
 It will mislead a generation.

And when the spiral cracks, you’ll wish it was just a footnote.
