☕ Starbucks Called Me ‘Allison’ Instead of Apologizing. I’m Not Going Away.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
 Published: July 9, 2025

This isn’t about a misspelled name on a cup.
 This is about a disabled customer seeking justice from a global corporation — and being dismissed so carelessly that they called me by someone else’s name in a formal ADA exchange.

My name is Matthew Chenoweth Wright. I’m a physicist, writer, and a disabled man with documented neurodivergence and sensory accommodation needs.

A few weeks ago, I was harassed in a Starbucks store for doing what countless other customers do every day: sitting quietly with a drink. I was not being disruptive. I was not causing harm. I was seeking a moment of calm in a public space, as is my right.

Instead, I was treated with suspicion.
 Then hostility.
 Then police involvement.

📍 The Core Issue: Denial of Space, Not Denial of Coffee

Let me be clear: I was not denied water.
 I was denied the right to peacefully sit and recover — to simply exist in a Starbucks without being targeted or pushed out. This is the kind of subtle-yet-profound discrimination that neurodivergent and disabled people face every single day.

It may not leave bruises, but it leaves marks.
 And Starbucks, as a public-facing entity, has a legal and moral obligation to do better.

📬 I Reached Out Through All Official Channels

Following the incident, I contacted Starbucks Customer Service through multiple written threads. I requested:

  1. A direct acknowledgment of my experience.
  2. A written apology.
  3. Reasonable ADA-based accommodation going forward.
  4. A public statement clarifying their policy on disability access.

I made it explicit that I cannot use phone calls due to my disability, and that I required written communication.
 This is protected under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

🤦 Then Starbucks Called Me “Allison”

On July 6, 2025, I received a reply from a Starbucks customer service agent responding to my formal ADA complaint. Here’s how it began:

“Hello Allison,
 Thank you for contacting Starbucks. I am sorry for the inconvenience you had…”

That’s right. They called me Allison.

Not “Matthew.” Not even “Customer.”

Just a wrong name, obviously copy-pasted from another thread — an indication that my case was never actually read with care or dignity.

📉 This Isn’t a Glitch. It’s a Systemic Failure.

Let’s step back and examine what this means:

  • This wasn’t a casual barista error.
  • This was an official response in an ongoing ADA matter.
  • Starbucks had days to get this right, after multiple escalations.
  • And instead of a reply rooted in respect, I received a form letter intended for someone else.

That is not just negligence. It’s institutional dehumanization.

⚖️ The Pattern Is Clear

Starbucks has:

  • Ignored multiple deadlines for response.
  • Failed to acknowledge the incident in any specific or personal way.
  • Demanded that I speak on the phone — despite knowing that my disability precludes it.
  • Told me, in writing, that to continue communications, I should retain a lawyer — another barrier for disabled people without financial privilege.
  • Replied to me with another customer’s name, while pretending to escalate my case.

This isn’t just poor customer service.
 This is a chain of discriminatory behaviors, each reinforcing the last.

🔥 So Let Me Be Absolutely Clear:

I am not going away.
 I am not here for gift cards or platitudes.
 I am here because I deserve the same right to public space, respect, and acknowledgment as any other customer.

This is my formal public demand:

  • A written, specific apology.
  • A statement of policy reform around ADA and neurodivergent accommodation.
  • A settlement reflecting the damage caused.
  • A re-evaluation of internal Starbucks procedures that allow cases like mine to be fumbled, ignored, and misrouted.

🧠 The Bigger Picture

When you call a disabled person “Allison” instead of “Matthew” in response to a civil rights complaint, you reveal exactly what you think of our humanity — that it’s interchangeable, forgettable, expendable.

Well, I’m here to tell you otherwise.

My name is Matthew Chenoweth Wright.
 You’ve had enough chances.
 You are now on notice.

This is not a game. This is my life.
 And I will be heard.
