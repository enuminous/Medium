When Banks Refuse to Write It Down

How Financial Institutions Evade Their Legal Duties

In the United States, banks are not free to pick and choose who gets access to the very foundations of economic life. There are laws that bind them:

Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA): If a customer expresses intent to apply for credit — even verbally — the bank must treat it as an application, record it, and evaluate it. “No paperwork, no record” is not an excuse.

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA): Banks must provide reasonable accommodations for disabled customers. That can mean flexibility in application formats, direct access to a decision-maker, or assistance in navigating the process. Denying those accommodations is discrimination.

Bank Secrecy Act (BSA): When a customer reports suspected financial misconduct or abuse, banks are obligated to escalate, review, and, if appropriate, file a report. Ignoring credible allegations is not optional.
These are not “nice to haves.” They are statutory requirements.

How the Game Is Played

Here’s how the evasion works in practice:

1. You walk in and ask for financing.
2. Instead of giving you an application, staff deflect: “You need collateral.”
3. You explain your collateral — whether property, intellectual property, or other assets.
4. They still don’t give you paperwork.
5. Months later, corporate comes back with the perfect circular response: “We have no record of an application.”

That’s not an accident. That’s how banks create a closed loop: refuse to record the application, then deny that it ever happened.

Why This Is Illegal

A verbal request for credit is an application under ECOA.

A customer disclosing disability is entitled to ADA accommodations.

A credible report of misconduct triggers review under the BSA.
Failure to act on these duties is not just “bad customer service.” It is unlawful discrimination, a breach of fiduciary responsibility, and a systemic denial of access to the financial system.

The Human Consequences

This isn’t just paperwork. This is life and death.

When a person is denied even the chance to apply for financing — when months of requests vanish into the void — the result is economic exclusion. Bills go unpaid. Evictions happen. Food insecurity follows. This is how people are starved out of existence, quietly, under color of banking procedure.

And here is the bitter truth: this isn’t one bank, one branch, one bad day. When the same pattern shows up at multiple institutions, the conclusion is unavoidable: this is systemic.

Why This Matters for Everyone

If it can happen to me — a physicist, a published author, someone who brought printed documentation, URLs, proof of work, and even personal discussions of collateral — it can happen to anyone.

If banks can pretend your application never existed, then the entire regulatory framework designed to guarantee fair access to credit is meaningless.

What Needs to Change

1. Enforcement: Regulators must actually hold banks accountable for refusing to record applications and ignoring ADA obligations.
2. Transparency: Banks should be required to log every request for credit, whether verbal or written, and provide the customer with a reference number.
3. Accountability: Customers who are harmed by “non-recording” tactics should have direct recourse, including damages for economic exclusion.

Closing

This isn’t about one person or one complaint. This is about whether the financial system operates under the law — or above it.

If banks can refuse to even hand you the paper, if they can make your application disappear with a shrug, then no one is safe.

We either have a financial system bound by duty, or we have an arbitrary regime where access depends on who you are, what you’ve published, or who you’ve angered.

I know which one I’m living in right now. The question is: will the rest of us demand better?
