Physically assaulted by the local public library just for riding past on a bicycle?

How high-frequency deterrents are harming the vulnerable, violating the ADA, and normalizing violence in public spaces—all in the name of safety.

Late tonight in Woodland, California, a man on a bicycle was brought to a halt outside a public library—not by any visible barrier, but by an invisible force: a piercing, oscillating hum emanating from a mounted device affixed to the building’s southwest corner. The sound caused disorientation, headache, and a near-loss of balance.

There was no warning sign, no public record, no way to contact a human being to deactivate it. The only escape was to retreat.

What he encountered wasn’t malfunction—it was policy.

The Science of Sonic Weaponry

High-frequency sound deterrents, including ultrasonic and parametric directional speakers, are increasingly deployed in urban architecture. Marketed under names like Mosquito, SonicShield, or Youth Control Systems, these devices are designed to:

Deter loitering

Drive away unhoused people

Prevent nighttime resting in public spaces

They do this by emitting sound frequencies ranging from 15kHz to 25kHz—often beyond the average adult’s hearing range, but painfully audible to many younger people, neurodivergent individuals, and those with sensory sensitivity.

Studies confirm that prolonged exposure to these frequencies can lead to:

Vestibular disruption (dizziness, balance loss)

Headaches and migraines

Auditory damage (especially when near reflective surfaces)

Psychological distress, particularly for individuals on the autism spectrum or with hyperacusis

Weaponized Urbanism and "Acceptable" Harm

These devices are installed under the cover of deterrent design—a growing school of architecture that incorporates hostile elements to “keep order.” Spikes on benches, sloped planters that prevent sleeping, and now—acoustic pain.

The justification? “It’s for the greater good.”

But who decides what “good” means when it’s deployed by those who do not feel its harm? And who becomes collateral damage?

A Direct Violation of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

The ADA prohibits discrimination by state and local governments in access to public spaces. This includes architectural barriers and sensory environments that prevent equal participation.

High-pitched deterrents:

Are not disclosed to the public

Are not regulated by posted signage

Cause physical pain to specific populations

Create an inaccessible perimeter around public resources (like a library)

This constitutes not only negligence—it may be a civil rights violation under Title II of the ADA.

It says that we’ve confused compliance with compassion.

When Silence Is the Only Answer

In the middle of the night, as the painful oscillating signal continued to radiate from the library wall, the man attempted the most basic act of civic responsibility: to call the institution responsible and report the harm.

He dialed the number for the Woodland Public Library.
What he got was not a person.
Not a voicemail.
But a cycling, impersonal message listing library hours and directing him to “visit our website.”

There was no way to leave a message, no human interface, no emergency line. The only recourse provided required one thing:

Internet access.

But the man—like many people who find themselves outside at night, without a home, or simply without a charged phone or Wi-Fi—did not have access to the internet at that moment. Nor is there any law, anywhere, that requires a person to carry a phone, a laptop, or to be online in order to be heard when their body is being harmed by a city’s own infrastructure.

This is a second violation layered on top of the first:

First, the use of painful acoustic deterrents with no warning or oversight.

Second, the denial of access to the means of reporting harm unless the individual is digitally equipped.

In effect, the library—a public institution—has created a system where the only people who can complain are those who are not being targeted by the deterrent.

That’s not public service.
That’s technocratic segregation.

If you must be online to demand not to be hurt,
then justice has already been firewalled.
