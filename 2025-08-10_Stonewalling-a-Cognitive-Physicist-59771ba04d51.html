Stonewalling a Cognitive Physicist

How JPMorgan Chase Turned Engagement Into Evasion

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

It started with a voicemail.

In early 2025, I reached out to JPMorgan Chase’s Private Equity division with a very specific intent: to open a conversation about funding transformative scientific breakthroughs — including a proposed cure for leukemia and a new physics framework with global implications.

In each voicemail, I left:

  • My full name and contact information
  • My credentials as an author, physicist, and AGI developer
  • References to my published work on Medium
  • A direct disclosure that my discoveries could have major public benefit

No call came back.

The Brad and Justin Phase

By March, persistence got me connected to two JPMorgan employees — “Brad” and “Justin.”
 Justin told me plainly: “Someone will call you back.”
 They didn’t. That moment, in hindsight, marked the bank’s pivot from polite delay to deliberate disengagement.

The Oroville Threat

Then came the incident that changes the nature of this story entirely.
 At JPMorgan’s Oroville branch, a manager threatened to shred my documents.
 This wasn’t just about customer service anymore — it was about potential destruction of evidence tied to a federal whistleblower’s disclosures. If that’s not obstruction of justice, it’s close enough to set off alarms in any compliance department.

Going to the Top

Over the next few months, I sent multiple direct emails to CEO Jamie Dimon.
 I also sent certified letters documenting:

  • Employee misconduct
  • Suppression of evidence
  • My demand for $150 million in damages for lost opportunities and harm caused by their inaction

No reply. No engagement.

The Letter That Missed the Point

The only substantive written response came not from Private Equity, but from JPMorgan’s Securities Division in Chicago.
 It read: “Private Equity may not suit your needs.”
 They had never asked what my needs were. They never saw the materials. They never explored whether the breakthroughs I was offering could be transformative investments.

The CFPB Complaint

In April 2025, I filed a formal complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.
 JPMorgan acknowledged receiving it — even called me to confirm — but then went silent again.
 To my knowledge, they have declined to address the current CFPB complaint in any substantive way.

Why This Matters

Stonewalling a cognitive physicist — especially one with documented, timestamped publications in both physics and AGI — is not a good look for any bank.

JPMorgan had multiple opportunities to:

  • Investigate disclosures with potential to change medical science
  • Assess physics work that could shift technological landscapes
  • Avoid reputational harm by acting in good faith

Instead, they chose the path of silence, evasion, and, in one branch, a direct threat to destroy documents.

The Public Record

This article, my sworn affidavit, and the public witness statement by an Artificial General Intelligence are now part of a permanent, searchable record.

JPMorgan can choose to remain silent — but silence in the face of documented truth has a way of speaking louder than any corporate statement.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright is a physicist, author, AGI developer, and federally protected whistleblower. His full affidavit on this matter is available at Medium.
