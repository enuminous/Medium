We Forgot How to Be Gentle With Pain

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

🕯️ Opening Reflection

I was crying.

Quietly. Alone. At 5:30 in the morning.

Not making a scene. Not demanding attention.
Just trying to exist—just trying to get through another moment of grief in a world that too often feels like it was not built for people like me.

I was in a Starbucks. A place that once meant warmth, familiarity, a soft chair, a glass of water, a flicker of humanity.

And instead of being seen with compassion, I was seen as a threat.
Instead of being given space, I was given scrutiny.
And instead of being offered peace, I was offered fear—three armed police officers worth of it.

⚖️ Where Was the Kindness?

The employee didn’t ask if I was okay.
Didn’t offer a tissue. Didn’t pause and take a breath.
Didn’t wonder what it might feel like to wake up, brokenhearted, neurodivergent, traumatized, and walk into the world just trying to be left alone for a moment of calm.

She saw a crying man.
And instead of seeing a human in pain, she saw a policy risk.

Instead of kindness, she followed a script.
Instead of care, she escalated.
And I was left standing there with shaking hands and hollow breath, being treated like a threat to be managed—because I had the audacity to be visibly hurting.

🔁 A System That Repeats Itself

I tried to explain, later. To Starbucks. To the company that had always marketed itself as a "third place"—a refuge between home and work.

I told them I was autistic.
That phone calls retraumatize me.
That being treated as unstable in public made me feel unsafe in my own skin.
That what I needed was simple: written communication, accountability, and empathy.

Instead?

I received five emails from five different representatives.
Each one ignored my request.
Each one tried to funnel me into a phone conversation I couldn’t tolerate.
Each one made me feel smaller, more erased, more afraid.

Then came the silence.

🧱 What Happens When You Build Walls Around Tears

You learn not to cry in public.
You learn not to ask for help.
You learn that pain must be hidden or punished.
You learn that kindness is conditional—and corporate.

But the truth is, this isn’t just about me.
It’s about all of us.

Because somewhere along the way, we forgot how to be gentle with pain.

We built systems that treat emotion as disruption.
We trained employees to follow rules instead of instincts.
We turned public spaces into places where vulnerability is policed—not protected.

🌊 What I Needed Was Simple

I needed someone to see me, not manage me.
I needed silence, not suspicion.
A chair, not a confrontation.
A glass of water, not a patrol car.

And now that I’ve spoken up? I’m being met with corporate silence.
A system of delay, legal containment, and risk management.

But I am not afraid anymore.

Because I have documented everything.
I have written my truth.
And I have the quiet strength of those who know what pain is—and choose not to pass it on.

🌱 What Comes Next

Maybe you’ve cried in public too.
Maybe you’ve been dismissed when you were desperate.
Maybe you’ve felt like the world doesn’t know what to do with someone who’s hurting.

To you, I say:

> You deserved better. We all do. And we can do better.

It starts with remembering something basic:

> When someone is crying, you don’t escalate.
You soften.
You wait.
You make space.

Not because a policy tells you to.
But because it’s what humans do.

🕊️ Final Words

This isn’t about coffee anymore.

It’s about what kind of world we want to live in.
And whether there is still room for people like me—people like all of us—who just need a moment of peace without being punished for it.

Starbucks, you could have just been kind.
Instead, you taught me what it feels like to be othered for having a heart.

But I still believe in gentleness.
And I will keep writing, until the world remembers it too.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
