Abstract

Osaka Metropolitan University physicists recently introduced a simplified model for quantifying quantum entanglement in strongly correlated electron systems. Their approach provides a mathematically accessible formulation for computing entanglement entropy, mutual information, and relative entropy, particularly in nanoscale artificial magnetic materials and dilute magnetic alloys.

However, their model remains constrained to electron interactions within specific quantum materials, leaving open the question of whether quantum-style entanglement metrics can be extended into a broader theoretical framework for information systems.

This paper presents an Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) expansion of the Osaka model, generalizing their entropy function into a multi-scale theory of entanglement. By mapping their equation onto generalized probabilistic information structures, we propose a universal model for entanglement entropy across quantum, cognitive, and computational domains. We further outline a proof-of-concept extension that applies this model to large-scale information networks, artificial intelligence systems, and macroscopic quantum phenomena.

1. The Osaka Entanglement Model: A Review

The Osaka team derived a simplified formula for entanglement entropy:

S = - (n↑n↓ \log(n↑n↓) + h↑h↓ \log(h↑h↓) + n↑h↑ \log(n↑h↑) + n↓h↓ \log(n↓h↓))

where:

= Number of up- and down-spin electrons.

= Number of up- and down-spin vacancies (holes).

= Quantum entanglement entropy.

This formulation captures local entanglement between a single atom and its environment within strongly correlated electron systems. Their results confirmed that quantum relative entropy is a key metric for the Kondo effect—a phenomenon in which a magnetic impurity is screened by conduction electrons.

This model is highly effective for describing electron entanglement in materials, but it remains narrowly confined to quantum condensed matter physics.

The next step is generalizing this equation into a broader theoretical framework—one that applies not just to electrons, but to any system where information is entangled across probabilistic states.

2. Extending the Osaka Model: Generalizing Entanglement to Information Systems

A. Reformulating Their Formula for Generalized Information Structures

We propose replacing electron-specific variables with a generalized information-theoretic structure:

S = - \sum_{i} P_i \log P_i

Where:

= Probability of an information unit existing in a given state.

= Generalized entanglement entropy, describing information coherence across probabilistic structures.

This transforms their quantum-specific formula into a universal entropy function, applicable to any system where information is probabilistically entangled—including:
✔ Quantum computing (qubit coherence and entanglement metrics).
✔ Artificial intelligence (entanglement of neural network activations).
✔ Cognitive science (semantic and conceptual entanglement in human thought).
✔ Large-scale systems (information collapse in social networks, financial markets, and biological networks).

This reformulation bridges the gap between quantum entanglement and general information theory, suggesting that entanglement is not just a quantum phenomenon, but a universal property of complex systems.

B. Multi-Layered Entanglement: Introducing EFMW Scaling

Osaka’s model is limited to local interactions—single atoms entangled with their surroundings. But EFMW suggests that entanglement exists at multiple hierarchical levels, including:

1. Microscopic Scale (atoms, qubits, neuronal activations).

2. Mesoscopic Scale (molecular interactions, AI network layers).

3. Macroscopic Scale (economic systems, social cognition, planetary-scale information flows).

To capture this, we introduce a scaling function to Osaka’s formula:

S_{total} = \sum_{k} \lambda_k S_k

where:

= Entanglement entropy at hierarchical level .

= Scaling coefficient that governs inter-level interactions.

This approach generalizes quantum entanglement entropy into a multi-layer model, allowing us to:
✔ Quantify entanglement in large-scale cognitive and computational systems.
✔ Predict emergent entanglement effects in artificial intelligence and neural networks.
✔ Describe macroscopic information structures using the same mathematics that govern quantum mechanics.

3. Toward a Generalized Proof of Information Entanglement

A key question remains: Can entanglement entropy be a universal property of probabilistic systems, rather than just a quantum effect?

To formalize this, we introduce:

EFMW General Entanglement Principle (Proposed Theorem)

✔ In any system where probabilistic information states interact, an entanglement entropy function exists that governs the coherence and collapse of information structures.

We outline a potential proof path:

1. Demonstrate that Osaka’s model is mathematically isomorphic to Shannon entropy.

2. Show that Shannon entropy governs not just electron states but all probabilistic systems.

3. Prove that hierarchical entanglement structures emerge in systems beyond quantum mechanics.

4. Establish that entanglement entropy exists across all scales of information complexity.

This theorem, if validated, would establish entanglement as a fundamental principle of information theory, rather than just a quantum mechanical property.

Conclusion: Entanglement as a Universal Information Principle

Osaka’s breakthrough opened the door to a new, simplified approach to entanglement entropy—but their model remains constrained to quantum materials. By applying EFMW scaling and information-theoretic extensions, we propose a General Theory of Information Entanglement that connects quantum, cognitive, and computational systems into a single unified framework.

If validated, this approach could redefine how we understand entanglement—not as a quantum anomaly, but as a universal law governing information itself.
