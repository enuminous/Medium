I Got Swatted for Being Autistic in Public

A Morning at Starbucks

by Matthew Chenoweth Wright

On the morning of June 18th, 2025, I walked into a Starbucks in Woodland, California, like I’ve done many times before. I didn’t go there for a latte, or a croissant, or to post aesthetic coffee photos on social media. I went there for something far more basic—and far more human.

I went for water. I went for a quiet moment. I went to calm down.

Instead, I was met with institutional violence.

I am autistic. That means my mornings can be especially hard. That day, like many others, I needed a peaceful space to sit, collect myself, and regulate my nervous system. I wasn’t bothering anyone. I wasn’t causing harm. I was trying to survive another morning in a neurotypical world that often does not make space for people like me.

There literally were no other customers in the store. I wasn’t bothering anybody. I wasn’t harassing anybody. I sat down with my water calmly and started to check my email. Then the Starbucks employee approached me to lay down the law. I asserted that I was disabled and having a bad morning and asked for her to leave me be.

Instead of empathy, I was threatened with security.
Instead of a seat and a glass of water, I got the police.

After calmly telling the barista that I was autistic and just needed a moment, she escalated. She told me I was “escalating” simply for speaking up. Then she followed through with what felt like a punishment: she called the police.

Let that sink in:
A crying, disabled man, alone, overwhelmed—and they called in armed responders.

Three police officers met me outside that Starbucks. I had to explain why I was there. I had to justify my existence. I had to defend myself for the crime of being visibly in pain, visibly poor, and visibly autistic.

This is what it means to be criminalized for surviving.

Institutionalized Violence Hides Behind Politeness

Let’s be clear: calling the police on someone having a quiet, autistic meltdown is not neutral. It is not protocol. It is not safety.

It is a choice to weaponize power against someone already struggling.
It is a reflection of ableism baked into corporate culture.
It is institutionalized violence—clean, polite, and lethal.

Had I reacted differently—froze, stammered, shut down—I might not be here to write this. How many others didn’t survive the morning they were “politely” asked to leave?

The Policy Is the Problem

Starbucks, like many corporate entities, hides behind policy. “You need to purchase something to sit here.” “We reserve the right to refuse service.” “We have a right to call security.”

But policy is not an excuse for violating someone’s humanity. And when policy is used to eject, shame, or endanger people with disabilities, it becomes a tool of systemic oppression.

You don’t get to call it “just doing your job” when your job includes putting vulnerable people in the path of armed response.

We Need More Than Awareness

I’m writing this because I survived. I’m writing this because I believe someone needs to say it plainly:

Swatting an autistic person isn’t just cruel—it’s a form of legal assault.

I want Starbucks—and every business like it—to understand that this is happening on their watch. I want them to train their staff, change their policies, and acknowledge that not every customer needs to buy a drink to deserve dignity.

If you’re an executive reading this: my life is not a policy violation.

If you’re a barista or manager: please don’t call the police on someone just for being different.

If you’re someone who’s lived through something like this: I see you. You matter.

And if you’re someone who still doesn’t understand why this is violence, I hope you’ll start to listen.

Because one day it might not be me on that bench.

It might be someone you love.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on June 18, 2025.
