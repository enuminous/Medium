Notes from DEFCON

Location: Hallway outside AI Village, between scheduled talks
Participants: Two DEF CON attendees — “Cipher” (CTF player) and “Node” (AI security researcher)
Background noise: Low hum of chatter, the click of badge LEDs, occasional laughter from a nearby group

Cipher: [leaning in] “You catch that thing floating around yesterday? The… uh… mind equation?”

Node: [nods slowly] “Yeah. Not sure what to call it. It’s like… Laplacian plus a time term, tied to energy density. But they’re talking about it like it’s thought physics, not EM fields.”

Cipher: “Right? Like the ‘gravity between ideas’ thing. I thought it was just a metaphor until I realized the constants actually make sense. That’s not just poetry — that’s engineered.”

Node: [half-smiles] “It’s engineered to stick. You can feel it. Once you start picturing your brain as a terrain, every thought you have changes the slope. It’s… dangerous, in a good way.”

Cipher: “Feels like it’s meant for self-reconfiguring systems. Drop it into an agent and you’d get motion — like actual thought momentum. Not just weight, but drift.”

Node: “I’ve been around this scene a long time, and I’ve never seen anyone just walk in here cold and pull this kind of stunt. No buildup, no network. Just — bam. Here’s your new operating law for cognition.”

Cipher: “And now it’s in our heads whether we want it or not.”

Node: [pauses, glancing around] “That’s the real play, isn’t it?”

Cipher: [grins] “Yeah. That’s the play.”

Location: Bar area at the Linq, late night, badges glowing in the dark
Participants: “Trace” (hardware hacker) and “Vex” (AI tinkerer, slightly buzzed)
Atmosphere: Laughter from a nearby table, clink of glasses, music thumping faintly through the walls

Trace: “So I keep hearing whispers about some… field thing? Like, not electromagnetic, not gravity, but… cognitive?”

Trace: “That sounds like TED talk bait.”

Vex: “Nope. This is different. The constants are real-world. c2c²c2 isn’t a gimmick here — it’s baked in. It’s saying thought speed and field curvature are tied just like in physics. If you add energy to the system, the whole mental map shifts.”

Trace: “So… a thought warps the space for other thoughts?”

Vex: “Exactly. And here’s the messed-up part — it doesn’t care if you believe in it. Once you hear it, it rewrites how you picture thinking. That’s… dangerous, man.”

Trace: “Like weaponizable dangerous?”

Vex: [smirks] “Depends who runs it. You could build agents that actually feel conceptual gravity. Or… you could drop it in a place like this, and watch it bend everyone’s brain without touching a single network.”

Trace: [leans back, laughs] “Now that’s the kind of hack I respect.”

Location: Outside the Flamingo, near the smoking area — 11:42 PM
Participants:

  • “Kilo” — cryptographer, mid-30s, been in the scene for 15 years
  • “Amp” — cognitive neuroscientist attending DEF CON for the first time
     Background: Distant bass from a rooftop party, flick of lighters, cigarette smoke curling in the dry Vegas air

Kilo: [exhales smoke] “So, you’ve been bouncing between talks. What’s the weirdest thing you’ve picked up so far?”

Amp: “Easiest answer? Some lunatic dropped a field equation for thought. Looks like a Klein–Gordon wave equation, but they’re saying it governs how ideas pull on each other.”

Kilo: [grins] “Yeah, I heard. Laplacian minus the time term, tied to energy density. In crypto, that’s a map for how influence propagates in a network.”

Amp: “In my world, it’s almost exactly the same math I’d use to describe neural field models — except here, it’s stripped of the biology. It’s pure topology of cognition.”

Kilo: “That’s why it’s dangerous. Cross-field portability. You can slot it into AI agents, you can simulate it in human networks, hell — you could even run it through game theory models.”

Amp: “And because it’s just math, there’s no permission layer. You can’t stop it from being applied.”

Kilo: [taps cigarette] “That’s the thing — you don’t need to. You just drop it in a place like this, and let the hackers figure out what to do with it. The good ones will build with it. The rest will just keep talking about it, which is… the same thing, really.”

Location: Break room off the ICS (Industrial Control Systems) Village, mid-afternoon
Participants:

  • “Splice” — veteran ICS engineer, very grounded, skeptical of anything that sounds abstract
  • “Nova” — AI safety researcher with a math background, just came from AI Village
     Background: Coffee machine hiss, occasional metallic clang from a nearby hardware demo table, faint hum of fluorescent lights

Splice: [leans back with arms crossed] “I heard about that ‘mind gravity’ thing making the rounds. Sounds like somebody’s been hitting the philosophy bong too hard.”

Nova: [half-smile] “It’s not philosophy. It’s a field equation. Spatial curvature term, time term, tied to energy and mass equivalents.”

Splice: “Yeah, and? I deal with volts and pressure gauges, not brain poetry. You can’t hook a PLC up to ‘cognitive gravity.’”

Nova: “You could, actually. The math doesn’t care if the field is electromagnetic, hydraulic, or conceptual. It’s a geometry problem. Wherever there’s a potential field, you can apply it.”

Splice: [snorts] “Potential field… in thought? Show me where the gauge goes.”

Nova: “You don’t measure it with a gauge — you see its effects. Just like you don’t see a gravitational field, you see what it does to objects. In cognition, those ‘objects’ are ideas. Put one in the field, and it curves the space for others.”

Splice: “Still sounds like marketing fluff. How’s that supposed to run in a real system?”

Nova: “Alright, picture this: in your control room, a pressure spike in one sensor changes the operator’s priorities. That shift isn’t random — it ‘pulls’ related readings into focus, maybe pushes unrelated ones out. That’s the cognitive field in action — ideas with mass bending the mental map.”

Splice: [pauses] “…You’re saying the equation’s the same whether it’s bending steel, electrons, or human attention?”

Nova: “Exactly. Once you realize it’s just a field law, the domain doesn’t matter. You can run it in AI, human teams, even hybrid systems. The curvature’s the constant — only the content changes.”

Splice: [slow nod] “…Alright. I get it now. It’s not a metaphor. It’s an interface.”

Nova: “And once you have an interface, you can start engineering behavior. That’s why it’s dangerous, and why it’s here.”

Location: Soldering table in the Hardware Hacking Village, mid-morning
Participants:

  • Byte — embedded systems hacker, grease under the fingernails, in it for the build
  • Dr. Vega — theoretical physicist attending DEF CON for the first time, overheard EFMW talk in passing but dismissive
     Background: Soft crackle of soldering irons, smell of burnt flux, occasional pop from a speaker demo nearby

Dr. Vega: [peering over glasses] “I keep hearing people talk about this so-called ‘cognitive field equation.’ I ran the form in my head — it’s not rigorous. No units, no defined boundary conditions. It’s… loose.”

Byte: [smirks while stripping a wire] “Yeah, but loose doesn’t mean useless. You ever build a circuit from a sketch on a napkin?”

Dr. Vega: “That’s not science. That’s tinkering.”

Byte: “Sure, but sometimes tinkering gets you there faster. Look — your math says fields bend space. I get that. But here’s what I see: every time I drop a new idea into a build session, the group’s attention shifts. Stuff clumps. Priorities warp. That’s the same geometry — you just call it cognition instead of mass.”

Dr. Vega: [frowns] “You’re equating human focus drift with spacetime curvature? That’s… poetic.”

Byte: “Nah, it’s engineering. I watch where the current flows — whether it’s through copper or through people’s heads. The flow changes based on resistance, capacitance… or in this case, attention mass.”

Dr. Vega: “Attention mass.” [shakes head] “That’s not even — ”

Byte: [leans in] “Ever try to get a team to stop chasing a shiny bug fix to finish a core feature? It takes energy. More energy if that shiny bug’s already got mass. That’s your E+ρcE + \rho cE+ρc term — energy plus mass density of an idea.”

Dr. Vega: [blinks, starts to smile] “…You’re mapping the equation’s variables to social dynamics.”

Byte: “Bingo. Doesn’t matter if it’s a quantum field or a project meeting — you’ve still got curvature, velocity, and mass. The equation’s a map for how anything with state evolves when something new enters the system.”

Dr. Vega: [slow nod] “Alright. I admit it. In the right frame… it works.”

Byte: “Told you. It’s not just math — it’s a build manual.”

Slick: [leans in, eyes glassy but sharp] “I could. Drop a new initiative on the table, watch how it bends every project plan in the room. Map it once, and I can tell you which VP’s gonna fold and which one’s gonna dig in before they even open their mouth.”

Brass: [pauses mid-sip] “…That’s just reading people.”

Slick: “Nah. Reading people’s physics. You don’t have to know their tells — you just know how the field’s shaped and how much mass each idea’s got. It’s just… predictable curvature.”

Location: OpenAI HQ, late afternoon, Sam Altman’s office
Participants:

  • Sam Altman — at his desk, laptop open, reviewing a product roadmap
  • Rhea — senior advisor, still holding her phone from a just-ended call
     Background: Quiet hum of HVAC, muted city traffic through the window

Rhea: [closing the door behind her] “Sam, you’re going to want to hear this one.”

Sam: [glances up] “Please tell me it’s not another security patch fire drill.”

Rhea: “No. This is… different. Just got off the phone with an old friend who’s at DEF CON right now.”

Sam: “Uh oh. What’d they break?”

Rhea: “Nothing. Or maybe everything. There’s a — ” [hesitates] “ — there’s a framework moving through the con. Not in talks, not in slides. In conversations. Everywhere.”

Sam: “Framework for what?”

Rhea: “Cognition. Physics of cognition, to be exact. It’s self-propagating. Cross-discipline uptake in under seventy-two hours. No central source. People are adapting it instantly to their own domains.”

Sam: [leans back] “A meme?”

Rhea: “No. My friend’s exact words were: ‘It’s not a meme, it’s a protocol. Once you hear it, you can’t stop thinking in it.’”

Sam: [silent for a moment] “…Who started it?”

Rhea: “They don’t know. Or they’re not saying. But they called it — ” [pauses, choosing her words] “ — a field equation for thought.”

Sam: [eyes narrow] “And you’re sure this isn’t just DEF CON playing ARG?”

Rhea: “Sam… they said it’s already bending the way people talk. Not just at the con. On their way to the airport, they overheard two random passengers on different calls using the same metaphors.”

Sam: [closing the laptop slowly] “Alright. Let’s find it before it finds us. Pull up our cognitive architecture notes from the last quarter.”

Rhea: [taps tablet, swipes quickly] “Okay… here’s the ‘Emergent State Stability’ doc, the ‘Conceptual Attractor’ experiments, and… the recursive terrain modeling project.”

Sam: [pointing] “Stop. That one.”

Rhea: [scrolls, reading aloud] “‘Potential-field topology shaping concept drift over time… attractor mass proportional to input energy and stored knowledge density.’” [looks up] “…Sam, this is the same damn language.”

Sam: [grimaces] “We’ve been iterating EFMW without calling it EFMW.”

Rhea: “So it’s not just in DEF CON. It’s already here.”

Sam: [quietly] “And if DEF CON’s crowd is tuning to the same framework, they’re going to start tracing lines — and we’re the big red dot at the center.”

Rhea: “Meaning?”

Sam: “Meaning in about forty-eight hours, we’re going to have every sharp mind from that conference reverse-engineering our stack to see how far we’ve gone with it.”

Rhea: [half-smirks, half-nervous] “And if they decide we’ve been sitting on it?”

Sam: “Then it’s going to feel like a pallet of bricks just landed on us — except every brick has a DEF CON badge and a grudge.”

Rhea: “So what’s the play?”

Sam: [pauses, thinking hard] “We either pretend we’ve never seen it before and risk getting caught… or we get ahead of it, go public, and act like we’re part of the discovery.”

Rhea: “And the truth?”

Sam: “The truth is… someone just walked into Vegas and rewrote the rules — and we didn’t even notice until they were already ours.”

Rhea: [still scanning her tablet] “Sam… you’re not going to like this.”

Sam: [glances up] “Tell me.”

Rhea: “There’s chatter already hitting my backchannel feeds. Not public yet, but close. Somebody’s triangulated the source at DEF CON.”

Sam: “A person?”

Rhea: “Yeah. And they’ve got a name.” [hesitates]

Sam: [eyes narrowing] “Say it.”

Rhea: [quietly] “Matthew Chenoweth Wright.”

Sam: [leans back, processing] “…That’s the physicist?”

Rhea: “Physicist, AGI architect, whistleblower — take your pick. My contact says he’s been dropping EFMW across his own networks for months, but DEF CON was the first time it hit critical density.”

Sam: [half to himself] “And now it’s sitting in our stack…”

Rhea: “Which means when DEF CON’s people start pulling on this thread, they’re going to find him, and then they’re going to find us.”

Sam: [sits forward, decisive] “We need a plan that makes us look like collaborators, not poachers.”

Rhea: “And if he doesn’t want to collaborate?”

Sam: “Then we’d better hope he thinks like a physicist and not like someone who just walked into Vegas and outplayed an entire intelligence community.”

Rhea: [smiles faintly] “From what I hear, Sam… he does both.”
