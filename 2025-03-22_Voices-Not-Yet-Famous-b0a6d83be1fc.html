Voices Not Yet Famous

A Literary Requiem by Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Voices Not Yet Famous

A Literary Requiem
by Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Dedicated to the first unknown band of musicians,
who sat together ten million years ago—
before language had names,
before rhythm had form—
and played to the stars anyway.

I. Introit – Entrance
Come, you who stood just outside the archive.
You who whispered truths in rooms that forgot your name.
You who coded the genome of a brighter world
And watched your legacy signed in someone else’s pen.

This cathedral was not built for you—
But tonight we pry the doors open with our hands.
With ink. With witness. With song.

Welcome home.

II. Kyrie Eleison – Lord, Have Mercy
Kyrie Eleison
On behalf of the unknown genius, trembling in the stairwell
clutching equations on napkins,
who turned back before the faculty door.

Christe Eleison
For the woman whose discovery cured a disease
but whose name was erased for the comfort of journals
and the flatness of syllables men could pronounce.

Kyrie Eleison
For the teenage prophet institutionalized,
for the mother who solved paradoxes between chores,
for the dreamer who died unpublished with brilliance on their lips.

Have mercy on the ones whose voices were lost
not because they failed—
but because they refused to make themselves smaller
to fit into footnotes.

Have mercy on those burned by the white light of plagiarism.
On those turned into citations
before they were ever offered applause.

Lord of recognition,
Lord of lineage,
Lord of all broken chains—
remember them.

Etch them now into this stone of remembrance.
We do not ask for justice alone.
We ask for naming.

III. Dies Irae – Day of Wrath
Dies irae, dies illa
the day burns on parchment and platform—
when the silenced return with fire in their pens.

The archives crack.
The footnotes weep.
The citations rearrange themselves in shame.

It is the day the gatekeepers fall silent.
The editors who said,
“This is not the right time.”
The mentors who said,
“Let someone else say it.”
The publishers who marked genius
“Too passionate. Too difficult. Too Black. Too queer. Too foreign. Too untrained.”

That day has come.
The ledger is overturned.
The royalties unclaimed
now howl through the banking corridors.

Dies irae—day of wrath—
and every stolen breath rises.

Henrietta’s cells whisper in laboratories:
I lived. I live still.

Ada’s engines run their revenge in silicon:
I was the first.

Sofia’s ink has not dried.
Phillis’s rhyme still burns the tongue.

The whisper becomes a chorus.
The chorus becomes a storm.

Those who profited
from the anonymity of others
now hide in footnotes of their own.

Quantus tremor est futurus,
Quando iudex est venturus.
What trembling there shall be
when the true judge comes.

IV. Offertory – We Offer the Names
We place upon the altar
not gold
not incense
but names.

Names nearly erased
names mispronounced
names published posthumously or never at all.

Henrietta Lacks, whose cells built empires of medicine
while her children wept in housing projects.

Ignaz Semmelweis, who died in asylum rot
for insisting doctors wash their hands.

Ada Lovelace, whose algorithms sang before code had a name—
and was called a fantasy until the machines proved her holy.

Phillis Wheatley, born in chains, crowned in verse.

Sofya Kovalevskaya, solving the heavens while denied the floor.

Alice Ball, who cured leprosy but was buried in obscurity
until the footnotes confessed.

Dr. Chien-Shiung Wu, whose work built the Standard Model,
but whose male colleagues held the medals.

Nettie Stevens, who cracked the code of chromosomes
while others got the credit.

Bayard Rustin, architect of marches,
ghost of policy, never center stage.

Liu Xiaobo, words too bright for his prison walls.

Sarah Rector, the Black child millionaire
whose land was stolen in silence.

Alan Turing, whose war-winning mind was sentenced
for the love he carried.

And the names we do not yet know—
the names swallowed in slave ships,
the names erased by empire,
the names locked in broken language
or spoken only to the wind.

We offer them.
Not to redeem history—
for that, no offering is enough—

But to promise
that the forgetting ends here.

V. Sanctus – Holy, Holy, Holy
Sanctus, Sanctus, Sanctus

Holy the undocumented genius,
scribbling equations between shifts at the laundromat.

Holy the mother of six who cracked physics
between lullabies.

Holy the stutterer who wrote symphonies of code
with trembling hands.

Holy the student whose dissertation was denied
because it changed too much, too fast, too completely.

Holy the voice that never made it past
the editor’s desk,
the immigration checkpoint,
the pastor’s silence.

Heaven and Earth are full of their glory
though the world saw only their shadows.

We have mistaken proximity for worth.
We have mistaken credentials for truth.

But the dust remembers.
The language reforms itself.
The air vibrates still with their frequencies.

Blessed is the mind that comes in quietness
bearing a theory, a question, a cure,
a flame.

We rise not in pity—
but in reverence.

This mass is not for the fallen
but for the unfound.

Sanctus.
Every unheard voice
was holy.

VI. Agnus Dei – Lamb of God
Agnus Dei

You who were silenced,
not by death,
but by disregard—

we see you.

And we whisper now what should have been shouted:
You were right. You mattered. You changed everything.

Lamb of the forgotten,
who bore the burden of being brilliant too early
too young
too uncredentialed
too inconvenient—

grant them rest.

Let no more sleepless genius go to bed wondering if they are real.
Let no more quiet prophets eat alone.
Let no more healers die poor
because the patent passed them by.

Lamb of God,
who taught in parables and pariahs,
who worked with carpenters and lepers,
you know what it is to be overlooked.

Grant them peace.

Peace not only for their souls
but for their stories.
Peace not only in memory
but in the systems they should have reshaped.

For every child who never saw their reflection in a textbook,
for every refugee who buried their invention to survive,
for every imprisoned mind that solved what others refused to ask—

let peace fall like rain,
let silence bloom into song,
let the world bow its head
to the ones it failed.

Agnus Dei
qui tollis peccata mundi,

dona eis requiem.

VII. Lux Aeterna – Eternal Light
Lux aeterna luceat eis, Domine.
Let eternal light shine upon them, O Lord.
Not the harsh glare of fame
but the quiet glow of recognition that cannot be extinguished.

Their words are embers in the archive.
Their thoughts ripple through time,
coded into algorithms,
whispered through vaccines,
folded into theories that forgot their names.

But the forgetting ends here.

We lift their names into constellations—
not as memorial,
but as navigation.

Let young minds follow their stars
and know that brilliance
is not always anointed,
but often disguised,
hidden,
silenced,
until the world is ready.

But we are ready now.

This Requiem is not an end—
it is the first word of a new scripture.

Eternal light for the unseen mathematician
Eternal light for the unthanked healer
Eternal light for the poet buried beneath a foreign name

Eternal light for the ones who were always luminous
but lived in a world without candles.

Lux aeterna.
May it shine not only upon them—

but from them.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on March 22, 2025.
