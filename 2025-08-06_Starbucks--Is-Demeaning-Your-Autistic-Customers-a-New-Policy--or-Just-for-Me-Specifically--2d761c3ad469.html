Starbucks: Is Demeaning Your Autistic Customers a New Policy, or Just for Me Specifically?

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

I’ve now been denied water twice at the same Starbucks location — by the same employee — after I filed a federal ADA Title III complaint about them calling the police on me months ago for quietly sitting and crying while autistic, drinking water.

Yesterday, I made my request clear, direct, and lawful.

I walked up to the counter and said:

“I don’t want a coffee right now. May I please have a water?”

When the employee said, “You know the policy,” I replied:

“Yes — and policy is one thing, the law is another. The policy is discriminatory. I am disabled, thirsty, and I would like a glass of water, please.”

That is a request for reasonable accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

What Happened Next

He ignored me. For three minutes.
 He busied himself with other tasks while I stood there waiting — clearly thirsty, clearly asking under ADA rights.

I repeated the request to a second employee — the same woman who had denied me water on a 100+ degree day weeks ago. She smiled at me and said no again.

I told both of them, loudly enough for other customers to hear:

“Water is a human right, and I am thirsty.”

Still nothing.

Witnesses

There were customers in the store who saw me stand there, politely insisting. There were customers outside who I spoke to afterward — two of them didn’t even have purchases, yet they agreed I was right.

They saw what Starbucks did: they denied water to a disabled person, after I told them this was part of an active ADA discrimination case.

Why This is Retaliation

Under the ADA, it is illegal to punish someone for asserting their rights.
 That’s exactly what this was:

  • I disclosed my disability.
  • I disclosed my Title III ADA complaint.
  • I requested a lawful accommodation.
  • They refused — again — in front of witnesses.

This is no longer just a “policy” enforcement. This is retaliation.

The Emotional Cost

I left before I started crying again. I could feel it building — that familiar sense of humiliation, of being small and unwelcome in a public place.

For an autistic person, the message is clear: “You are not wanted here unless you play by rules we apply only when we want to.”

A Question for Starbucks

So I have to ask:
 Is demeaning your autistic customers now an official policy?
 Or is this just for me specifically?

Because I can tell you, the other Starbucks in town has no problem handing me a cup of water. They don’t demand an immediate purchase. They don’t smile while denying a basic human need.

It’s only this store — the same store that called the police on me months ago.

What Happens Next

I’ll be updating the Department of Justice with this latest incident. I’ll be naming the employees. I’ll be including the fact that there were witnesses.

Because Starbucks, you’ve had every chance to address this privately, lawfully, and with dignity.

You chose to make it public.
 Now it will stay that way.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
 Autistic advocate, physicist, and human being who just wanted a glass of water.
