Mark Havens: When Scientific Dishonesty Becomes a Public Disservice

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Science thrives on criticism — but only when that criticism is honest. When someone publicly misrepresents another’s work, after being shown the truth, it’s not just an opinion anymore. It’s misconduct. And when that misconduct is done in public, repeatedly, it crosses into the territory of reputational harm and defamation.

This is what Mark Havens has done to me.

The Falsehoods

In his public Medium review of my EFMW framework — a theory that explicitly contains formal equations, derivations, and definitions — Havens declared:

  • “No explicit equations, derivations, or tensorial formulations are provided for the EFMW framework.”
  • “Nothing to falsify.”
  • “Incoherent.”

These are not critiques of style or sufficiency. These are statements of fact about the content of my work — and they are false.

The Truth

My published EFMW paper contains:

  • An explicit field equation with defined tensors Gμν,Iμν,Tμν.
  • Clear derivations showing the role of each term.
  • Defined variables and falsifiable predictions.

The math was present before Havens wrote his review. Screenshots, publication timestamps, and the paper itself prove this beyond question.

Actual Malice

After I brought this to his attention — providing the equations, pointing him directly to the relevant sections — Havens did nothing to correct the record.

He doubled down.
He kept the original statements live.
He maintained the public falsehood that my work had “no equations” and was “nothing to falsify.”

That’s not debate. That’s knowing misrepresentation — or, at best, reckless disregard for the truth.

Why It Matters

These aren’t harmless insults. In science, accusing someone of having “nothing to falsify” is accusing them of producing pseudo-science. Calling their work “incoherent” in this context is an attack on professional competence.

These claims damage reputations, influence potential collaborations, and erode public trust in honest scientific exchange.

When someone behaves like this, they aren’t serving science — they are serving their own ego and protecting their own narrative, at the expense of the truth.

The Line Has Been Crossed

Mark Havens wants to wear the mantle of “fellow seeker of recursive coherence” — yet he refuses to meet the most basic standard of coherence himself: aligning his public statements with verifiable reality.

When you knowingly state falsehoods about another person’s work in public, you’re not a critic. You’re a bad actor.

A Public Call

I am demanding that Mark Havens:

  1. Retract his false statements in full.
  2. Publish a correction acknowledging that my EFMW work contains formal equations and derivations.
  3. Cease making factually false claims about my work in any forum.

Until then, I will treat his review not as a good-faith critique, but as an act of intellectual dishonesty and reputational attack — and I will continue to document it as such.

I also must now advise Mr. Havens that defamation in a professional setting is civilly actionable, and he should consult with an attorney about his statements in public about my work, if he has any doubts about his legal risk, which is now, in my opinion, considerable.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
Physicist, author, and unwilling target of Mark Havens’ public falsehoods.
