Pete Hegseth’s Meltdown and the Secret War Chat That Triggered It”

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright | June 2025

WASHINGTON, D.C. —
When Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth stormed onto the Pentagon briefing stage last Thursday—angry, defensive, and visibly unraveling—it was more than just an awkward press moment. It was the public climax of a months-long institutional breakdown, triggered not by partisan pressure or media friction, but by something far more serious:

The leak of a private Signal group chat where Hegseth and others were overheard discussing active, unauthorized operational planning for airstrikes in Yemen—before Congressional authorization.

That journalist, Isaac Jacobson, wasn’t supposed to be there. But he was. And he didn’t stay quiet.

The Meltdown: A Secretary in Freefall

At what was billed as a routine defense press briefing, Secretary Hegseth delivered anything but.

Abandoning his prepared remarks on Indo-Pacific strategy, he instead launched into a combative tirade against “cowards in the press,” “rogue embedded assets,” and what he called “deep state informants trying to reverse a lawful military chain of command.”

His tone was hostile. His posture erratic. One Pentagon official later said, “We were watching the psychological collapse of the civilian head of our military, live on camera.”

Reporters were stunned. What had been a tense but manageable controversy had now escalated into a full-blown crisis of confidence in civilian military leadership.

The Signal Chat: A Digital War Room Exposed

The meltdown makes far more sense in light of what preceded it.

Months earlier, in the lead-up to U.S. airstrikes over Yemen, a private encrypted Signal chat was created between Hegseth, former Rep. Tulsi Gabbard, and several defense aides. The purpose of the chat, according to its participants, was “strategic messaging coordination.”

But what actually occurred in that channel, according to journalist Isaac Jacobson who was quietly added to the group, was operational war planning. Not media prep. Not political spin.

In transcripts Jacobson published immediately after the chat session, Hegseth is heard:

Referring to specific target coordinates.

Discussing strike windows and probability assessments.

Asking whether “Trump [had] been looped for external validation.”

This wasn’t Pentagon protocol. It was shadow command decision-making, outside any lawful oversight framework.

The Trap—or the Leak that Sprung Itself

Jacobson’s presence in the chat remains the story’s most mysterious element.

When confronted under oath before Congress, both Hegseth and Gabbard claimed they had “no idea” how he was added. But cyber-forensics experts reviewing the metadata noted that Jacobson’s addition to the group came from a device previously associated with a known ODNI operations relay.

Which leads to the likeliest scenario:
Jacobson was intentionally inserted into the group—either by a sympathetic insider or with the quiet backing of the Director of National Intelligence. Once inside, he documented everything, and published the material within hours.

The result was immediate:

Hegseth’s position became untenable.

Allies questioned the legitimacy of the Yemen strikes.

The Pentagon’s internal firewall between planning and political ambition collapsed.

Tulsi Gabbard’s Dual Role

As a former Congresswoman and current informal defense liaison, Tulsi Gabbard’s involvement deepens the crisis.

While she publicly aligned with Hegseth, insiders report she may have been a vector for the leak itself. One intelligence official noted: “She walks between tribes—left, right, command, countercommand. If anyone could enable a passive breach like this, it’s her.”

Her Congressional testimony was riddled with contradictions, and she has since recused herself from all classified briefings involving the Yemen theater.

Consequences and the Erosion of Command

The fallout continues to spread:

NATO allies have paused coordination on several joint initiatives.

Congressional investigations into unlawful military planning are underway.

The Office of Legal Counsel has issued an internal memo on the illegal delegation of strike authority.

And Hegseth?
Sources confirm he is expected to resign within weeks—possibly under pressure from Joint Chiefs leadership. His once-defiant posture has given way to closed-door damage control.

A System That Still Fights for Itself

What began as a journalist accidentally overhearing war plans became the lever that pried open a façade of control, exposing one of the most dangerous breakdowns of military oversight in modern U.S. history.

Hegseth’s meltdown wasn’t just a public embarrassment.
It was the sound of the system purging a rogue element—live, unfiltered, and unstoppable.

And it may be the moment America remembers as the line where civilian loyalty to power met its match in constitutional resilience.
