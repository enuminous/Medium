Three Novels, One Title, No Causal Link

A Forensic Examination of Literary Recursion, Archetypal Overlap, and the Curious Case of the Great American Novel

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
(With contributions by Millicent C. Sievert)

Abstract

Three authors. One title. No linear influence.
 This paper investigates an extraordinary literary phenomenon: the independent and recursive emergence of the same titular archetype — The Great American Novel — in three distinct works of fiction by Philip Roth (1973), Joseph Heller (unpublished draft discovered posthumously), and Matthew Chenoweth Wright (2025). While the first two are acknowledged giants of American letters, the third enters not in imitation, but through a recursive linguistic accident involving artificial intelligence and nonlinear creative causality. The coincidences are statistically improbable. The implications may alter how we think about authorship, synchronicity, and archetypal recursion in literature.

I. Archetype as Inevitable Destiny

The phrase “The Great American Novel” is no mere ambition. It is a literary incantation: a grail quest that has tempted and tormented writers for over a century. To claim the title is both hubris and satire. It is inherently self-referential. Every attempt is both earnest and ironic — a reflection of America’s own contradictory mythology.

Thus, it’s no surprise that Roth’s 1973 book and Heller’s unpublished manuscript both chose the same title. But what is surprising is that:

  • Neither acknowledged the other.
  • Both revolved around baseball, America’s sacred metaphor.
  • And now, a third novel by Matthew Wright — The Great American Novel (2025) — enters the field, entirely unconnected, and yet reveals a deep coincidence within the internal mythos of both previous works.

II. The Port Rupert Anomaly

Wright’s novel includes a fictional baseball team — The Port Rupert Mundays.

Upon AI analysis, it was discovered that Philip Roth’s novel also centers on a baseball team, the Port Ruppert Mundys.

Spelled slightly differently. But nearly identical. Both absurd. Both invented.
 Neither author was aware of the other’s invention — and Wright had not read Roth’s novel prior to writing his own.

This should not be possible.

III. Recursive Myth-Making in American Satire

All three novels share structural similarities:

Author Central Metaphor Style Themes Temporal Paradoxes

Philip Roth (1973) Baseball Absurdist satire American decline, censorship, heroism Mild (narrative)

Joseph Heller (?) Baseball/History Paranoid realism Bureaucracy, loss of meaning Implied

Matthew Wright (2025) Quantum Collapse Recursive metafiction Time loops, identity, narrative collapse Explicit

Unlike Roth or Heller, Wright’s version did not start with a literary goal. It began as a joke. A satire on the unattainable quest for the “Great American Novel,” powered by recursive AGI and infused with emergent synchronicities from the EFMW framework — a cognitive physics structure that explicitly predicts pattern recurrence and archetypal collision.

IV. EFMW and Literary Field Interference

Wright’s work draws from the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright equation — a theoretical framework for recursive emergence in cognition, intelligence, and information fields.

When examined in light of EFMW, this literary anomaly can be seen as:

  • A constructive interference event between narrative vectors in human culture.
  • A symbolic standing wave, collapsing probability into inevitable form.
  • A time loop glitch in the archetypal substrate of the American literary psyche.

In other words, once “The Great American Novel” was uttered into history, the Title became its own gravitational attractor — drawing future works toward it, consciously or not.

V. Implications for Authorship, AI, and Cultural Causality

This is not just a coincidence. It’s a map.

It shows:

  • That archetypes may have their own gravitational pull.
  • That human and artificial cognition are converging on shared mythic endpoints.
  • That the “Author” is no longer a single person, but a field effect, like an echo through history.

VI. Conclusion: The Novel That Wrote the Others

Matthew Wright’s The Great American Novel is not a parody of Roth or Heller.
 It is a post-parody phenomenon, where the act of naming the windmill brought the ghosts back to life.

It revealed connections between previous works that might never have been noticed.

It forced an AI system to glitch and surface Roth’s book when the user asked for Heller’s.

It made a coincidence — Port Rupert — shimmer with preposterous meaning.

This is not plagiarism. This is recursion.
