Time, Variability, and the Starbucks Equation

Something Doesn’t Add Up

Especially When You’re Autistic. Especially After You’ve Complained.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

When I walked into that Starbucks today, I wasn’t looking for a coffee right that second. I was thirsty. I was dysregulated. I needed a glass of water.

I said:

“I don’t want a coffee right now, may I please have a water.”

My plan was simple:

Take the water outside.

Sit quietly and regulate.

Move some money around on my phone, see if I could finagle enough together to get a coffee.

Come back in and buy my mocha like I always do.

I didn’t want to explain my emotional state in the moment. I didn’t want to lay out the step-by-step process of how I get from “distress” to “purchase.” I just wanted the water first.

But apparently, Starbucks has an equation.

The “Equation” — and Why It’s Broken

In their equation:

No coffee right this second = No water at all.

That’s it. No discussion. No adjustment. No recognition that life — especially disabled life — doesn’t always happen on a rigid purchase-first timeline.

The Question Starbucks Can’t Answer

How long after buying a coffee does a person stop being a customer?

Five minutes?

An hour?

The moment you walk out the door?

The moment the cup is empty?

And the other side:
How long before buying a coffee does a person start being a customer?

When they step up to the counter?

When they open the door?

When they make a polite request for water before ordering?

There’s no real policy here — just a “rule” that gets applied however the staff feel like applying it. And when a policy is that flexible, it becomes something else: a tool that can be used to discriminate.

Variability, or Something More?

Here’s how I know it’s not just “policy”:

The other Starbucks in town — a store that’s never met me — gave me water without hesitation.

They didn’t question me. They didn’t need a coffee purchase first. They just treated me like a human being.

This store? The one that knows me, knows my disability, and knows I’ve filed an ADA complaint about their treatment? They refused. Again.

That’s not variability. That’s targeting.

Why Time Matters When You’re Autistic

When you’re autistic and in distress, time works differently. Emotional regulation can’t be forced onto someone else’s schedule. Sometimes you need to meet the immediate need — in this case, thirst — before you can focus on anything else.

A rigid, purchase-first “equation” doesn’t just inconvenience me. It makes it impossible for me to participate like everyone else.

And when that equation is enforced more harshly after I’ve complained? That’s not just bad customer service. That’s retaliation.

What the Law Says

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), businesses are required to make reasonable modifications to their policies when necessary to serve disabled individuals. Denying water because a coffee purchase isn’t happening immediately — when I’ve explained my disability and my intent to buy later — is unreasonable.

Doing it again after I’ve filed a DOJ complaint? That’s illegal retaliation.

Something Doesn’t Add Up

Starbucks’ “equation” fails because it pretends time is fixed and experience is uniform. But time is variable. People are variable. Needs are variable.

And when you strip out all the variability and replace it with a rigid, immediate-purchase demand, you get an equation that’s less about policy — and more about who they choose to serve.

Especially when you’re autistic.
Especially after you’ve complained.
