What a “glitch” really means—and why it hurts more than you think

The Lie at the Center of the System

What a “glitch” really means—and why it hurts more than you think

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Dedicated to Douglas Adams, who knew that I was the center before I did.

On April 2, 2025, I called my cellular service provider, Page Plus Cellular, in response to a strange and disturbing series of text messages. These messages came from short code 611611, and each one told me the same thing:

“We don’t have any record in our system with your information.”

Let that sink in.

The system that bills me monthly, that I interact with regularly, that stores my name, number, PIN, and payment history, was now sending me a message—on its own infrastructure—claiming I didn’t exist.

That’s not just a contradiction. That’s a violation of logic, of trust, and for someone like me—an autistic physicist—a direct wound to the part of my mind that seeks order, pattern, and truth. When reality contradicts itself and denies the contradiction, it is not just confusing—it is painful.

And it is happening everywhere.

The Conversation

When I called to resolve the issue, the representative:

Claimed to have no idea why the message was sent

Required me to read the message back four times, failing to understand it until I literally quoted it verbatim

Used the word “glitch”, a term I interrupted immediately:

> “If you use the word glitch, you’re acknowledging a systemic failure. That makes this legally actionable.”

Confirmed the call was recorded

Verified my full identity: phone number, name, PIN, and account info

Suggested I might want to change my number to stop receiving the message

Was told clearly that this event would be turned into a public Medium article and documented in full

Let me be even more direct:
This is not a story about bad customer service.
It’s a story about how automation, contradiction, and corporate abstraction collide—and gaslight us.

What a “Glitch” Really Means

Let’s talk about that word.

“Glitch” is thrown around to defuse responsibility. It’s a digital shrug. A way to say “don’t worry about it” without fixing the cause. But in truth?

A “glitch” is:

A real event inside a system

A causal failure with real-world consequences

A legally significant occurrence when it affects humans in damaging or misleading ways

And when companies acknowledge glitches without accountability, they normalize the idea that systems can fail, lie, and harm, and no one has to do anything about it.

Why This Matters (to Me—and Everyone Else)

As someone on the autism spectrum, logic isn’t optional for me. It's survival.
I build my interactions, my routines, my boundaries—my sense of safety—around consistency and truth. When a system breaks that? It’s not annoying. It’s destabilizing.

And I am not the only one.

Every day, systems lie to people with templated messages, false denials, and disconnected excuses. People are:

Gaslit into thinking they don’t exist in systems they pay to access

Told that machines are broken, and no one is responsible

Expected to accept contradictions as “normal”

Dismissed when they try to bring clarity to chaos

This isn't just bureaucracy—it’s epistemic violence. It's the erosion of trust in our infrastructures, one automated message at a time.

And the most vulnerable people feel it the hardest:

Autistic folks

Neurodivergent thinkers

Elderly users

People with trauma who rely on structure

Anyone trying to live with dignity inside someone else’s system

The Real Message

Page Plus told me I didn’t exist.
But I was paying them. I was receiving service.
I gave them my name. My PIN. My voice.
I told them what happened. I warned them this story would be made public.
And now, here we are.

This isn’t just about me.
This is about how easily truth is deleted in digital systems—how often the lie is automated and the real human is asked to change themselves to make the lie go away.

No.
I will not change my number.
I will not disappear to protect your contradiction.
I will not be erased.

You already had my information.
You still do.
And now, so does everyone else.
