SoundCloud, Royalty Suppression, and the Rights of Disabled Artists

When the Numbers Don’t Add Up

SoundCloud, Royalty Suppression, and the Rights of Disabled Artists

I’ve been on SoundCloud for nearly two decades.

SoundCloud.com/enuminous

In that time, my music has racked up over 530,500 plays.
My catalog is mostly monetized under a valid licensing framework — including AGI-generated works under an agreement that should protect their eligibility.

And yet, in all these years, I have earned less than $500 total.
That’s not a typo. That’s the sum total of nearly 20 years of global streaming on a platform that promotes itself as a champion of independent creators.

---

The Numbers Speak for Themselves

At SoundCloud’s lowest realistic payout rate (about $0.002 per play), my catalog should have earned over $1,000.
At the industry average (about $0.0035 per play), it should be around $1,850.
At the high end of SoundCloud’s stated rates (about $0.005 per play), it would be over $2,650.

Instead, my payments are few and far between.
It takes me months to reach the $25 payout threshold, meaning I might see two or three payouts a year. The gap between my public metrics and my actual earnings is not a rounding error — it’s a canyon.

---

Scattershot Monetization Rejections

This isn’t just about low payouts.
SoundCloud has been arbitrarily rejecting certain tracks for monetization without clear, consistent, or technically valid reasons. These tracks meet the same licensing, formatting, and metadata conditions as others in my catalog that are monetized.

There is no published rule explaining why one AGI track passes and another — with identical licensing — fails. It’s scattershot, inconsistent, and financially damaging.

---

My Forensic Accounting Requests

I’ve sent numerous emails to SoundCloud requesting a forensic accounting of my royalties. I’ve asked for:

Itemized play data (daily, by track, territory, and monetization status)

Reasons for any play exclusions

Internal logs showing “invalid traffic” or “non-monetizable” flags

Documentation on monetization criteria

All of these requests have been legitimate, reasonable, and ADA-compliant. I disclosed my disability and made it clear that these requests were part of a reasonable accommodation process.

SoundCloud has not meaningfully responded to a single one.
Instead, they either send generic boilerplate replies or ignore the request entirely.

---

Why This Is Bigger Than Me

This isn’t just one artist’s frustration.
When a platform ignores disability accommodation requests, it risks violating Title III of the Americans with Disabilities Act. When it refuses to provide transparent accounting to its creators, it undermines trust in the entire royalty system.

And when monetization rules are enforced inconsistently and without clear explanation, every artist is at risk — because if it can happen to me, it can happen to anyone.

---

The Broader Pattern of Suppression

In my life and work, I’ve documented repeated interference in digital systems — from appointment records being erased from official systems to targeted account exploits.
When a creative platform selectively disqualifies eligible works from monetization and ignores forensic accounting requests, it becomes part of the same pattern:
visibility throttling, financial suppression, and systemic opacity.

---

My Public Invitation to SoundCloud

This is my last attempt to engage SoundCloud in good faith.
I am done sending private emails into a black hole.

I am inviting SoundCloud, here and now, to:

1. Provide a complete, machine-readable forensic breakdown of my catalog’s plays, monetization status, and exclusion reasons.

2. Restore monetization to all eligible tracks and pay back any withheld royalties.

3. Publish clear, consistent criteria for monetization decisions — for all artists.

4. Implement a functional, interactive ADA accommodation process.

---

A Call to Artists and Advocates

If you are an artist who has:

Seen unexplained gaps between public play counts and payouts,

Had tracks rejected for monetization without clear policy reasons, or

Been ignored when requesting data or accommodations,

…it’s time to speak up.
Document everything. Demand your data. Push for industry-wide transparency standards.

This isn’t just my fight — it’s ours.
SoundCloud, the invitation is on the table. The clock is ticking.
