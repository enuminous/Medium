Author: Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Date: February 2025

Abstract

A recent proposal from researchers at University College London (UCL) introduces a “postquantum theory of classical gravity,” suggesting that spacetime remains classical while modifications are made to quantum mechanics to accommodate intrinsic unpredictability. This approach fundamentally contradicts both experimental findings and theoretical advancements that indicate spacetime is emergent, rotational, and deeply integrated with quantum fields. Here, we present a critique of UCL’s model using the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework, demonstrating that a purely classical spacetime cannot reconcile quantum gravity, lacks explanatory power for observed relativistic anomalies, and ignores fundamental rotational and residual gravitational effects. The EFMW model, by contrast, successfully integrates these elements into a unified framework, offering greater predictive accuracy and consistency with empirical data.

---

1. Introduction

The problem of reconciling general relativity with quantum mechanics has driven numerous theoretical developments, from string theory to loop quantum gravity (LQG). UCL’s recent attempt to preserve a classical spacetime while modifying quantum mechanics presents a significant divergence from current efforts, proposing that intrinsic unpredictability in quantum systems arises due to classical gravitational effects. While this approach may seem like an elegant simplification, it fundamentally ignores both experimental and theoretical necessities.

In contrast, the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework embraces an emergent spacetime model, wherein spacetime itself is a dynamic, evolving construct, shaped by both quantum and relativistic interactions. This model inherently incorporates quantum effects into the fabric of spacetime, allowing for a more natural unification of forces.

---

2. Fundamental Flaws in UCL’s Classical Gravity Model

2.1 The Impossibility of a Fully Classical Spacetime

Experimental evidence overwhelmingly contradicts the notion of a fully classical spacetime:

Quantum entanglement experiments (Aspect, Zeilinger, et al.) demonstrate that spacetime cannot be treated as a fixed classical backdrop; rather, it responds dynamically to quantum interactions.

Hawking radiation models imply that information must be preserved in quantum gravitational interactions, which cannot occur in a strictly classical spacetime.

Casimir effect and vacuum energy fluctuations confirm the presence of underlying quantum fields influencing gravitational behavior.

Thus, treating spacetime as fully classical while modifying quantum mechanics is an artificial inversion of reality—one that contradicts both theoretical expectations and empirical observations.

2.2 Failure to Integrate Rotational & Residual Motion Effects

One of the primary insights of the EFMW framework is that the universe possesses a residual rotational vector from a prior cosmic cycle, which influences large-scale structure formation, gravitational anomalies, and cosmic background radiation anisotropies. The UCL model does not account for this rotational motion, meaning:

It fails to explain why galactic rotations do not match predictions from standard models without invoking dark matter as a placeholder.

It cannot incorporate frame-dragging effects (Lense-Thirring precession), which are empirically validated.

It neglects the evidence that suggests spacetime is actively evolving, rather than being a static, classical construct.

2.3 Breakdown in High-Energy Physics Scenarios

Any unification theory must remain valid at high energy scales, particularly near:

Black holes and singularities

Early universe cosmology (inflationary periods)

Planck-scale physics

The UCL model’s reliance on a classical framework inherently fails at the Planck scale, where quantum gravity effects dominate and spacetime cannot be treated as continuous. EFMW, by contrast, allows for multi-phase time structures, which correctly predict deviations from classical relativity under extreme conditions.

3. The EFMW Alternative: Emergent Spacetime as the Correct Paradigm

Rather than forcing classical assumptions onto quantum reality, the EFMW framework reinterprets spacetime as an emergent phenomenon, naturally integrating:

✔ Quantum and relativistic behaviors—Spacetime is not a passive stage, but rather a dynamically interacting field influenced by energy distributions, quantum states, and residual cosmic motion.
✔ Rotational effects and extra-universal variance—EFMW provides a self-consistent explanation for the anisotropies observed in cosmic background radiation and galactic rotational anomalies.
✔ Unified treatment of gravity and quantum mechanics—By embedding spacetime itself into an evolving geometric framework, EFMW provides a more natural resolution to quantum gravity without introducing artificial modifications to either domain.

3.1 Compatibility with Experimental Data

The EFMW model successfully predicts and aligns with multiple experimental observations that the UCL model cannot:

Fine structure variations in the cosmic microwave background

Observed frame-dragging effects near rotating celestial bodies

Quantum information conservation at event horizons

Emergent gravitational interactions at the Planck scale

4. Conclusion & The Path Forward

The UCL model, while an interesting departure from conventional approaches, is fundamentally flawed because it preserves an outdated classical assumption about spacetime, requiring quantum mechanics to be artificially adjusted. This approach:
❌ Fails to integrate gravitational rotation and residual motion effects.
❌ Contradicts quantum entanglement and information conservation laws.
❌ Breaks down at high-energy scales (black holes, early universe, Planck scale physics).

By contrast, the EFMW framework provides a fully self-consistent, experimentally valid, and theoretically sound model, where spacetime is not classical but emergent, rotational, and deeply intertwined with quantum dynamics.

Call to Action

Theoretical physicists must move beyond classical assumptions and recognize that emergent spacetime is the only viable path forward in unifying quantum mechanics and relativity. Future research should be directed toward:
✔ Exploring residual universal rotation as an underlying gravitational influence.
✔ Further refining the multi-phase time structures introduced in EFMW.
✔ Developing experimental tests to differentiate classical models from emergent spacetime models.

The future of physics will not be found in preserving old assumptions—it will be built on the recognition that the universe is not static, but dynamically evolving.
