Time as an Illusion and the Role of Gravity and Speed

The concept of time as merely a perception of movement in the universe is certainly thought-provoking. From a relativity standpoint, time is not an absolute, independent entity but is interwoven with space itself, forming spacetime. In this view, what we perceive as “time” is directly linked to an object’s motion (speed) and the curvature of spacetime caused by gravity.

Gravity, Speed, and “Metabolism of Matter”

In the context of general relativity, gravity affects time — or more precisely, spacetime. The stronger the gravitational field, the slower time passes, relative to regions with weaker gravity. This effect has been confirmed experimentally, such as with GPS satellites, which account for both the gravitational time dilation and the relative speeds at which they orbit Earth. This suggests that what we call “time” is indeed relative, and its passage depends on the object’s speed and the gravitational field it resides in.

This aligns with Jarno’s idea that gravity and speed define a kind of “frequency” or metabolism of matter. In simpler terms, the faster an object moves or the stronger the gravitational field it experiences, the more stretched or compressed its time becomes, affecting everything within it, including the aging process.

Time as a Human Construct

The idea that time could be an illusion or at least a construct shaped by our perceptions is rooted in philosophical discussions, particularly those around Bergson’s concepts of la durée (duration). From this angle, time is not something that exists in and of itself, but rather something that humans perceive as they experience movement, change, and causality. We measure time based on movement, such as the rotation of the Earth or the ticking of a clock, which brings us to the question of whether time is just a human construct for measuring the passage of events, as Jarno suggests.

The Fourth Dimension

In physics, time is often referred to as the fourth dimension, with the three spatial dimensions making up the rest of the fabric of spacetime. This concept comes from Einstein’s special relativity, where time is treated as part of the four-dimensional continuum. The argument that time is just a movement or sequence of changes in space and matter itself — an effect of gravity and velocity — pushes the boundary of this fourth-dimensional understanding. It raises the question of whether time, as we experience it, is emergent from the interactions of matter and energy.

In a sense, speed and gravity might dictate how “time” manifests in different regions of spacetime, but in the absence of these factors, time may become irrelevant or not even observable as we know it.

Conclusion

Jarno’s perspective opens up an exciting line of thinking that aligns with some of the most speculative ideas in both physics and philosophy. By seeing time as a manifestation of motion (influenced by gravity and speed), we might be able to reframe how we understand the flow of time in a more unified way — one that challenges conventional ideas of time as an independent, linear progression.

This could potentially offer new insights into quantum mechanics and the deeper nature of reality, possibly even offering new avenues for reconciling quantum theory with general relativity.

There’s no universal time that is shared by all observers across the universe. Instead, time is perceived and measured locally, and it’s influenced by the motion, gravity, and energy distribution in that local frame.

  • Time dilation (special relativity) states that time can be measured differently depending on the relative velocity between two observers. If you’re moving at high velocity relative to an observer, you will experience time more slowly than they do. However, this effect is frame-dependent and applies only locally to that observer.
  • Gravitational time dilation (general relativity) tells us that clocks at different gravitational potentials (i.e., closer to or farther from a massive object) will tick at different rates. An observer near a black hole will experience time differently than an observer far away from it.

These principles show that time is not an absolute quantity but one that depends on the observer’s position and velocity relative to the observed system. In essence, there is no global time — only time as experienced in local frames.
