Cashli Just Destroyed My Account After I Blew the Whistle — The Cover-Up Is Worse Than the Crime

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Earlier today, I sent an email to Cashli’s team regarding potentially illegal behavior I had uncovered in their app. Specifically, I raised red flags about:

The requirement to email sensitive bank information manually, even after using industry-standard verification via Plaid.

The presence of deceptive legal language pressuring users to authorize charges before they’re fully informed.

My explicit ADA accommodation request to communicate by email only, due to a disability — a request they ignored.

My clearly worded directive that under no circumstances was I to be charged.

I attached a Medium article detailing all of this, and I made it clear I was speaking publicly — as a whistleblower — to protect other consumers from potential harm.

Instead of replying with an explanation, they destroyed my account.

A Screenshot Tells the Story

When I attempted to log in, I was met with a now-infamous popup:

> “Closed account. A previously active account has been closed by action of the customer or the Cashli team…”

Except I didn’t close the account.

They did — in retaliation.

Just days before, I had received another popup, this one far more alarming: a screen stating that my bank account required further review and instructing me to send my bank name and last four digits of my account number via email.
Yes — via plain email. After already linking through Plaid.

Let’s be very clear:
That is not standard practice. That is a liability. That is a data security threat.

Then comes the “FTC Compliance” claim — a smokescreen. Their app states that their Terms of Use were written by a Federal Trade Commission (FTC) compliance lawyer and are legally binding in court. Yet, within the same breath, they demand bank credentials through email and try to lock users into a $3.88/month “platform fee” under layers of coercive legalese.

You can say your contract is enforceable.
But you don’t get to break federal law while saying it.

What They Did Wrong — In Plain Language

1. Retaliated against a federal whistleblower, violating protections under the Dodd-Frank Act (15 U.S.C. § 78u-6(h))

2. Violated the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) by ignoring a reasonable request for email-only communication

3. Cut off a verified customer from accessing their own financial information without explanation

4. Ignored industry-standard protocols by requesting sensitive banking info over unsecured channels

5. Used intimidating legal language to pressure consent for charges with no meaningful recourse

The Cover-Up Has Begun

Let’s be blunt: Cashli is now trying to hide its tracks.
They locked me out of my account not because I did something wrong — but because I told the truth, publicly, and with receipts.

And the oldest lesson in the book applies here:
It’s not just the initial act that sinks the ship — it’s the cover-up.

America, Wake Up

Fintech apps like this are everywhere, targeting vulnerable populations — those without access to traditional banks, those who are disabled, underemployed, or financially strained. They use flashy design, legal-sounding disclaimers, and buzzwords like "FTC compliance" to create the illusion of legitimacy.

But real compliance means transparency.
It means honoring ADA accommodations.
It means not shutting down a customer's account for speaking out.

This is what happens when regulatory oversight fails to keep up with fast-moving apps built on profit-first, ethics-last frameworks.

I am not the only person this has happened to. But I may be the first to say it this loudly.

To Cashli: you’ve made a very serious mistake.

To America: pay attention. The apps in your pocket may be the next crime scene.

We’re watching now.
We’re documenting everything.
And we are not going away.
