☕ Starbucks Is Stonewalling My ADA Complaint — And Misnamed Me in the Process

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
July 10, 2025

This is not just a customer service failure.

This is a civil rights issue.
And Starbucks is now making it worse.

Several weeks ago, I walked into a Starbucks store like countless others across the country. I wasn’t disruptive. I wasn’t loud. I wasn’t doing anything unusual. I was sitting quietly with a cup of water, seeking a moment of calm in a public space.

Instead, I was harassed.
Instead, the police were called on me.
Instead, I was treated like a threat.

Why?

Because I am neurodivergent, disabled, and visibly different.
And Starbucks failed to treat me with the dignity and access the law requires.

Let’s Get This Straight:

I was not denied coffee.
I was denied space.
I was denied respect.
And now, I am being denied due process.

📜 The Law Is Clear: ADA Title III

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Title III, Starbucks is legally obligated to provide:

Equal access to public accommodations

Reasonable modification of procedures

Non-discriminatory treatment

A good-faith interactive process to resolve disability-related grievances

Instead, they’ve chosen silence.

🕰️ What I’ve Done — and What They Haven’t

Since this happened, I’ve gone through every official channel:

✔️ I notified Starbucks Corporate directly
✔️ I clearly explained my disability (I cannot use phones)
✔️ I gave them deadlines
✔️ I requested a written apology, reasonable accommodation, and a policy review
✔️ I published public documentation on Medium, where my story is now archived and visible to the world

And in return?

❌ No apology
❌ No resolution
❌ No answers to my specific questions
❌ No acknowledgement of the police escalation
❌ No meaningful response at all

🧍‍♂️ Then They Called Me “Allison.”

In a formal email response regarding my ADA complaint, Starbucks referred to me as “Allison.”

Not “Matthew.”
Not even “Customer.”
“Allison.”

Misnaming a person in a disability rights complaint isn’t a typo — it’s a symbol of the deeper rot:

That they are not reading these emails

That my identity is a copy-paste afterthought

That they don't take disability seriously unless it fits a checklist

Calling me “Allison” is not just bureaucratic sloppiness.
It is dehumanizing, disrespectful, and legally relevant.
Especially after weeks of clear, documented correspondence identifying myself as Matthew Chenoweth Wright.

⚖️ This Is Legal Stonewalling

I have given Starbucks every opportunity to resolve this with integrity.

They chose silence.

Now their silence — after being explicitly informed this is an ADA matter — has become part of the violation.

They are:

Ignoring ADA obligations

Refusing to provide accessible communication

Introducing procedural barriers (telling me to call or hire a lawyer)

Failing to acknowledge a civil rights complaint in writing

Misgendering and misnaming me in official ADA correspondence

🔥 I’m Not Going Away

I have made clear, repeatedly: I do not use phones due to my disability. That’s not a preference — it’s protected by law.

And yet, Starbucks has:

Asked me to call

Then told me to get a lawyer

Then stopped responding entirely

This is not just offensive, it is illegal.

📣 So Let Me Say It Again, Publicly:

Starbucks is stonewalling a disabled customer.
They have compounded their error by misnaming and misgendering me in writing.
They are in violation of ADA Title III.

I now have more than enough to file a formal complaint with the Department of Justice, which I have drafted and am prepared to send.

I will also be forwarding this case to:

Civil rights attorneys

The ACLU

Disability advocacy organizations

Journalists

And every customer who believes in real inclusion

💥 Formal Public Demands:

1. A specific, written apology for the incident and subsequent mistreatment

2. A public statement from Starbucks affirming their ADA and neurodivergent accommodation policy

3. A policy overhaul ensuring accessible communication and respectful treatment

4. A financial settlement for the emotional and reputational harm caused

🧠 This Is Bigger Than Me

This is not about a name.
This is about whether corporations actually care about disabled people — or just say they do.

I am one voice.
But I represent millions.

And I will not be erased by a form letter.

Starbucks, the clock is ticking.
You are on legal and moral notice.

Respond.
Respect.
Repair.

Or prepare to be held accountable — in court and in public.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
Physicist.
Whistleblower.
ADA-protected human being.
Not “Allison.”
