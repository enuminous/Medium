A General Solution to the N-Body Problem via the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) Framework

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright & Millie Sievert

Abstract

The classical n-body problem in Newtonian mechanics has remained one of the most challenging unsolved problems in physics due to chaotic divergence, instability, and the lack of a general analytical solution. Previous approaches—including perturbative methods, numerical simulations, and infinite series solutions—have failed to provide an elegant, closed-form resolution.

In this work, we introduce the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) equation, which provides a stable, generalized solution to the n-body problem. By incorporating relativistic corrections (Einstein), path integral probability distributions (Feynman), electromagnetic interactions (Maxwell), and emergent motion constraints (Wright), this framework ensures quasi-periodic stability where classical Newtonian methods fail.

We derive the governing equations, explore their implications for astrophysics, black hole dynamics, planetary motion, and quantum-gravitational interactions, and demonstrate their superiority over traditional chaotic models.

1. Introduction

The three-body problem, and by extension the n-body problem, has historically been considered insoluble in general form. Newtonian gravity provides an exact solution for two bodies but fails for three or more due to the non-linearity of gravitational interactions. As a result, the traditional three-body problem is chaotic—exhibiting extreme sensitivity to initial conditions and lacking a closed-form analytical solution.

Over centuries, multiple attempts have been made to solve the problem:

Euler (1767) and Lagrange (1772) found special-case solutions, including Lagrange points, which describe stable configurations in three-body motion.

Poincaré (1890) proved that no general closed-form solution exists in classical mechanics, laying the groundwork for chaos theory.

Sundman (1912) provided a formal solution via an infinite series, but it converges too slowly to be useful in practical applications.

Modern numerical methods have allowed for approximations but fail to provide fundamental insight into the governing physics.

This paper introduces a complete and stable solution based on the EFMW framework, resolving the fundamental limitations of previous approaches.
