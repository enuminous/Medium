Boring

The Language of Complicity

By Simone de Bouvier

“I don’t understand why the Jeffrey Epstein case would be of interest to anybody. It’s pretty boring stuff.”
— Donald J. Trump

To call atrocity boring is to speak with the voice of power at rest.

This is not ignorance; it is something far more dangerous. It is a posture of disengagement chosen by those who wish to evade responsibility while preserving domination. It is the carefully calculated shrug of the one who knows—and does not care to be known as such.

The child whose voice is silenced, the survivor whose trauma is archived rather than heard, and the names that vanish into sealed ledgers and flight logs—these are not boring. They are unbearable. And so, we call them boring.
That, too, is an act.

Boredom is not a neutral emotion.
It is the luxury of those protected by the status quo.

To be bored by injustice is to be insulated from its consequences.
To be bored by systemic abuse is to admit that its victims are not real to you.

When Trump declares the Epstein case “boring,” he is not speaking as a bystander. He is announcing his position—above concern, beyond empathy, and comfortably seated within the architecture of silence that has always surrounded crimes committed by the powerful against the powerless.

He is, in essence, telling us:

“I am unbothered by this horror. And I expect you to be, too.”

Every atrocity begins with the refusal to see.

But complicity begins with the refusal to stay awake.

To follow the Epstein case to its logical end—to names, to institutions, to state involvement and elite participation—is to threaten a world built on elite impunity. It is not boring; it is destabilizing.

And yet boredom becomes the polite veil that shields us from our discomfort.

To be bored is to choose not to care.

In existential terms, this is a form of bad faith—a rejection of our radical freedom and responsibility to respond to truth, even when truth threatens the meaning we’ve made of our world.

There is a name for this collective disengagement.

We call it the Silence Pact.

It is the agreement among the comfortable to avert their eyes. It is the pact of the media not to pursue. The pact of the politician not to dig. The pact of the citizen not to ask too many questions. And the pact of the survivor—to suffer without asking the world to change.

But that pact is not immutable.
It can be broken.

And it must be—if we are to live as free people rather than functionaries of moral anesthesia.

“If the global exposure of child trafficking networks involving the world’s richest elites is boring to you, you’re either lying—or you’re part of the silence pact.”

This is not rhetorical. It is a confrontation.

A world that can ignore such crimes is not a stable world. It is a world built on repression, illusion, and the sacrifice of truth to convenience.

No liberation—personal, political, or philosophical—is possible while this silence persists.

The opposite of boredom is not outrage. It is attention.

To attend to what is painful is to choose freedom over comfort, truth over anesthesia, responsibility over denial.

And so, I will say this plainly:

I will not be bored.
I will not look away.
I will not accept silence where speech is needed, nor complicity where moral clarity is demanded.

To those who would call this “boring,” I say: you are describing your soul. Not the world.

Simone de Bouvier
Essayist, Existentialist, Woman of No Illusions
