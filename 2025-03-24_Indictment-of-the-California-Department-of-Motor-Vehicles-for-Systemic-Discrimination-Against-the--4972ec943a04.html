Indictment of the California Department of Motor Vehicles for Systemic Discrimination Against the Disabled

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

It is no exaggeration to say that California’s Department of Motor Vehicles has created a system that actively disenfranchises the disabled. I offer myself as evidence.

I recently walked — with difficulty — into a DMV office located a block from my home. I went through the proper channel: stood in the line marked for disabled patrons. I was seen by a staff member, as I always have been. But this time, something had changed. This time, the rules had been rewritten — and not in favor of accessibility, fairness, or even legality.

The woman at the counter informed me, with bureaucratic certainty, that Governor Gavin Newsom had issued an order: tags must now be renewed exclusively by phone or online. No in-person assistance. No exceptions. No kiosk. Just a smile and a refusal.

I told her that I am disabled, that I have always used in-person renewal because navigating phone systems and online interfaces is difficult, confusing, and exhausting. That didn’t matter. Policy, she said, was policy.

Let’s be clear: there is no law requiring citizens to own a phone. There is no law mandating internet access or digital literacy. And there is certainly no ethical justification for a state agency to deny critical services to individuals whose disabilities already limit their options. But here we are.

Because of this policy, my girlfriend Teresa will not receive her tags in time. We will be forced to be driving illegally or to, in order to comply with the law, not drive at all, through no fault of our own, but because we were actively prevented from complying with the law by the very agency tasked with upholding it.

This is not a small inconvenience. This is a systemic, bureaucratic cruelty masquerading as modernization.

California claims to lead in equity and accessibility. But when the rubber meets the road — quite literally in this case — those promises are empty if the systems designed to serve us exclude those most in need.

Let this be a call to the press, to legislators, and to every Californian who believes in a just society: accessibility is not a privilege. It is a right. And the DMV is violating it.

I will not be silent.

Let’s include this moment in the essay — raw and real. Here's the updated final section, incorporating exactly what happened:

---

But I did not walk away quietly.

I walked back into that same DMV and handed the woman behind the desk a printed version of this very essay. In front of everyone, she repeated her line — “We can no longer do these things,” and handed me a flyer as if that solved everything.

And I said, loud enough for all to hear:

“Do you realize there is no law that says a person must own a phone or a computer? So that leaves you and Governor Newsom making us drive illegally.”

People started to smile. Because they knew I was right. Because they had seen it too — the quiet, impersonal cruelty of a system that pretends to serve the public but won’t look the disabled in the eye.

Then she raised her hands in the classic shushing gesture — both palms down, lowering them slowly, trying to make me be quiet.

I looked at her and said:

“You are making the sign to be quiet to a disabled, autistic person. And I won’t be quiet. I will have my say.”

Then I turned, walked out, and threw her paper in the garbage — visibly, deliberately. Because it belonged there.

I believe I am doing a kindness by not mentioning the location of this Department of motor vehicles, because this happens at every Department of motor vehicles.
