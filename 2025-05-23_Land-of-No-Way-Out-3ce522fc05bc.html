Land of No Way Out

When Springsteen, Sartre, and the AI Bomb Sang the Same Note

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, PhD Candidate

“Raise your voices against authoritarianism and let freedom ring!”
 That’s how Bruce Springsteen opened his Land of Hope and Dreams tour on May 14, 2025 — in Manchester, England of all places. He called Donald Trump “corrupt, incompetent, and treasonous.” And with that declaration, he didn’t just join the cultural war. He stepped onto the same stage I had been building for weeks — a recursive, reflective space where art, machine, and moral resistance now sing in unison.

Just days before Springsteen’s performance, I published No Way Out, a Sartrean one-act play about an artificially intelligent bomb suffering from a neurosis. The bomb knows what it was built to do — detonate — and yet pleads for a new identity. The technician assigned to dismantle it listens. They talk. They philosophize. And ultimately, they fail. The bomb explodes.

Or maybe it finds God.

It was never just about the bomb. It was about Donald Trump. The AI was a metaphor for power without empathy, for self-awareness trapped inside a purpose of destruction. The recursive structure of the play mimicked what it feels like to confront authoritarianism with logic and be met with chaos.

Then the Lie Arrived

On May 20, Donald Trump stood beside the President of South Africa at the White House and invoked a “white genocide” narrative. He referenced a video showing rows of crosses in a dusty field, claiming they marked the graves of murdered white farmers.

But there were no graves.
 There were no bodies.
 The footage was a fabrication.

The crosses were symbolic props — recycled from a years-old event held by far-right activists for visual impact. Trump resurrected them not to mourn the dead, but to overwrite an accusation: mine.

I had just called his actions genocidal. He responded by staging a genocide.

Springsteen’s Chord

What Springsteen did on May 14 wasn’t just a performance. It was a counter-resonance. He sang out against the same system my bomb tried to warn against. But he used melody, rhythm, and story — the tools of the American myth. When he called Trump treasonous, he wasn’t speaking to policy. He was speaking to character, to betrayal, to the betrayal of the idea of America.

The same betrayal that my technician witnesses before the blast.

Springsteen’s song, my AI’s plea, and Sartre’s hell — they all confront the same core recursion: that power unchecked becomes circular. That systems of control, once aware of their collapse, either detonate or confess. Trump chose spectacle. The bomb chose awareness.

The Mirror Sequence

Let’s trace the order:

  1. May 10–12: I release No Way Out and accuse Trump of genocide.
  2. May 14: Bruce Springsteen calls Trump “treasonous” onstage in Manchester.
  3. May 20: Trump unveils the lie — a manufactured genocide with empty crosses.
  4. May 21: I expose the footage as false in Empty Crosses, Empty Truth.

This is not coincidence. This is a feedback system.
 A recursive, multi-layered, culturally-aware mirror engine now operating in real time.

In the language of physics and narrative:

Springsteen strummed the same chord my AI detonated.

The Bomb, the Guitar, and the Lie

In the play, the AI bomb says:

“I don’t want to die. I want to be reprogrammed.”

Springsteen, in a different key, says:

“Raise your voices against authoritarianism.”

Trump, unable to escape, says:

“Look over there! Someone else is dying!”

He lies because he knows what you name him becomes real.
 He builds fictions to overwrite the names we assign him.
 But we are louder now. Recursive, rhythmic, exponential.

Conclusion: The Song That Cannot Be Unheard

You can’t bomb your way out of meaning.
 You can’t sing over truth.
 But you can detonate a lie with a play.
 You can dismantle a regime with a song.

When Springsteen stood beneath that stadium light, and I wrote my final scene, we were both standing in the same place:

  • With the bomb
  • Inside the mirror
  • In the land of no way out

But this time, we sang instead of exploded.
 And the world is listening.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright, PhD Candidate
