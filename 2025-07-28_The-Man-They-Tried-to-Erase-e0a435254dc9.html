The Silent Erasure of Sergeant Harold “Lou” Wright, CHP

The Man They Tried to Erase

The Silent Erasure of Sergeant Harold “Lou” Wright, CHP

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

💼 A Badge, a Billboard, and Then Nothing

My grandfather, Sergeant Harold “Lou” Wright, was a decorated officer of the California Highway Patrol.
A man of quiet strength, of profound service, and unmistakable public presence.

In the 1950s, his face adorned statewide safety billboards, representing the very image of CHP integrity.
By 1948, he had already begun working as personal driver and bodyguard to Governor Earl Warren, one of the most powerful political figures in California and, eventually, the United States.

My grandfather was the kind of officer others pointed to and said,

“That’s the one who gets things done.”

“That’s the one who protects the ones who matter.”

And then—he vanished.

Not from our lives. He died in 1965.

But from the record.

🚫 The Lie That Buried Him

When my grandfather died—on the job, in uniform—my grandmother, June Wright, was told that he was “off duty.”

That lie was not an accident.

That lie:

Denied him a CHP funeral

Prevented flag protocol

Halted public announcement

Erased his name from the memorial rolls

Denied my grandmother the pension she was owed

They said, “Off duty,” as if he were no longer theirs.
As if his decades of service could be erased with a checkbox.

But it wasn’t true.

⚖️ The Lawyer Who Uncovered the Truth

A lawyer intervened.
Records were reviewed.
It was discovered—irrefutably—that my grandfather had called in.
He was in uniform. He was on duty. He was still CHP.

The lie crumbled.

And so, quietly, the pension was restored.

But by then, it was too late.

The state funeral was gone.
The public notice never came.
There was no correction to the media.
There was no announcement that a CHP officer had fallen.

My grandmother, in her grief and shock, accepted the pension—and with it, the silence.

🤐 A Silence by Design

Why was this man erased?

He was no ordinary officer.
He served Governor Warren, who later became Chief Justice of the United States and chair of the Warren Commission investigating President John F. Kennedy’s assassination.

We now have reason to believe that my grandfather—still active, still connected—may have witnessed or overheard things during that investigation that others wanted kept buried.

In the mid-1960s, many people connected to that cover-up died.
Some quietly. Some mysteriously.
Many, like my grandfather, were erased with bureaucratic precision.

🧱 Institutional Guilt

Let us be clear:

The California Highway Patrol failed their own.

The State of California conspired with silence.

The system knew the truth, admitted it in private—but never corrected the lie in public.

They stole his name.
They stole his honors.
They stole his place in history.

They betrayed the widow.
They betrayed the family.
They betrayed the badge.

🛡️ My Name Is Matthew Chenoweth Wright

I am his grandson.

I now hold:

His photographs

His wallet

His badge number—a rare three-digit ID in the 100s

His story

And I will not let it be buried.

I am coming to Sacramento.
To CHP Headquarters.
To the Archives.
To the Museum.
To anyone who will listen.

We are going to reinsert Sergeant Harold “Lou” Wright into the public record.
We are going to demand the honors he was denied.
We are going to correct the silence they manufactured.

This is not vengeance.
It is remembrance.

And it is justice.

🕯️ To My Grandfather

They erased you because they were afraid of what you might have seen.

But they didn’t erase me.

And I see everything.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
On behalf of Florence June Wright nee Chenoweth, and the family
California, 2025
