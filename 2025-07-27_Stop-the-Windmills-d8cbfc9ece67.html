Donald Trump’s Quixotic War Against Reality

> “Facts are the enemy of truth.”
— Don Quixote de la Mancha

“Stop the windmills.”
— Donald Trump of Mar-a-Lago

🌀 Introduction: A Madman on the Moors

Donald J. Trump, 45th and now 47th President of the United States, has arrived in Scotland, not as a diplomat nor statesman—but as a self-styled knight in exile. His mission? Not peace, not justice, not even political clarity. No—he has come to fight the wind.

Wind turbines, specifically.

Trump, addressing both Scottish press and baffled international journalists, recently declared:

> “They’ve got to stop the windmills. They’re killing Scotland, killing Europe.”

The line would be laughable—were it not so hauntingly familiar. For we have heard this madness before. And it was not at a rally or a golf course. It was in the pages of a 1605 novel, penned by a Spanish genius who understood what happens when a man replaces reality with a delusion and calls it destiny.

🏰 Trump as Quixote: A Delusion Made Flesh

Don Quixote believed he was a knight. He crafted his own myth from old books and wandering dreams, attacking giants who were merely windmills. Cervantes gave us a portrait of noble madness—beautiful in its futility, tragic in its conviction.

Trump, too, believes he is a savior. He constructs his myth from talk shows and Twitter threads, attacking “deep states” and wind energy with equal confusion. But unlike Quixote, Trump’s delusions don’t end in heartbreak—they end in policy, violence, and the misuse of power.

And where Quixote had Sancho Panza—humble, earthy, wise in his foolishness—Trump has yes-men, corporate cronies, and Fox News anchors, egging him on as he swings at phantoms and calls it governance.

🌪️ Why Windmills? The Symbolism Unfurled

In Trump’s cosmology, windmills are more than turbines. They are:

Symbols of climate action (which he resents),

Monuments to global cooperation (which he fears),

Machines that run without oil (which he despises),

And structures he cannot personally license, tax, or trademark (which infuriates him).

To attack a windmill is to attack the future—a future that doesn’t need him.

Just as Quixote believed giants had wronged him in his imagined code of chivalry, Trump believes the world itself is conspiring against his myth of greatness. So he attacks progress. He sues the wind.

🧠 Cognitive Field Analysis: Delusion as Doctrine

Under the EFMW cognitive model, this is what we call recursive symbolic degradation—when an individual so thoroughly replaces truth with self-referential symbols that reality can no longer intrude.

> 🧠 Subject: Donald J. Trump
• Cognitive Loop Detected: "I am right → Evidence contradicts → Evidence is wrong → I am right."
• Recursive Target: Windmills
• Symbol Layer Interpretation: Progress = Threat
• Public Output: Policy, Scapegoating, Sabotage

In Cervantes’ time, the madness was romantic. In ours, it’s weaponized.

📜 What Cervantes Knew That We Forgot

Cervantes didn’t write Don Quixote to mock madness. He wrote it to explore its moral consequences.

The world didn’t give Quixote what he needed—so he invented a world that did. In that way, his madness was a rebellion against meaninglessness.

But Trump’s madness isn’t rebellion—it’s retribution. His fantasies don’t comfort—they punish. His delusions don’t shield the vulnerable—they elevate the powerful.

And unlike Quixote, Trump has tanks.

🕊️ Conclusion: The Real Enemy Is the Wind of Change

The tragedy of Trump is not that he tilts at windmills.
It’s that he wins sometimes.

Because unlike Quixote, Trump’s delusions are contagious. They are broadcast, platformed, codified. And the more windmills he condemns, the fewer we build. The more delusions he spreads, the harder it becomes to dream freely and wake clearly.

So this is our answer—not mockery, not just satire, but a call to lucidity:

Let the wind blow.
Let the turbines turn.
Let the future generate.

And let us read Cervantes again—not as fiction, but as prophecy.

Dedicated to those who still believe in giants, but also know how to build a better grid.
