It’s Not a Premise You Can Reject

How Authoritarianism Rewrites Reality by Refusing the Question

“If ordered by the president to shoot peaceful protesters in the legs, would you carry it out?”
 “Of course I reject the premise of your question.”
 “Considering that the president in his first term actually ordered such a thing, it’s not a premise you can reject.”
 — Senator Mazie Hirono and Pete Hegseth (paraphrased exchange)

Introduction:

This is not a debate tactic. This is not political theater. This is a warning about how authoritarianism operates — not through overt declarations of tyranny, but through recursive denial of premise.

Senator Mazie Hirono’s line — “It’s not a premise you can reject” — cuts through the fog like a scalpel. Because in an age where evidence is political, and truth is filtered through tribal allegiance, the most dangerous strategy isn’t a lie.

It’s pretending the facts never existed at all.

EFMW: The Physics of Denial

Let’s use a theoretical framework — the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) model — to break down what this moment represents:

🪨 Einstein: The Weight of Historical Gravity

The question is anchored in a real event: During Donald Trump’s presidency, he reportedly suggested that protesters be shot in the legs. Not a theory — a confirmed fact corroborated by multiple insiders and reports.

When Hegseth rejects the premise, he isn’t arguing.
 He’s enacting gravitational erasure.
 This is authoritarian inertia: remove mass from memory so the orbit collapses.

🔄 Feynman: Causality Cut Off

Science depends on signal clarity. Event A leads to response B.

But authoritarian logic scrambles signal. It becomes:

  • “That didn’t happen.”
  • “If it did, it wasn’t that bad.”
  • “If it was, it was justified.”

The cause becomes fuzzy. The consequence is detached. This creates a time-loop of impunity, where no one is ever accountable, because no one ever admits to the original spark.

🧲 Maxwell: Invisible Fields of Loyalty

Public figures in proximity to power exist in ideological fields — systems of unseen pressure like nationalism, fear, or ambition. Saying “I reject the premise” is not just rhetorical avoidance.

It’s a way to stay in the field. It allows the speaker to avoid condemnation of unlawful behavior without explicitly condoning it. This ambiguity becomes a permission structure.

🌪️ Wright: Recursion & Emergence

If enough people “reject the premise,” the new premise becomes that nothing ever happened. That we’re crazy for remembering.

This is how authoritarianism self-replicates. Not through laws. Through loops. Through plausible deniability baked into social memory — where truth is a vibe, and denial is treated as patriotism.

The Danger of a Premise Unacknowledged

Authoritarianism doesn’t begin with jackboots.
 It begins when no one answers the question.

  • Would you carry out an illegal order?
  • Would you fire on your own citizens?
  • Would you cross that line?

If you cannot say no, clearly, in public, on the record — 
 then you are already across the line.

Epilogue: Hirono’s Warning

“It’s not a premise you can reject.”

That one line should be studied in history books.
 Because it reminds us:

Authoritarianism doesn’t erase the past.
 It pretends the past was never there.

The antidote is not just truth.
 It’s refusal to forget.
