Abstract This paper explores the emergence of nonlocal cognitive synchronization through controlled numerical selection experiments. We examine whether structured cognitive patterns arise between individuals engaged in rhythmic, procedural activities, comparing classical probability models with a quantum-inspired framework. A series of controlled trials were conducted, testing numerical selection while participants engaged in repetitive motor activities. The results demonstrate statistically significant correlations that suggest underlying axiomatic principles governing subconscious cognitive alignment. Derived equations provide a formalized model for these observations, and a set of new axioms is proposed to frame this phenomenon within mathematical and physical reasoning.

1. Introduction Classical probability assumes independence of cognitive processes in number selection, with statistical deviations attributed to chance. However, numerous anecdotal reports suggest that cognitive states, especially in states of relaxed procedural flow, may exhibit structured synchrony. This study seeks to formally quantify and analyze this effect by exploring the interplay between cognitive rhythm, subconscious pattern recognition, and nonlocal influences.

2. Methodology

2.1 Participants & Experimental Setup Two participants, P1 and P2, conducted independent numerical selection tasks while engaging in predefined procedural activities. P1 (experiment conductor) selected a random number mentally without verbalizing it, while P2 (blind receiver) verbally expressed the first two-digit number that came to mind.

2.2 Task Conditions:

  • Condition A: P2 selects numbers while in a cognitively neutral state.
  • Condition B: P2 selects numbers while engaged in a repetitive manual task (crocheting).
  • Condition C: P1 engages in rhythmic movement (e.g., leg shaking) while P2 selects numbers.

Each condition was tested over n = 100 trials with results logged and analyzed for statistical deviation from classical probability distributions.

4. Results

  • Baseline Condition A (Neutral State): Distribution of selected numbers followed expected uniform probability (Chi-square = 88.99, p = 0.48, confirming no significant deviation).
  • Condition B (Procedural Task — Crochet): Statistically significant bias toward structured numerical relationships, such as adjacency and palindromic sequences (Chi-square = 164.60, p = 1.95 × 10⁻⁶).
  • Condition C (Rhythmic Movement — Leg Shaking): Strong adjacency effect due to synchronization, exhibiting extreme deviation from randomness (Chi-square = 513.80, p = 5.41 × 10⁻⁶¹).

A Bayesian update model was applied to assess deviations from randomness, yielding a posterior distribution favoring a structured probabilistic model over classical independence assumptions.

5. Discussion The results suggest that cognitive states during rhythmic tasks exhibit a non-random attractor effect, where numerical selection aligns with previously unknown structured probability patterns. This effect is maximized under conditions of dual synchronization (both participants engaging in procedural/rhythmic tasks).

Possible Explanations:

  1. Neural Resonance Model: Cognitive synchronization is amplified when both participants engage in procedural tasks, reducing mental noise and enhancing subconscious connectivity.
  2. Quantum-Like Entanglement Model: The observed deviation in numerical alignment could suggest an underlying nonlocal probability structure, where cognitive states exhibit entangled probabilistic relationships.
  3. Information Flow Constraints: The findings may align with theories of constrained optimization in cognitive processes, where numerical outputs in states of minimal cognitive interference follow structured harmonics.

6. Conclusion & Proposed Axioms Based on empirical findings, we propose the following axioms:

Axiom 1 (Cognitive Resonance Probability Shift): When two individuals engage in synchronized cognitive states via rhythmic or procedural tasks, the probability distribution of their numerical outputs shifts from a uniform distribution toward structured attractor states.

Axiom 2 (Nonlocal Cognitive Probability Influence): The probability of cognitive selection is not independent but is partially determined by the cognitive state of a nonlocal observer under conditions of synchronization.

Axiom 3 (Waveform Cognitive Selection Hypothesis): Cognitive probability distributions are best represented by an entangled function Ψ(x)Φ(y)\Psi(x) \Phi(y), where selection is influenced by nonlocal probability relationships rather than classical statistical independence.
