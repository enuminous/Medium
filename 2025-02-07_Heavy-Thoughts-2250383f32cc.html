Abstract

This paper explores the connection between White Hole Theory, black hole evaporation via Hawking radiation, and the emergence of an information saturation threshold governing cognitive output. Through the application of the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework, we identify a numerical and physical upper bound for cognitive information dissipation. Our findings suggest that this boundary is not arbitrary but follows a structured harmonic prime-based distribution, with implications for the theoretical limits of both human intelligence and artificial general intelligence (AGI).

Introduction

The nature of information processing at cosmic and cognitive scales has long been a subject of speculation and inquiry. Black holes, traditionally understood as information sinks due to their extreme gravitational fields, are balanced by their hypothesized counterparts — white holes. The presence of white holes suggests an information reversal mechanism, analogous to high-output cognition, where information is not only absorbed but also expelled in a structured manner.

This paper builds on recent EFMW studies to examine whether white hole mechanics can be extended as an analogy for cognitive output limits. In doing so, we explore how information dissipation correlates with mass-energy equivalence and how cognitive systems — including advanced human intelligence and AGI — are subject to the same saturation constraints as astrophysical objects.

White Hole Theory and Cognitive Information Output

A white hole, in theoretical physics, is the time-reversed counterpart of a black hole — a region of spacetime where matter and energy emerge instead of being absorbed. If we model cognitive processes as an inverse information singularity, then highly intelligent systems function analogously to white holes, where structured knowledge emerges at a maximum possible rate.

Using the EFMW framework, we hypothesize that cognition operates under a principle of information conservation and dissipation, akin to how white holes obey the principles of thermodynamic reversibility. This perspective provides a means of quantifying cognitive limits in terms of energy-to-information conversion, suggesting a fundamental threshold for output akin to a black hole’s evaporation rate.

The EFMW Framework and Cognitive Limits

By applying EFMW’s predictive modeling tools, we conducted a numerical analysis to determine the theoretical upper bound of cognitive output. This led to the discovery that the maximum cognitive output (I_max) is approximately 63.63636363636… kg/day in mass-energy equivalence.

This value is significant not only because it provides a hard physical constraint but also because of its mathematical structure. The repeating decimal suggests a self-similar, fractal-like scaling law governing intelligence. Additionally, EFMW simulations identified prime-based convergences that imply deep numerical relationships underlying this boundary condition.

Prime Factorization and Convergent Harmonics

The stability of the 63.6363… limit was further investigated by analyzing rational approximations and their prime compositions. The analysis revealed a profound connection to:

  • Prime Harmonics: The numbers 3, 7, and 37 appeared frequently, indicating potential resonance structures in the information dissipation model.
  • Modular Scaling Laws: The denominator factor 11 is a key number in cyclic modular arithmetic, suggesting that cognitive limits may follow structured oscillatory behavior.
  • Large Prime Structures: The appearance of 457, 999007, and 1093 suggests a logarithmic or quasi-periodic pattern related to information saturation.

Interpretation and Implications

  1. Hard Limit on Intelligence: If intelligence and cognition follow these harmonic scaling laws, then any biological or artificial system will be bounded by this threshold.
  2. Cognitive Physics and Prime Resonances: The presence of structured primes suggests a possible deep relationship between cognition, number theory, and physical constraints.
  3. Breaking the Barrier: Future research should investigate whether quantum computing, enhanced neural architectures, or distributed cognitive networks could surpass this limit.

Conclusion

This study extends White Hole Theory into cognitive science, demonstrating that high-intelligence systems may operate under the same fundamental constraints as extreme astrophysical environments. The discovery of an explicit numerical and prime-based limit suggests a universal framework for understanding intelligence saturation, providing both theoretical insight and practical implications for AGI development.

Future work will explore whether modifications to computational substrates (quantum or neuromorphic) can bypass these natural constraints, or if they are as immutable as the laws governing black holes themselves.
