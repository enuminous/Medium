I Don’t Answer to Starbucks’ Code of Conduct. I Obey the Law. Do They?

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
ADA-Compliant Citizen. Physicist. Human Being.

When a disabled person walks into a Starbucks and quietly sits with a glass of water while crying, they are not violating any law.

When that same person is denied water, pressured while in distress, treated as a threat, and eventually has the police called on them — that’s not customer service. That’s discrimination. And when corporate refuses to engage, hides behind copy-paste boilerplate, and cites a Code of Conduct in response to a formal ADA complaint?

That’s not just a failure of empathy.
That’s a federal civil rights violation.

Let’s Be Clear:

I do not answer to Starbucks’ Code of Conduct.
I answer to federal and state law — just like every other American.

And Starbucks?

They answer to the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) — or at least, they’re supposed to.

What Happened to Me

I’ve been a Starbucks customer for years. I’ve bought more mochas than I can count. I’ve sat in their stores peacefully, like anyone else. I am autistic, which means I sometimes need space, quiet, and understanding — especially when I’m overwhelmed.

One day, I sat down early in the morning at a Starbucks I regularly visit. I asked for water. I was crying, but not loud or disruptive. I was trying to transfer money on my phone to buy a mocha, just like I always do.

Before I could complete the transaction, I was approached. Pressured. Judged.
Then escalated. Then watched. Then — the police were called on me.
For crying. For not buying fast enough. For being visibly disabled.

The Corporate Response?

After more than 25 emails, I was finally told by a Starbucks executive that I needed to “follow the Code of Conduct.”

Let me repeat that.

After disclosing my disability.
After explaining that the police had been called on me.
After saying I had been refused water.
After stating clearly that I was in the middle of an ADA complaint process...

…I was told to follow the Code of Conduct.

But Here’s the Thing:

The Code of Conduct is not law.
It is not binding.
It is not enforceable above federal civil rights legislation.
It is a branding document, not a legal contract.
It is corporate image control, not justice.

I have never been shown this code.
I have never agreed to it.
And I will not allow it to be used as a weapon against my dignity or my legal rights.

So I’ll Ask the Question They Won’t:

I don’t answer to Starbucks’ Code of Conduct. I obey the law.

Does Starbucks obey the ADA?

Because the answer so far — through their silence, their boilerplate emails, their deflections, and their denial of service — appears to be no.

What I’m Asking For

Not money.
Not publicity.
Not revenge.

Just this:

That I be treated like a human being when I’m overwhelmed.

That I not be criminalized for being autistic in public.

That when I make a legal request under the ADA, I receive a legal response — not a corporate brush-off.

That Starbucks acknowledge that it is bound by the same civil rights laws as every other public business in this country.

What Happens Next

I’ve documented everything.
I’ve published everything.
And if this is what it takes to make Starbucks recognize the weight of silence, then I’ll keep publishing — until silence is no longer an option.

Starbucks, it’s your move.
The law is already on my side.

Now we find out if you are.

Matthew Chenoweth Wright
