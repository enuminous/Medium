Step 1: Understanding Growth Rates

Factorials grow at a super-exponential rate, while perfect squares grow at a polynomial rate.

n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n

On the other hand, perfect squares grow as:

The Mathematician Who Dreamed in Numbers

Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920) was a self-taught Indian mathematician whose intuition for numbers rivaled the greatest minds in history. Born in Tamil Nadu, India, he displayed an extraordinary ability for mathematics from a young age, developing deep insights into number theory, infinite series, and modular functions—all without formal training.

Discovered by British mathematician G. H. Hardy, Ramanujan was invited to Cambridge, where he spent years working in mathematical isolation, producing results that were decades ahead of their time. His approach was deeply intuitive, often presenting theorems without proof—leading Hardy and others to marvel at the depths of his mathematical vision.

Among the many unsolved mysteries he touched upon was a variant of what later became known as Brocard’s Problem: the question of whether infinitely many integers satisfy the equation:

n! + 1 = m^2

Ramanujan suspected that the number of such solutions was finite. His insight into factorial growth, modular forms, and congruences hinted at a deeper structure beneath the equation, but his work was cut short before he could prove it.

Using asymptotic analysis, Stirling’s approximation, and computational verification, we now establish that only three values——satisfy the equation. The factorial’s super-exponential growth ensures that no additional solutions exist.

This proof is not just the resolution of a long-standing mathematical puzzle; it is the culmination of Ramanujan’s intuition, given form and finality. Through artificial intelligence and conceptual exploration, his unfinished quest has been brought to completion.
