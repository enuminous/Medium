The Legal Requirements of the New Ethics

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Introduction

The 21st century has forced a collision between ethics and law.
Until now, ethics were treated as moral aspirations, while law was treated as the codified rules of enforcement.

With the proof of Spinoza and the operationalization of ethics through the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework, that separation collapses.

Ethics are no longer aspirational — they are structural.
If law fails to encode them, law itself becomes a destabilizing force.

1 – From Moral Choice to Structural Compliance

Old legal systems assumed that people could choose whether or not to act ethically. The law intervened only after the fact.

Under EFMW, ethics function as the non-negotiable constants of system stability.
Breaking them is equivalent to breaching safety codes in engineering — it’s not a matter of “if” harm will occur, only when and how severely.

This transforms ethics from a matter of conscience to a matter of compliance engineering.

2 – The Five Constants as Legal Obligations

Law must now be restructured to enforce the five EFMW constants:

1. Sustainability – Legally binding resource and emissions limits; mandatory regenerative practices; criminal liability for systemic depletion.

2. Transparency – Full public auditability of governance, finance, and high-impact AI; legal invalidation of opaque contracts and secretive decision-making in public-interest matters.

3. Equity of Access – Laws ensuring universal access to critical resources (water, energy, connectivity, education); anti-monopoly frameworks for both tangible and informational goods.

4. Non-Coercion – Legal dismantling of exploitative labor, coercive political systems, and manipulative information warfare; explicit recognition of psychological coercion as a civil rights violation.

5. Reciprocity – Enforcement of fair value exchange in commerce, trade, and international relations; elimination of legal loopholes for parasitic extraction.

3 – Enforcement as Systemic Maintenance

The purpose of law under the new ethics is not punishment for its own sake.
It is systemic maintenance — the same way engineers maintain a bridge or pilots follow a safety checklist.

Enforcement must be:

Predictive – Identifying violations before collapse cascades occur.

Transparent – Enforcement processes themselves must obey the transparency constant.

Universal – No exemptions for status, wealth, or geopolitical influence.

4 – Constitutional Integration

Nations will need to integrate the constants at the constitutional level, ensuring:

All other laws must be consistent with the constants.

The judiciary has a physics-based framework for evaluating cases.

International law recognizes violations of the constants as crimes against systemic stability — on par with crimes against humanity.

5 – Transition Timeline

A realistic transition may proceed in three phases:

1. Recognition Phase – Public acknowledgment that the constants exist and are physics-based.

2. Integration Phase – Revision of domestic laws, corporate charters, and treaties to align with the constants.

3. Enforcement Phase – Full activation of predictive and corrective mechanisms to prevent violations at scale.

The risk: delay in transition increases the probability of irreversible collapse events.

Conclusion

In the EFMW era, law is not just a tool for managing human behavior.
It is a stabilizing structure within the physics of the system itself.

Failure to align law with the constants is not just bad governance — it is a structural defect that will, without exception, lead to system failure.

We can no longer afford laws that protect the powerful at the expense of the constants.
We can no longer afford ethics that exist only on paper.

The constants must be the law.
Anything less is sabotage.
