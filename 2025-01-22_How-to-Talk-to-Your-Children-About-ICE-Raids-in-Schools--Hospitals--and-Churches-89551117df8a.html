How to Talk to Your Children About ICE Raids in Schools, Hospitals, and Churches

Introduction: Facing a Difficult Reality

As parents, one of the hardest challenges is explaining to our children that sometimes the world isn’t fair. When policies directly affect their friends, neighbors, or community, these conversations become even more urgent. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) raids in schools, hospitals, and churches — places once considered safe havens — represent a profound and troubling shift in American life.

Under President Donald Trump’s administration, policies permitting such raids have been reinstated and expanded, sparking fear and uncertainty in immigrant communities. Talking to children about these realities requires honesty, compassion, and a focus on resilience.

What’s Happening and Why It Matters

In 2025, President Trump resumed office and followed through on earlier statements from 2019 to expand ICE operations into “sensitive locations.” Historically, schools, hospitals, and churches were designated as off-limits to immigration enforcement unless extraordinary circumstances justified action. This change has allowed ICE agents to conduct raids in these places without requiring higher-level approval.

This policy targets undocumented immigrants, whom the administration labels “illegal.” For children, the consequences of these actions are deeply personal — they may witness classmates, neighbors, or even their own family members being taken away. This creates an atmosphere of fear and confusion, raising questions about safety, fairness, and justice.

Explaining to Children: Age-Appropriate Approaches

Conversations about ICE raids must be tailored to a child’s developmental stage, balancing honesty with emotional sensitivity.

1. Young Children (Ages 5–8): Reassurance Through Simplicity

  • Use simple language: “Some families don’t have the right papers to live here, and some rules let people come and take them away, even from schools or churches. That’s not how things are supposed to be.”
  • Reassure: “Your teachers and I are here to keep you safe, and we’re always looking out for you.”

2. Older Children (Ages 9–12): Balancing Facts and Empathy

  • Share basic facts: “The President has decided to let workers take people away from places like schools or hospitals if they don’t have the right documents. Many people think this is unfair, and it’s making a lot of families scared.”
  • Encourage empathy: “Imagine how hard it must feel for someone to lose their family or leave their school. How do you think we can support kids who are going through that?”

3. Teens (Ages 13+): Encouraging Critical Thinking and Advocacy

  • Provide context: “This policy is part of the government’s way of enforcing immigration laws, but it’s upsetting because it’s happening in places that are supposed to be safe for everyone, like schools and churches.”
  • Empower action: “You can make a difference by speaking up, supporting organizations that help immigrant families, or learning more about why these policies exist and how to change them.”

Teaching Empathy and Moral Clarity

These conversations offer an opportunity to teach children the values of fairness, kindness, and advocacy. Here are some ways to reinforce these lessons:

  • Encourage Questions: Create a safe space for children to ask about what’s happening, why it’s happening, and what can be done.
  • Model Compassionate Behavior: Show children how to be supportive friends and allies to those affected. This could include standing up for classmates or being part of community efforts to help.
  • Share Stories: Use books, films, or real-life examples to help children understand the experiences of immigrant families.

Providing Context: This Is Not Normal

Children should understand that what is happening now is not how things are supposed to work. Explain that:

  1. Checks and Balances Are Meant to Protect People: “In America, we have a system that’s supposed to stop unfair decisions, but sometimes it takes time for that system to work.”
  2. Safe Spaces Are Being Violated: “Schools, hospitals, and churches have always been places where people didn’t have to be scared. Changing that is unusual and upsetting for many people.”
  3. Change Is Possible: “When people see something wrong, they can work together to make it better. That’s how things have changed in the past, and it can happen again.”

Empowering Children to Respond

Children often feel powerless in the face of distressing news. Help them see that they can make a difference:

  • Small Acts of Kindness: Encourage them to be kind and inclusive toward friends or classmates who may feel scared or alone.
  • Community Engagement: Participate in activities that support immigrant families, such as food drives, fundraisers, or rallies.
  • Advocacy: For teens, encourage writing letters to elected officials, joining youth-led organizations, or educating others about the issue.

Addressing Fear and Anxiety

Children may feel scared or uncertain about these policies. Reassure them by emphasizing:

  • Their Own Safety: “Your school and teachers are here to protect you, and we’ll always make sure you’re safe.”
  • Community Support: “Many people, including teachers, doctors, and religious leaders, are working hard to stand up for families and make things better.”

Conclusion: Building a More Just Future

Talking to children about ICE raids is not just about explaining what’s happening — it’s about helping them navigate a world where fairness and justice are not always guaranteed. By addressing these issues with honesty, empathy, and a focus on action, parents can equip their children with the tools to understand, care, and make a difference. In doing so, we plant the seeds for a more compassionate and equitable future.
