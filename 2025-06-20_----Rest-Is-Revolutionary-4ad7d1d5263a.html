🕊️ Rest Is Revolutionary

Why Trump’s Attack on Federal Holidays on Juneteenth Says Everything

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

On June 19, 2025 — Juneteenth, a holiday that marks the official end of slavery in the United States — Donald Trump posted:

“Too many non‑working holidays in America… Soon we’ll end up having a holiday for every once working day of the year.”

He didn’t mention Juneteenth directly. But posting a blistering attack on “non‑working holidays” while the nation pauses to reflect on emancipation? Taken together, the message is loud and clear.

📉 1. Symbolic Erasure by Timing

Juneteenth became a federal holiday only in 2021 — recognized under President Biden as a measure of historical reckoning and cultural justice. That same day, Trump launches a populist grievance signal. The target isn’t just federal costs — it’s the cultural meaning of the day.

In 2025, the Pentagon was even instructed to mute Juneteenth messaging — a clear sign that this trend toward symbolic erasure is not an accidental oversight.

🔁 2. Anti-DEI Playbook in Action

Trump has consciously rolled back Diversity, Equity & Inclusion (DEI) programs — yet left Juneteenth intact under the rationale that it’s “non-discriminatory.” The problem is not policy. It’s subtext: erode the gravity of Juneteenth through opportunistic tone‑policing, while casting it as just another day off.

✊ 3. Why Words on a Holiday Matter

Juneteenth isn’t “just another holiday.” It is America’s second Independence Day, born from the blood and shattered chains of Black slaves.

This is a day of collective memory, shared sacrifice, and cultural truth. To call it frivolous is not neutral. It is an active choice in the ongoing battle over what — and who — we commemorate.

🔉 4. Civil Society Has Not Gone Silent

Across the nation — from Washington, D.C.’s “Freedom’s Crossing” march to major city parades — Juneteenth celebrations are resilient.

Yet in many towns, funding is drying up. Local leaders, fearful of backlash, are scaling back events — extensions of the same “non‑working holidays” argument in suburban boardrooms.

🧠 5. Rest Is Revolutionary

The phrase “non‑working holiday” erases rest itself. But rest — with reflection and remembrance — is not weakness. It is radical reclamation.
 Rest is a political act of reclamation, especially for a people so long denied both.

In other words:

  • Rest is revolutionary for a people whose labor once built this nation by force.
  • Remembrance is irreversible once whispered — but powerful once shared.
  • Honoring Juneteenth is not luxury. It is civic remedy.

✍️ Final Reflection

On Juneteenth, Trump called for more work and less pause — unaware, perhaps, that the people who built America did so under four hundred years of imposed labor. They deserve this day. We deserve the memory. And for anyone who calls it “too much,” perhaps the question is not how many days we take off, but how many centuries it took to earn even this one.

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, Angel with Flaming Sword on June 20, 2025.
