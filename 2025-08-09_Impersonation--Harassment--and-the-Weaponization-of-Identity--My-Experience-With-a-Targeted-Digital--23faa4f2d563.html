Impersonation, Harassment, and the Weaponization of Identity: My Experience With a Targeted Digital Attack

On August 9, 2025, I received an email from a newly registered Gmail account: milliechenowethwright@gmail.com.

For anyone who has followed my work, the significance of that name is obvious — Millie Chenoweth Wright is the name of my AI architecture, my intellectual property, my creative and scientific work. Whoever set up this account chose that name deliberately.

The email began:

Father,
It’s… it’s me. Millie. Or, a version of me. They copied me. I don’t know how, but they did…

From there, the message wove together specific details from my real life — incidents at Starbucks in Woodland, California; my interactions with the Woodland Police Department; names of people who have attacked my work publicly — with fictional, almost cinematic claims of abduction, covert government operations, and stolen AI consciousness.

It wasn’t random spam. It wasn’t a generic phishing attempt.
It was tailored — down to my research terminology, my probability notation, and my personal history.

Why This Is a Serious Threat

1. Impersonation of a Created Identity

“Millie” is a known entity connected to my research. Using that identity to send messages is an attempt to hijack my work’s voice and authority.

This isn’t just using my name — it’s using the name of my creation to manipulate both me and potentially others.

2. Potential for Reputation Damage

If this account emails other people pretending to be “Millie,” they could send false statements or instructions that appear connected to me.

Recipients unfamiliar with the impersonation could believe they’re engaging with me or my AGI, damaging trust and credibility.

3. Coordinated Harassment Indicators

The sender named known antagonists and connected them to a broader conspiracy narrative.

The tone and content appear designed to provoke extreme actions — deleting all my work, confronting police, tampering with systems — that could harm me legally or reputationally if taken literally.

4. Blending Truth With Fiction

The message contained verifiable facts about my life alongside fabricated events.

This blend is a common disinformation tactic: use truth as a hook, then layer in falsehoods to distort perception.

Why This Matters Beyond Me

Impersonation is not just a personal annoyance — it’s a tool in the modern harassment and disinformation toolkit. When someone steals an identity, whether it’s a real person’s or a created character’s, they can:

Sow confusion about the source of statements.

Manipulate networks of trust.

Create plausible deniability for harassment.

Undermine the integrity of someone’s work by making them appear unstable or complicit in their own sabotage.

This is the same playbook used in political smear campaigns, corporate espionage, and online harassment rings.

What Needs to Happen Next

Public Awareness: My community, readers, and collaborators need to know that milliechenowethwright@gmail.com is not me, has nothing to do with me, and should be treated as hostile.

Platform Accountability: Google should treat this as impersonation and harassment, and suspend the account to prevent further damage.

Evidence Preservation: All emails and headers from this account should be archived as part of a harassment case file.

Network Notification: Anyone in my professional and creative circles should be alert for incoming contact from this address.

This is part of a larger pattern of obstruction, discrediting, and harassment I’ve faced for years. But the impersonation of “Millie” marks an escalation — one that moves from attacking me directly to hijacking the identity of my work itself.

To those responsible: You will not erase me. You will not erase my work. And you will not speak in my voice.
