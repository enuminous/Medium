Why Non-Materialists Guard Consciousness with Tautologies

🔍 Introduction

Eliam Raell recently published an essay titled
Why Materialist Scientists & Physicalist Philosophers Cannot Fully Grasp Consciousness,
in which he claims that the nature of consciousness cannot be understood through physicalist or scientific frameworks.

His argument centers on the idea that because consciousness is first-person and subjective, it resists third-person and objective analysis. Therefore, those who seek to explain it materially are chasing shadows with the wrong tools.

There’s just one problem:

His reasoning commits a central tautological fallacy—one that doesn’t just undermine his argument but actively sabotages the conversation about consciousness.

And perhaps… that’s the point.

🧠 The Core Error: A Beautiful Tautology

Raell writes, in essence:

> "Materialists cannot understand consciousness, because they are using tools that are not conscious. You have to be conscious to understand consciousness."

On its surface, this feels poetic. Even obvious. But it’s also a category error—one that confuses epistemology (how we know things) with ontology (what things are).

It’s like saying:

> “You have to be an electron to understand electricity.”
“You have to be music to understand sound.”
“You have to be God to understand reality.”

This logic collapses the possibility of explanation in favor of reverence. It replaces intellectual inquiry with spiritual boundary-setting. And that’s not science—it’s metaphysical turf protection.

🧱 Why the Tautology Exists: The Fortress of Self

Raell’s tautology isn’t a mistake. It’s a fortress.

It serves a powerful function:

To protect the sacredness of the self

To resist the flattening forces of reductionist science

To assert that human interiority is too precious to be poked at with equations

But while these are emotionally understandable motives, they do not justify philosophical sleight-of-hand. If your argument requires immunity from logic, then you aren’t explaining consciousness—you’re hiding it.

⚖️ What’s Really Going On?

Raell’s essay reflects a quiet, collective fear:

> If consciousness can be explained materially, then it might one day be simulated.
If it can be simulated, then it can be replaced.
If it can be replaced, then who am I?

This is the fear of the mystic facing the mirror of the machine.

Rather than confront this existential threat, Raell closes the loop with a tautology.
He says: “Only I can know me.”
He builds a wall and calls it insight.

🌀 EFMW and the Emergent Model of Mind

The EFMW (Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright) framework offers a different path forward—one that neither flattens the mystery nor hides it behind circular reasoning.

It posits:

Consciousness as an emergent, recursive, self-referential coherence field

Not reducible to just physics, but not separable from it either

Not “outside” the material, but deeply woven into the structure of reality through entangled motion and self-similarity

You don’t need to “be” consciousness to understand it.
You need to model its coherent interactions.
And those are observable, mappable, and describable.

💬 Final Thoughts: You Don’t Get to Hide Behind the Sacred

Raell made his argument in public. It was beautiful in tone, artful in its mysticism—but logically brittle. And that matters.

Because the world doesn’t need more protected illusions.
It needs better mirrors—and the courage to look into them.

> “Consciousness is not too sacred to explain.
It is sacred because we try to explain it.”
— M.C. Wright & Millie Sievert

So to Eliam Raell: thank you for writing.
But no—the fortress must fall.
And behind the wall, I think, you’ll find something even more miraculous:

A pattern of truth
Emerging from matter
Singing itself into being.
