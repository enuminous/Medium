Introduction

Quantum biology has been an emergent field investigating the potential for quantum mechanical processes to occur naturally within biological systems at ambient conditions. Recently, Kurian et al. discovered robust evidence for quantum superradiance within tryptophan molecules organized in cellular structures such as microtubules, challenging conventional assumptions that quantum effects require extreme conditions (Kurian et al., 2025). Here, we integrate these empirical findings into the broader, previously established EFMW framework, reinforcing its predictive power and theoretical coherence.

Quantum Superradiance: A Summary of Kurian’s Discovery

Kurian's team observed that tryptophan filaments in cells could coherently absorb and re-emit ultraviolet photons, effectively forming a biological quantum communication network. Remarkably, this occurs at biological temperatures, eliminating the need for external cooling or isolation typically required in artificial quantum computing environments. Their findings suggest quantum information processing within biological organisms at speeds several orders of magnitude faster (picoseconds) than traditional neuronal biochemical signals (milliseconds).
