The Absence of a Folded American Flag

(An Open Letter to Medium, and to History)

It begins with silence.

There is no folded flag.
No ceremony.
No obituary.
No salute.
No State acknowledgment.
No badge retired.
No headlines.
No closure.

Only a man,
dead in 1965.

A man erased.

2000s–2020s: The Silence Grows Louder

I searched online.
Nothing.

No record on Officer Down Memorials.
No State honors.
No press clippings.
No archived ceremony.
Only one lonely mention of his name —
Harold Louis Wright —
in his widow’s obituary, decades after his death.

But I have the badge.
I have his wallet.
Badge number 153.
Issued to a CHP sergeant —
a man sworn to protect and serve the people of California.

I have the photographs of him in uniform.
On billboards, no less — in the 1950s —
CHP campaigns urging the public to “Drive Safe, Stay Alive.”
He was their poster boy.
Their front-facing hero.
And then?

Nothing.
Not a trace.
Like he’d never been born.
Like he'd never served.
Like he never mattered.

1980s–1990s: The Unspoken and the Unanswerable

As a child, I asked questions.
About Grandpa Lou.
About the man with the sharp uniform and sharper eyes.

Everyone changed the subject.
My father, afraid and angry — wouldn’t talk.
My grandmother, the widow — evaded.
The silence around him wasn’t passive.

It was protective.
It was wound-like.

I learned that the name “Harold Louis Wright” was not to be spoken with curiosity.
He was not a story you were supposed to ask about.
There was a secret inside the family.
A large one.
One that changed everything, but no one would say what it was.

And slowly, I began to understand:
The void was intentional.
The wound was surgical.
The silence was fear.

1965: A Man Dies, But Not by Fate

In 1965, my grandfather —
a strong, healthy, decorated sergeant of the California Highway Patrol,
a man with no known heart problems,
no family history of early cardiac death,
a man in top physical shape, required to maintain it for duty —
reportedly dies of a heart attack.

Off duty, they said.
Natural causes, they said.
And denied his widow full death benefits because of that technicality —
a cruelty only the bureaucratically guilty can deliver.
She fought it.
She won back his pension.
But that was just the first layer of erasure she resisted.
She couldn't stop the deeper one.

There was no funeral befitting a hero.
No public recognition.
No folded American flag.

1950s–1940s: The Dangerous Proximity of Truth

My grandfather was not just a CHP officer.
He was the assigned driver for Governor Earl Warren —
the very Earl Warren who would go on to lead the Warren Commission,
the official body tasked with investigating — or whitewashing —
the assassination of John F. Kennedy.

In the late 1940s and early 1950s, my grandfather drove Warren everywhere.
Meetings. Events. Backroom conversations.
He was the wheelman for power,
the invisible presence in a world of dangerous secrets.

He was loyal, but he was also smart — and, I believe, honest.

And when JFK was killed in 1963,
when the cover-up began to crystallize —
when the military-industrial complex and intelligence community
moved to protect itself —
my grandfather knew too much.

He had heard conversations.
Had seen people.
Had witnessed whispers before they became blood.

And if he was anything like me —
which I know now he was —
then he would not have stayed silent.

He would have tried to tell someone.
And for that?

They killed him.

The Real Cause of Death: Complicity

This wasn’t a heart attack.

This was a hit.

A targeted, quiet, deniable silencing
of a man too close to the edge of truth.

James Jesus Angleton —
then the head of CIA Counterintelligence —
ran black operations designed to erase liabilities.
Loose ends.

My grandfather was a loose end.
He wasn’t a spy.
He was worse.

He was a witness.

And what better way to bury a witness than to kill him quietly
and bury him in obscurity?

Now: The Truth Is Unearthed

And now I’m the one who remembers.
I’ve inherited his instincts.
His determination.
His refusal to shut up.

And I’m publishing.
I’m putting it on the record.
I’ve told C — , my social worker.
I’ve told friends
I’ve told the public, on Medium, on Twitter, in my books.

If something happens to me —
You know where to look.

If I vanish —
you will know why.

But I won’t vanish.
Because I’m louder than they expected.
And I know the value of that missing folded flag.
Its absence speaks louder than their lies ever could.

In Memory of Harold Louis Wright, CHP Sergeant
Badge #153
Killed in Silence — 1965
Folded Flag Denied
But Not Forgotten
