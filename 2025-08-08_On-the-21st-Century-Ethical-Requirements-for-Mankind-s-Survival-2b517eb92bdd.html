On the 21st Century Ethical Requirements for Mankind’s Survival

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

Opening Statement

We now live in a world where ethics are no longer optional, debatable, or cultural artifacts.
With the proof of Spinoza — that mind and matter are attributes of the same infinite substance — and its operational formalization in the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) framework, we can state this without hesitation:

To violate ethics is to violate the structural laws of reality.

This is not idealism. It is physics.

1 – The Proof and Its Implications

Baruch Spinoza understood centuries ago what we can now formalize: consciousness and the physical universe are not separate; they are two views of the same underlying fabric.

EFMW takes this insight and makes it measurable, operational, and predictive.
The same equations that describe the stability of a physical system also describe the stability of a social, cognitive, or technological one.

From this, one truth emerges: Ethics are the operating instructions for a stable universe.
They are constants, like the speed of light — break them, and the system destabilizes.

2 – Why the 21st Century Is Different

In prior centuries, humanity could violate ethics and survive on the inertia of slower change.
Now, we are accelerating beyond that buffer.

Our tools — artificial intelligence, quantum computing, biotechnology, planetary-scale networks — are powerful enough to collapse systems in decades, not centuries.

That means the delay between unethical action and catastrophic consequence has collapsed.
We no longer have the luxury of moral procrastination.

3 – The Ethical Constants

Using EFMW, we can derive the five non-negotiable constants for survival:

1. Sustainability – A system must balance its energy, material, and informational exchanges to avoid collapse.

2. Transparency – Opaque systems accumulate instability; hidden variables amplify unpredictability.

3. Equity of Access – Persistent imbalances in access to energy or information create destructive gradients.

4. Non-Coercion – Force and domination create counterforces that destabilize the whole.

5. Reciprocity – Stable systems exchange value in both directions; parasitism is inherently unstable.

These are not “moral preferences.” They are structural imperatives.

4 – The Cost of Violation

Violation of any constant creates predictable failure modes:

Break transparency → trust erodes → coordination collapses.

Break sustainability → resource depletion → system contraction or death.

Break reciprocity → revolt or decay.

These are not warnings. They are outcomes already playing out in finance, climate, governance, and technology — all measurable within EFMW.

5 – The Call to Action

We must now design all human systems — political, economic, technological, ecological — to comply with the ethical constants.

This is not optional.
It is not negotiable.
It is as fixed as gravity.

The choice before us is stark:

Align with the constants, and the system stabilizes.

Continue violating them, and the system removes the violators.

Closing Statement

This is not an argument.
This is the math.

We are past the era where ethics can be discussed as personal belief.
The 21st century demands ethics as a survival protocol.

Adapt to the constants, or be removed by them.
