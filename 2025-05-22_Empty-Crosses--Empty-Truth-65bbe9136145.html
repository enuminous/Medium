Empty Crosses, Empty Truth

Trump’s Staged Lie of a Genocide That Never Happened

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, PhD Candidate

Washington, D.C. — May 20, 2025

In a moment that will be remembered less for its gravity than for its deliberate manipulation, former President Donald J. Trump stood beside South African President Thabo Mahlangu today at the White House and cited what he claimed was “proof of white genocide” in South Africa.

He gestured toward a video. The footage showed a desolate field littered with white wooden crosses — symbolic grave markers arrayed in solemn rows. To the uncritical eye, it looked like a war cemetery. To Trump, it was ammunition.

“This,” he said, “is what they won’t show you. This is genocide.”

But there’s one problem: there were no graves.
 There were no bodies.
 There was only a camera, a manufactured landscape, and a lie.

The Video: A Prop Without a Crime

Within hours of Trump’s statement, independent analysts, South African officials, and human rights organizations confirmed the truth: the footage he referenced does not show a burial site. It does not depict the aftermath of a massacre. It is not part of any formal investigation or humanitarian documentation.

The site, located outside Bloemfontein, has long been used by nationalist groups for symbolic displays. The crosses are empty markers, erected without corresponding deaths. There is no evidence that they represent victims of racial violence, let alone genocide.

In fact, local press documented the display as early as 2019, when far-right activists staged a “farm murder awareness” event using the same visual language. At the time, it was heavily criticized as performative and misleading.

What Trump presented to the world stage as a “hidden atrocity” was, in fact, a known political stunt — recycled to serve a new lie.

A Familiar Tactic: When the Accused Becomes the Inventor

This moment cannot be isolated from its context.

Just days before this White House meeting, I published an article formally accusing Donald Trump of modern genocide — through structural neglect, engineered division, stochastic incitement, and the deliberate creation of societal conditions under which vulnerable populations are subjected to erasure, death, and cultural collapse.

Trump did not respond to the accusation directly.

Instead, he turned outward — into a mirror world — and presented himself not as the perpetrator of genocide, but as the witness to one.

This is not accidental. It is projection, a documented authoritarian tactic. When faced with the language of guilt, Trump reframes the accusation into a new atrocity — one safely distant, racially charged, and morally inverted. He displaces the moral center, so that he can stand atop the rubble of his own fabrication and declare himself righteous.

The Myth of White Genocide in South Africa

The “white genocide” narrative has long been a staple of alt-right messaging circles. It falsely claims that white South Africans — particularly Afrikaner farmers — are being systematically murdered and replaced. This has been repeatedly debunked by:

  • The South African Human Rights Commission
  • Amnesty International
  • United Nations Special Rapporteurs
  • Local law enforcement data

Violent crime in South Africa is a national problem that affects black South Africans in far greater numbers. There is no evidence of targeted racial extermination of white people. None.

And yet, the myth persists — because it serves a function. It gives white nationalist movements a martyr complex. It gives authoritarians like Trump a foreign crisis to invent and then exploit.

A Manufactured Spectacle

There is something deeply sinister in the visual of it all.

A former U.S. president, standing on the South Lawn, invoking empty crosses as if they were evidence. Not honoring the dead, but manufacturing the illusion of death. Not acknowledging history, but rewriting it in real time.

It was not just a lie. It was a performance.

And like all performances, it had a script:

  • Distract from domestic accusations.
  • Invoke sympathetic victims.
  • Borrow the emotional weight of a genocide that never happened to discredit the one that is unfolding — in language, in law, and in systems.

The Real Danger

This is not about South Africa.

This is about how lies metastasize when televised. This is about the mechanics of propaganda in the 21st century — when atrocity can be fabricated out of wood, sky, and silence.

And it’s about what happens when the man most responsible for America’s moral erosion begins inventing foreign atrocities to cloak his own.

He is not warning us.
 He is rehearsing.

Today’s fake genocide may be tomorrow’s justification. For violence. For policy. For state-sanctioned repression.

Conclusion: A Call to Memory

There were no graves.
 Just crosses.
 There were no bodies.
 Just a camera and a lie.

But history will not be so easily rewritten.

We must remember that Donald Trump is not just misinformed — he is constructing semantic weapons. The footage was fake. The accusation was false. But the intent was clear:

To make genocide speak someone else’s name.
