The Sex Trade in America

Why We Permit It — Or Do We?

Introduction: A Century Apart, the Same Transaction

In 1891, a man named Friedrich Trump — grandfather of the 45th President — left Germany and made his fortune running hotels and restaurants in the Pacific Northwest. These establishments were widely reported to have included prostitution as part of their draw.
More than a century later, the United States is still looking at headlines about sex for sale — only now the brothels have URLs, private jets, and offshore accounts.

From the Backpage.com takedown in 2018 to the collapse of Jeffrey Epstein’s network in 2019, the scale and sophistication have changed, but the transaction hasn’t. Women’s bodies — and in some cases, girls’ bodies — are still traded openly in systems that intersect with politics, business, and culture.

Backpage: The Digital Marketplace

Backpage didn’t invent the online sex trade — it industrialized it.
By the time federal prosecutors seized its servers, the site’s “adult services” section had become a hub for both consensual sex work and trafficking victims, with ads posted in dozens of U.S. cities every day.
The removal of Backpage’s listings didn’t end the trade. It fractured it into encrypted apps, invite-only groups, and new marketplaces with the same buyers and sellers.

Epstein: The Private Network

Jeffrey Epstein’s operation was not a street-level marketplace; it was a curated network for the wealthy and connected. It involved recruiters, transporters, financiers, and cover entities — from Southern Trust Co. in the U.S. Virgin Islands to private aviation firms and island compounds.
The collapse of his network didn’t end the demand. The infrastructure — financial, logistical, and social — is still visible in corporate registries, settlement records, and unexplained renewals of shell companies.

Hollywood, High Society, and the Cultural Cover

This isn’t confined to the “underworld.”

High-profile “Hollywood madam” cases showed how elite client lists overlap with politics, entertainment, and corporate leadership.

The pornography industry, legal but largely unregulated, blurs the line between autonomy and exploitation, creating a cultural backdrop where the commodification of women’s bodies is normalized.

Both spheres feed the same underlying narrative: that access to women’s bodies is a resource to be purchased, whether in cash, influence, or career advancement.

The Intersectional Reality

The sex trade in America is not a monolith. It operates on multiple levels:

Street-level and survival sex work — often criminalized without addressing poverty or coercion.

High-end escorting and elite brokering — shielded by wealth, secrecy, and connections.

Cultural commodification — in film, media, and online content that conditions public attitudes toward sexual access.

Across all levels, women’s bodies are treated as assets — to be bought, sold, traded, or leveraged — with systems in place to protect the buyers more than the sellers.

Why We Permit It — Or Do We?

America has never truly dismantled the sex trade. It has rebranded it, digitized it, and in some cases, legitimized it. Law enforcement cracks down in bursts — a seized website here, a high-profile arrest there — but the market adapts and continues.

The harder question is not whether we permit it, but why. Is it because:

Men with political, economic, and cultural power have always been among its top clients?

It’s easier to target the sellers than to disrupt the buyers?

We are unwilling to confront the overlap between the “respectable” economy and the exploitation economy?

Conclusion: The Comfort of Looking Away

From Friedrich Trump’s brothel to Backpage to Epstein’s island, the geography changes but the dynamic doesn’t. Women’s bodies are still traded — in public, in private, in plain sight.

The only question is whether we keep pretending these are separate stories, or whether we acknowledge them for what they are: chapters in the same book we have yet to close.
