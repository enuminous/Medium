When a Bright Young Man in a Green Apron Calls You by Name

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

I’m 57 years old. Disabled. I’ve lived enough life to know when a conversation is just a conversation — and when it’s a performance.

Today, I sat outside a Starbucks. Not inside. Not in the way. Just me, the air, my breath, and a little space I thought I could claim while I recovered from a panic attack. I’d been riding my bike when it hit — hard enough that I had to stop.

I sat there quietly, trying to unwind.

Then a tall, fresh-faced young man in a green apron stepped out the door. He looked straight at me and asked:

“Are you Matthew?”

That’s Not How Policy Starts

Policy doesn’t know my name.
Policy says “sir” or “ma’am” or “excuse me.”

But he knew my name.
Which means this wasn’t about policy — at least not in the casual, every-customer sense.

This was about me.

A Calculated Calm

He was intelligent. You could see it in his eyes. Not flustered, not fumbling for words. He knew exactly what he was there to do.

And I knew exactly what was coming.

I tried to get ahead of him. I tried to work with him, to de-escalate before we even started. I told him plainly: I have a disability. I’m a federally protected whistleblower. I have an active ADA Title III case against Starbucks. What you are about to do will be considered retaliation by the Department of Justice.

He didn’t even blink.

The Shift

His body changed. Arms crossed. Shoulders tightened. His weight shifted into a stance that said, I’m not backing down.

He told me I had to move along because of “policy.”
The same “policy” that apparently requires the barista to know your name before applying it.

Losing Ground

I could feel the tears starting before I could stop them. The panic rising again. I told him: I don’t want to be put in this position. I can’t help this. I don’t want to feel like this.

He kept going.

That’s the thing about these moments — when someone chooses to keep going after you’ve told them you’re in distress, it’s no longer about the rule they’re quoting. It’s about control.

The View From 57

When you’re Heinz years old and disabled, you’ve seen enough to know when you’re being singled out. You’ve seen enough to recognize when someone is playing their part in a script they didn’t write, but still chose to read.

You’ve seen enough to understand that sometimes the harm isn’t just in the words, but in the fact that the person speaking them is smart enough to know exactly what they’re doing.

That bright young man in the green apron didn’t need to know my whole history. He didn’t need to have met me before.
He only needed to know my name — and whatever was said to him before he walked out that door.

And that was enough for him to look me in the eye and tell me to move along.

This was his choice. It was his Manager’s choice to tell him my name. At least he didn’t call me Allison. but this was not anything other than what it completely appears to be: Illegal retaliation against a protected Federal whistleblower who was having a panic attack, and needed support and assistance, not dismissal. Certainly, accommodating me by allowing me to sit and breathe on their property for a few minutes is not outside the preview of the Americans with Disabilities Act. As an autistic human being, I have the right to emotionally regulate myself by any allowable means necessary at the time for my own personal safety. Asking for water, and space. That’s all I did. Why are the police, and now today, a security guard, being involved?
