Locked Out of Starbucks

Discrimination at 4:30 AM

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

It’s 4:30 in the morning. Starbucks’ posted opening time. Their website says they’re open. Their hours on the door say the same. I’m standing outside. The door is locked.

Inside, I can see employees moving, machines running. Outside, I’m in plain view, patiently waiting. And while I stand here — in the early-morning quiet of a small American town — the drive-thru is buzzing. Three, four, five cars glide through. Lattes are served. Orders fulfilled. Steam from espresso machines rises like everything is normal.

But I’m not being served.

I’m being excluded.

This Isn’t Just Coffee. This Is a Civil Rights Violation.

At 4:36 AM, I’ve already counted multiple vehicles being served while I, a pedestrian, am refused access.

At 4:39 AM, an employee finally cracks open the door to tell me, “We’re opening 10 minutes late. Sorry, Matthew.”

Yes, by name.
Yes, they know me.
Yes, I’ve filed multiple complaints.
Yes, I’ve explained my disability.
And yes, I told her: “You’re serving cars. I’m on foot. I don’t have a car. I would like a coffee.”

She closed the door in my face.

A Pattern of Disrespect, Disruption, and Denial

This isn’t a one-off. This location has:

Opened late without notice

Closed early with vague signs

Locked customers out of the lobby citing “short staffing” — while maintaining full drive-thru service

Ignored previous complaints filed through official channels

They claim operational excuses. What they’re practicing is selective access. And when you do that to someone with a known disability, who’s also on foot, who has already been mistreated before — that becomes discrimination.

Starbucks: A Public Accommodation That Won’t Accommodate

Let’s be clear: This is a place of public business.
Not a gated coffee fortress for cars.

When a Starbucks location serves vehicles but refuses to serve a person standing at the front door, it’s not just inconvenient — it’s exclusionary.
It violates the spirit — and possibly the letter — of the Americans with Disabilities Act.

When I raise this with the employee? Dismissal. A shrug. A door slammed shut.

They’re not just late — they’re weaponizing access.

A Brand That Sells Inclusion While Practicing the Opposite

Starbucks loves to sell itself as progressive, inclusive, community-focused.
They sponsor Pride campaigns.
They tweet about social justice.
They platform themselves as ethical.

But here I am — outside their locked door.
Watching steaming cups of coffee pass hand to hand in a drive-thru.
While I — an autistic, pedestrian, early-rising customer — am refused service. Again.

I’m Not Asking for a Free Drink. I’m Asking for Dignity.

It should not be a political act to stand outside a business and expect service at the time they say they’re open.

But apparently, being on foot — and disabled — makes me less worthy of coffee in the eyes of Starbucks. Or at least of this particular store. Or perhaps just in the eyes of a few corporate managers who’d rather this quietly go away.

I’m Not Going Away.

You’ve had your chance, Starbucks. I gave you months.
I sent the reports.
I told you the truth.

And now, here I sit — still outside your locked door — telling the world.

Let this article be the next complaint, the next witness statement, the next record.

Because I’m done being polite.
And I’m done pretending that being locked out is anything less than systemic, silent exclusion.

The drive-thru is open.
The lobby is not.
The machines are humming.
And I’m still sitting here — waiting for basic human decency in a cup.

Filed: July 2, 2025 — From the sidewalk outside Starbucks, watching another car get coffee while I am denied it.
