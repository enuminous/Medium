🛑 Breaking the Conveyor Belt

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

There’s something quietly suffocating happening in our public spaces — a kind of invisible machinery humming underneath everything. You feel it when you walk into a store, or a hospital, or a school. It’s in the fluorescent lights, the time limits, the “code of conduct” signs taped to windows. It’s in the tone of voice that says, “Can I help you?” — but means, “Why are you here?”

We have mechanized our civilization into something cold, tight, and smooth. Something that no longer resembles a culture, but a conveyor belt.

And when you don’t move the way they want you to, when you step even slightly out of rhythm — you are corrected.

---

⚙️ Conveyor Belt Formalism

That’s what I’m calling it.
This quiet violence.
This systemized rhythm of behavior compliance, behavioral judgment, and behavioral erasure.

It tells you: “You may sit here… but only if you’ve purchased the proper beverage.”

It tells you: “You may use this space… but only if you perform usefulness.”

It tells you: “You may exist… but only if you disappear emotionally.”

It rewards speed, efficiency, numbness, politeness, and purchase.
It punishes grief, stillness, confusion, disability, need.

---

📍 My Moment Off the Belt

One morning, I sat in a Starbucks with a cup of water. I was grieving. I was trying to think. I hadn’t bought coffee yet, because I needed to check my email, figure out how to move money around, and maybe stabilize myself enough to feel like I could be a customer. Not a bother. Not a weight. Just… a person.

And instead of receiving space or grace,
the police were called.

Because I didn’t move fast enough.
Because I didn’t purchase quickly enough.
Because I didn’t “snap into line” on the conveyor belt.

That morning, I wasn’t seen as a human being —
I was seen as a clog in the machine.

---

🧠 They Don’t Understand Time

What struck me most was how they spoke to me — as if my identity was frozen in a single moment.

> “You’ve been told about the water before.”

“You can’t just sit here.”

“You’re causing a disturbance.”

But they don’t understand time as I do.
As a disabled person. As a grieving man. As a neurodivergent human navigating emotional waves.
I exist across time, in episodes, in recursions, not in single policy violations.

And I don’t owe an explanation for why I sat down with a cup of water instead of a coffee.
Because water is a right.
Because public spaces should be safe for everyone.
Because disability doesn’t punch in on a clock.

---

💥 The Oppression of Unspoken Rules

There’s a deeper violence in being told you’re breaking a rule you didn’t know existed.

Not a law.
Not a regulation.
But a vibe.

A floating standard. A feeling in the air.

> “You’re not doing this right.”
“You’re not welcome unless you blend in better.”

You feel it when they watch you from behind the counter.
When they whisper to each other about “that guy who’s been here too long.”
When they send out a manager.
When the cops are summoned like a reset button.

All for crying quietly.
All for being visible in grief.
All for stepping off the conveyor belt — even for a moment.

---

🔧 Breaking the Belt

What happens when we refuse?

When we say:

> “No, I won’t be polished into perfect behavior.”

“No, I won’t apologize for needing time.”

“No, I will not be erased to make your environment smoother.”

That’s when the belt shudders.
That’s when the system flinches.
That’s when something real starts to crack through the plastic.

---

✊ This Is Bigger Than Coffee

This is about how we treat each other in shared space.
This is about how corporations police feelings.
This is about how we criminalize slowness, softness, sadness — all the things that make us real.

I was thrown off the conveyor belt.
And I’m not getting back on.

Instead, I’m naming it.
Breaking it.
And writing for everyone who has ever felt judged just for being visible.

You don’t need to be perfect to be present.
You don’t need to purchase to deserve a seat.
And you don’t need to disappear to be allowed to breathe.

---

Let’s break the belt together.
Let’s rebuild public space with memory, mercy, and time.

— Matthew Chenoweth Wright
