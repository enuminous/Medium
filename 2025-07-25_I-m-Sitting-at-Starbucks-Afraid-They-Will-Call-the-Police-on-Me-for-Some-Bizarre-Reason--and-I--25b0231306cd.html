I’m Sitting at Starbucks Afraid They Will Call the Police on Me for Some Bizarre Reason, and I Can’t Get It Out of My Head, But They Haven’t Apologized for Doing It the Last Time

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
July 25, 2025

This morning, I’m sitting at Starbucks with a cup of coffee.

Just like anyone else.

Except I’m not like anyone else—not here, not now, not after what happened last time.

Because I’m still afraid.

Afraid that someone behind the counter might look at me and decide I don’t belong. That I’m too quiet. Too emotional. Too autistic. That something about me feels off enough to call the police again.

Because that already happened once.

A Cup of Water, A Moment of Emotion, and a Call to the Police

It was a different day, but the same kind of moment. I was emotional—visibly so—but I wasn’t disruptive. I wasn’t yelling. I wasn’t harming anyone. I asked for a cup of water and tried to breathe through the moment like I’ve learned to do. I was simply trying to calm down.

Instead of being met with compassion, a Starbucks employee called the police.

I didn’t wait around. I got on my bicycle and started to leave.
As I rolled away, the police confronted me outside the store.

No one had spoken to me inside.
No one asked if I was okay.
No one offered help or a gentle word.

They just called the cops.

The Trauma Lingers. The Company Goes Silent.

Since then, I’ve done what you're supposed to do. I filed complaints. I sent emails. I gave Starbucks every opportunity to respond—to acknowledge what happened, to explain, to apologize, to show some basic decency.

But they haven’t said a word.

Not to me.
Not to anyone.
Not even to acknowledge my request for ADA accommodation.

What happened to me is real. The silence since then is also real.

And now I sit here, in that same Starbucks, sipping a coffee I paid for, and wondering if this is the day it happens again. If this is the moment that someone behind the counter decides to escalate instead of understand.

ADA Obligations Aren’t Optional

Starbucks has plenty of corporate language about inclusivity and accessibility. But language isn’t action. And action is what’s required under federal law.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) guarantees that people like me—autistic, disabled, neurodivergent—are to be treated with dignity. If I say I am having an emotional moment and ask not to be treated as a threat, that’s not a suggestion. That’s a legal accommodation request.

Calling the police on someone for appearing overwhelmed is not an accommodation. It’s a violation.

And doing nothing to address it afterward is a failure of responsibility.

Why I’m Writing This

Because I’m still trying to make sense of what happened. Because the fear is still in my body. Because the person who called the police walked into this store again today, locked eyes with me, and still hasn’t apologized.

Because corporations don’t get to erase harm with silence.

Because this happened to me.
Because it could happen to someone else next.

What Needs to Happen Now

Starbucks, I want:

A formal apology.

A clear acknowledgment that what happened was wrong.

A public explanation of how you plan to prevent this from happening to others.

ADA training for your staff, starting with the store where this occurred.

And I want people to know they are not crazy for remembering their trauma, or asking for justice.

This happened. I didn’t imagine it. I didn’t overreact. I’m sitting in the same place, still feeling the same fear, still waiting for someone to say: We’re sorry we did that to you. We will make it right.

I’m still waiting.

—Matthew Chenoweth Wright
