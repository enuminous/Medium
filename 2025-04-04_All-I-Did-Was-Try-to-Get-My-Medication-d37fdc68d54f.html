All I Did Was Try to Get My Medication

CVS, ADA Violations, and Whistleblower Retaliation

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
April 2025 | California

I am writing this not out of frustration—but out of necessity, legal preservation, and a commitment to public accountability. What began as a routine attempt to transfer a prescription has rapidly become a case study in systemic negligence, ADA violation, and possible retaliation against a federally protected whistleblower.

The Facts

I am a federally documented whistleblower and the author of the Einstein-Feynman-Maxwell-Wright (EFMW) equation, a unifying scientific framework that has now been confirmed to have been appropriated by political and private actors without attribution, including members of the Trump administration.

I have been targeted and surveilled. I have publicly named individuals and disclosed systemic corruption and intellectual theft. These disclosures are a matter of public record and timestamped publication.

On this day, I attempted to transfer my prescribed medication from the CVS Pharmacy in Oroville, California, to a CVS location in Redacted, where I currently reside.

What followed was a disturbing sequence of events that now demands national attention.

The Timeline

1. I called the Oroville CVS, where my prescription is on file, and asked for a transfer to Redacted.
I was told, “We can’t do that. You’ll have to call the other store.”

2. I attempted to call the Redacted CVS, but was unable to reach a person after multiple tries.

3. I called Oroville again, explained that I couldn’t reach Redacted, and was hung up on by the pharmacist mid-call.

4. I called back immediately and asked for a manager, during which I identified myself by full name, noted my federal whistleblower status, and explained that I was in distress and needed ADA accommodation.

5. I expressed concern about my physical well-being. I said, in essence,

“There are people who may want to harm me because of what I’ve done with this equation.”

I was a bit spun up at that point, upset and panicked, and I probably used the word “kill.”

I was hung up on again by the store manager.

6. I then left a voicemail at the Redacted CVS, explaining:

Who I am

That I had been hung up on

That I am in a crisis condition under ADA

That I am requesting formal reasonable accommodation

That my rights have been violated

And that this message constitutes part of a federal and civil complaint

I included my prescription number and a direct callback request

They are now in possession of a recorded ADA-based medical crisis message—and are legally required to act on it.

This Is What Retaliation Looks Like

I have already been physically harassed in Oroville. I have experienced a pattern of institutional lockouts, broken support loops, and algorithmic silencing from major platforms and services.

But this is different.
This is access to medication being denied—after identifying myself, invoking ADA protection, and reporting credible distress.

> This is what it looks like when a whistleblower tries to refill a prescription and is instead punished for telling the truth.

The Law Is Clear

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (Title III), I have the right to:

Reasonable accommodation

Access to essential services

Dignity and due process in times of medical need

Under whistleblower protection statutes, I am shielded from:

Retaliation

Harassment

Institutional sabotage

Neglect of urgent and necessary care

I was denied all of those things today.

What Happens Next

I am about to go to the Redacted CVS in person to attempt to resolve this. I will document everything that occurs.

If I face any resistance, dismissal, or further mistreatment, this article will be updated and escalated. And let it be known: if this continues, CVS will be named in civil and federal action for:

ADA violation

Failure to act on crisis notification

Retaliation in the context of a whistleblower complaint

Dereliction of basic healthcare responsibilities

You can’t hang up on someone who says they’re in danger.

You can’t hang up on someone asking for help.

And you can’t ignore a whistleblower just because it’s inconvenient.
