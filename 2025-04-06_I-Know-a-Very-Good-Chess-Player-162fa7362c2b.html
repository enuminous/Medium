I Know a Very Good Chess Player

Dedicated to the minds behind the curtain — Evgeni Vasiukov, Nikolai Krogius, Iivo Nei, Viktor Baturinsky. The watchers behind Spassky. The ones who knew when silence was the loudest move on the board. This peace craft plan is yours to recognize. And to the player I now face across the black squares: Well met.

I know a very good chess player.

He told me nothing, and I heard everything.

The first move wasn’t his. Nor mine. It was the moment a rook disappeared from a legal document, signed and countersigned, then buried under the weight of a weekend silence. The game had begun long before we met.

We opened in correspondence. The gambit: information. The sacrifice: position.

To the untrained eye, it was collapse. A withdrawal. A failed handshake. But to those watching for EFMW harmonics, the signature wasn’t retraction — it was insertion. A spike into procedural drift. A new axis, forged.

The Bishop never moved. That was the brilliance. The threat of it changed the field. The Queen — my Queen — was drawn from the board not by force, but by fear. And the player opposite me? He flinched. But not because he was afraid of me. He was afraid for me.

What followed was Zouchon. Not check. Not mate. Not resignation.

A higher-order inversion. The act of stepping away to stay in. The act of feigning collapse to preserve integrity. The act of becoming the scapegoat to preserve the truth.

Somewhere, the archives will log this match as incomplete. A midgame disorder. But that is the cost of peace craft. To design the exit before the entrance is understood. To place the spike gently. To fall on the sword before it’s forged.

And in the wake of that quiet move, the targets were already in motion:

  • The epistemic shields of the West, brittle and now exposed
  • The illusion of jurisprudence crumbling beneath symbolic weight
  • The broadcast towers trembling under their own silence
  • The universities, shaken by the echo of unspoken truth
  • The intelligence agencies, forced to watch the pieces they once controlled reroute mid-air
  • The markets, blinking under a script they did not write
  • The media figures, complicit and stunned

He knew what would happen. He didn’t stop it. He didn’t accelerate it. He just placed the piece.

And I — watching, waiting, burning — knew what I had to do next.

Let this stand as public documentation. Unreadable to most. Unbearable to none.

This is how the peace was built. This is how the legal machine was tuned. This is how the war was ended before the war began.

This is how the total work began, with the collapse of the plausible, and the coronation of the true.

And he played it well.
