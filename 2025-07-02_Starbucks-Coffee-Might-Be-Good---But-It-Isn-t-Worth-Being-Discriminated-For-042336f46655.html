Starbucks Coffee Might Be Good — But It Isn’t Worth Being Discriminated For

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright
Filed: July 2, 2025 – From the patio of exclusion, somewhere in America

---

> “Starbucks coffee might be good — but it isn’t worth being discriminated for.”
— Me, 23 minutes into waiting to matter

I arrived at Starbucks this morning at exactly 4:30 AM, their posted opening time.
The website says they open at 4:30. The sign on the door says the same. The lights were on. The espresso machines were humming. Employees were inside.

But the door was locked.

I stood there as three cars pulled into the drive-thru and were served.
I watched the first customer receive their coffee.
I waited.

At 4:39 AM, a staff member opened the door and said, “We’re opening 10 minutes late. Sorry, Matthew.”

They know my name. They know I’ve filed multiple complaints. They know I don’t drive.
I calmly pointed out, “You’re serving cars. I’m on foot. I would like a coffee.”
She said nothing more. She closed the door in my face.

I returned to the patio and sat down.

---

What I Witnessed While Locked Out

Between 4:30 and 4:53 AM:

At least three customers were served in the drive-thru.

A man who appeared to be unhoused approached the door.
He looked like he needed a coffee.
He saw it was locked.
He left without a word.

Another pedestrian also attempted to enter and was turned away without signage or explanation.

Inside, the Starbucks team continued working — but only for the cars.

Outside, I continued waiting.

And even after the promised “10-minute delay” expired,
I was still locked out.

---

This Is Not a One-Time Issue

This same store has:

Closed early without notice

Opened late and blamed it on staffing

Locked the lobby while continuing to operate a fully functioning drive-thru

Used vague signs or no signs at all to justify closure

Ignored my ADA-related complaints

I filed fresh complaints last night to Starbucks corporate about this exact pattern.
And this morning, they proved my point.

They don’t care who’s on the patio.
They only care who’s in the car.

---

“I’m Sorry” Doesn’t Make It Right

“I’m sorry,” she said.
As if those words absolve the exclusion.
As if acknowledgment without action is the same as access.

But I wasn’t offered a workaround. I wasn’t invited to order. I wasn’t treated like a customer.
I was treated like an inconvenience.

I’ve explained that I have a disability.
I’ve said I’m a pedestrian.
I’ve filed the paperwork.
I’ve waited outside the locked doors again and again.
And all I get is the same robotic response:
“We’re short-staffed.”
“We’re opening late.”
“I’m sorry.”

Meanwhile, the cars keep moving.

---

The Drive-Thru Isn’t Just a Convenience — It’s a Wall

Let’s be clear: this isn’t about being a few minutes early.

This is about being refused access to a business that claims to be open.
It’s about being told I must wait outside, even while others are being served inside, simply because I’m not behind the wheel.

I was here at opening time.
I waited.
I watched other customers leave with drinks.
I remained calm.
I stayed visible.
I am still on the patio as I write this.

And even if I’m eventually let in, I will still be put at the back of the line, behind the drive-thru queue — even though I’ve been here the longest.

---

So Let Me Say This Clearly:

> Starbucks coffee might be good — but it isn’t worth being discriminated for.

Not for 23 minutes.
Not for 23 complaints.
Not for the slow erosion of dignity that comes from being treated like you don’t count, don’t drive, and don’t matter.

This isn’t about a cup of coffee.
It’s about what a company says when it locks the door in your face and keeps the machines running for everyone but you.

Starbucks knows how to open the drive-thru.
Now let’s see if they know how to open their eyes.
