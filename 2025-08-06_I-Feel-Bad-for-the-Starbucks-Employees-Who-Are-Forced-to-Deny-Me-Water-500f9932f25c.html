I Feel Bad for the Starbucks Employees Who Are Forced to Deny Me Water

They Know It’s Wrong. Yet They Don’t Want to Lose Their Jobs.

Yesterday, I walked into my local Starbucks and asked for a cup of water — something I’ve done a thousand times before.

The barista looked me in the eye and said, “It’s policy.”

Those words told me everything I needed to know.

It meant they’d been instructed — by someone higher up — to deny me water unless I made a purchase, even though they know I have an ADA Title III complaint pending with the Department of Justice.

It meant they were just following orders.

When “Policy” Becomes a Shield

History has taught us that just following orders is rarely a moral defense. It’s the phrase used when conscience is outsourced to authority.

On paper, the Starbucks directive is framed in neutral terms — “apply policy consistently,” “avoid exceptions” — but everyone involved knows the subtext: when I come in, deny him water, no matter what.

The words are bureaucratic. The effect is personal.

Good People Put in Bad Positions

This is not about mean baristas. The people at this store know me. They’ve chatted with me about my work in physics. They’ve laughed at my jokes. They know I’m autistic. They know water is a basic human need.

And I can see the hesitation in their faces when they say no. They don’t want to deny me. They just don’t want to lose their jobs.

(With the possible exception of this one employee — she seemed to relish smiling at me and suggesting that perhaps I could buy water.)

That’s the trap: the company issues the order, and the employees carry it out — even when it conflicts with their own sense of right and wrong.

The Barista Who Had to Call the Police on Me

In an earlier incident, a barista was told to call the police on me for sitting quietly in the store with a cup of water.

She didn’t decide that on her own. It came from a manager who was acting on guidance from higher up the chain. And by making that call without lawful justification, she was put in personal legal jeopardy.

The order came from above. She carried it out. Just following orders.

The Harm Inside the Store

Directives like this don’t just hurt the target — in this case, me. They harm the employees forced to execute them.

Psychologists call this “moral injury” — when people are required to act against their own ethics. It breeds resentment, fractures trust, and turns a workplace into a pressure cooker.

That’s the dirty little secret: Starbucks isn’t just creating an inhospitable environment for certain customers. They’re creating a hostile work environment for their own employees.

This Was Never My Fight

I didn’t start this conflict. I didn’t want to put baristas in an impossible position.

I just wanted a cup of water — something any decent human being would give without a second thought. Something the law says I’m entitled to as a reasonable accommodation.

But instead of fixing the problem, Starbucks doubled down. And now they’ve made their employees the enforcers of an indefensible policy.

Starbucks Could Change This Overnight

It’s simple:

Stop retaliating against customers who file complaints.

Honor ADA accommodation requests.

Don’t make your employees carry out orders that they know are wrong.

Until then, every time a barista says “It’s policy” to me, we both know what’s really being said.

They’re just following orders. And that’s never been a good excuse.
