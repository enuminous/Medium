A Practical and Political Guide to Clearing the Vines Before They Eat the Bones

🪓🌿 How to Remove Kudzu from Your Town—and from Your Town Administration

A Practical and Political Guide to Clearing the Vines Before They Eat the Bones

🌱 Introduction: Green Isn’t Always Good

Kudzu is called “the vine that ate the South.”
But the truth is: it didn’t eat it.
We fed it.

Imported with good intentions, planted by bureaucrats, praised by planners—it was meant to hold the soil together.
Instead, it held the future hostage.
It didn’t stop erosion. It stopped time.

And just like kudzu, corruption doesn’t start with fangs.
It starts with roots.
Then comes the silence.

This is a guide for how to pull it all out—leaf by leaf, contract by contract.

🪱 Part I: How to Remove Actual Kudzu from Your Town

> “They told us it was a gift. Now it grows over everything we love.”

🔍 1. Identify the Invasion

Kudzu grows fast, up to 60 feet per season.

It strangles trees, power lines, porches, schools.

Your town may already be budgeting to mow it back—which is like brushing your teeth while the jawbone is rotting.

🧯 2. Start at the Roots—Literally

Kudzu’s crown is where its power lives: a root mass that regenerates from even small fragments.

Digging it up is brutal, but burning it alone doesn’t work.

Use a combo of:

Controlled digging

Herbicide (like triclopyr or glyphosate), responsibly applied

Grazing goats (yes, goats—nature’s precision pruners)

📊 3. Mobilize a Local Task Force

Make kudzu removal a civic project:

Hold a “Kudzu Day” with local volunteers.

Turn it into an art event: photograph the damage, document the stories.

Apply for USDA eradication grants if applicable.

Plant fast-growing native species in its place so it doesn’t come back.

🏛️ Part II: How to Remove Kudzu from Your Town Administration

> “Vines don’t care about votes. They grow in silence while the people sleep.”

🧠 1. Learn to Recognize Institutional Kudzu

Administrative kudzu looks like:

Bloated budgets for useless tech

“No-bid contracts” with lifelong insiders

Code enforcement weaponized against the poor

Endless strategic plans with no community implementation

A city council that smiles while the neighborhood dies

Ask:
Who is gaining shade while the rest of us can’t see the sky?

🧹 2. Cut the Vines, Not Just the Leaves

Attend council meetings and ask direct questions on the record

FOIA everything: contracts, emails, budgets—especially around:

Infrastructure deals

Police equipment upgrades

Corporate partnerships

Organize town halls outside the “official” ones

Build alternative maps of decision-making influence: Who is really making the calls?

⚖️ 3. Use Institutional Grazers

Just like goats help clear plant kudzu, bring in systems that eat administrative overgrowth:

Local journalists

Citizen auditors

Whistleblowers with protection

Youth organizers who don’t owe anyone favors

Support them. Feed them. They’re the ones who chew through lies.

🔄 Part III: What Happens After You Clear the Vines?

> “When the sky comes back, so does the future.”

Removing kudzu isn’t glamorous.
You’ll get dirty. You’ll be resented.
Some people like the green silence.

But if you succeed—even in one park, one agency, one vote—you’ll notice something strange:

Light.

Ideas begin to photosynthesize again.
Children play where it was once too thick to move.
Truth begins to grow.

You didn’t just remove a plant.
You rewrote the narrative of your town.

🌿 Conclusion: Kudzu Was Never Just a Plant

Kudzu is the story we tell ourselves when we ignore small corruption until it becomes unstoppable.
It is the metaphor we live in.
And the metaphor we must escape from.

Your town doesn’t need more trimming.

It needs weeding.
