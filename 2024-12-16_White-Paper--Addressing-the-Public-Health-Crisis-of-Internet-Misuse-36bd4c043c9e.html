White Paper: Addressing the Public Health Crisis of Internet Misuse

Executive Summary

Since the advent of the internet, its misuse has contributed to significant harm, including societal polarization, public health crises, and deaths directly or indirectly linked to cyberattacks, disinformation campaigns, and the amplification of harmful content. For instance, the WannaCry ransomware attack in 2017 disrupted healthcare services globally, delaying critical treatments and likely contributing to preventable deaths. These effects have escalated in the last two decades, with estimates suggesting that over 500,000 lives have been lost globally due to internet-related misuse. This paper outlines the problem, identifies its root causes, and proposes actionable solutions to mitigate the harmful impacts of internet misuse while preserving its benefits.

The Problem

The internet has been a transformative tool, fostering communication, commerce, and knowledge-sharing. However, its potential for harm has been increasingly weaponized, resulting in widespread societal, economic, and personal damage. Key categories of harm include:

Cyberattacks on Critical Infrastructure:

  • Healthcare systems have been targeted with ransomware, delaying treatment and causing preventable deaths. For example, the 2020 ransomware attack on a German hospital caused the death of a patient when emergency services were diverted.
  • Power grids, transportation systems, and water supplies have been compromised, creating cascading risks for public safety. Notably, the 2015 cyberattack on Ukraine’s power grid left hundreds of thousands without electricity during winter months, highlighting the severe risks posed by such vulnerabilities.

Disinformation Campaigns:

  • Anti-vaccine propaganda contributed to thousands of preventable deaths during the COVID-19 pandemic, as documented in studies such as a 2021 report by the Center for Countering Digital Hate, which found that online misinformation was a key factor in vaccine hesitancy.
  • Hate speech and divisive narratives have incited violence, such as the Rohingya genocide in Myanmar and domestic unrest in the U.S.

Radicalization and Extremism:

  • Online platforms have been used to recruit and incite violence, leading to terrorist attacks and mass shootings.

Social and Mental Health Crises:

  • Cyberbullying, harassment, and addictive algorithms have exacerbated mental health issues, particularly among youth.

Scope of the Impact

Global Death Toll

  • Over 500,000 deaths globally are estimated to be attributable to internet misuse over the last two decades, stemming from disinformation, cyberattacks, civil unrest, and the amplification of extremist ideologies. This estimate includes categories such as preventable deaths linked to anti-vaccine misinformation, fatalities from cyberattacks on critical infrastructure like hospitals, violence incited by online hate speech, and deaths from extremist attacks fueled by radicalization through internet platforms.

Economic Cost

  • Cyberattacks cost the global economy an estimated $6 trillion annually, while misinformation contributes to economic inefficiencies, public mistrust, and healthcare system overloads.

Erosion of Trust

  • The weaponization of misinformation has undermined trust in institutions, including governments, healthcare systems, and democratic processes, further destabilizing societies.

Root Causes

Platform Business Models:

  • Social media and internet platforms profit from engagement-driven algorithms that amplify sensational, divisive, and harmful content.

Lack of Accountability:

  • Section 230 of the U.S. Communications Decency Act shields platforms from liability for user-generated content, creating a regulatory vacuum.

Weaponized Information:

  • State and non-state actors exploit the internet to conduct hybrid warfare, targeting populations with disinformation, propaganda, and cyberattacks.

Public Vulnerability:

  • Low digital literacy leaves the public susceptible to manipulation by malicious actors.

Proposed Solutions

1. Strengthen Accountability for Platforms

  • Amend Section 230 to introduce a “duty of care” standard for platforms, holding them accountable for knowingly amplifying harmful content. This standard would require platforms to actively identify and mitigate foreseeable risks associated with their algorithms and content moderation practices. For example, platforms would need to limit the reach of false or harmful content flagged by fact-checkers, prevent algorithmic amplification of extremist content, and regularly publish transparency reports detailing steps taken to reduce harm.
  • Require transparency in algorithm design and the publication of annual harm audits.

2. Regulate Algorithmic Amplification

  • Mandate that platforms deprioritize harmful or false content flagged by independent fact-checkers.
  • Implement independent oversight bodies to audit and regulate algorithmic impacts.

3. Expand Cybersecurity Protections

  • Increase funding for critical infrastructure cybersecurity, prioritizing healthcare, utilities, and transportation systems.
  • Strengthen federal coordination with state and local governments to defend against cyberattacks.

4. Combat Disinformation

  • Create a National Disinformation Response Center to track and counter disinformation campaigns.
  • Fund public education campaigns to improve digital literacy and resilience to manipulation.
  • Provide grants to independent fact-checking organizations and require platforms to prominently link to verified corrections.

5. Protect Vulnerable Populations

  • Enforce stricter age verification and content moderation standards for platforms accessed by minors.
  • Fund mental health resources and support services for victims of online harm, including harassment and cyberbullying.

6. Foster International Collaboration

  • Lead global efforts to establish norms for internet governance, including regulations on content moderation and cybersecurity standards.
  • Work with allies to counter transnational disinformation campaigns and cyberattacks.

7. Engage the Public as Stakeholders

  • Encourage grassroots movements and citizen participation in combating disinformation and monitoring harmful activity online.
  • Provide tools for communities to hold platforms and malicious actors accountable.

Conclusion

The internet is a double-edged sword: a powerful enabler of progress but also a vector for harm when misused. Addressing the public health crisis of internet misuse requires coordinated action from governments, corporations, civil society, and individuals. Congress must lead by implementing robust regulations, fostering accountability, and empowering the public to combat these harms. By treating internet misuse as a public health issue, we can save lives, rebuild trust, and ensure the internet remains a force for good.

Call to Action

We urge policymakers to prioritize this issue and act swiftly to address the misuse of the internet. The human cost is too great to ignore, and the time for meaningful change is now.
