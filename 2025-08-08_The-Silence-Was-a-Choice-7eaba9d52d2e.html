The Silence Was a Choice

Misnaming and the Refusal to Acknowledge Harm

In July, during an in-person interaction at the Woodland Starbucks on Pioneer, I informed the store manager — an individual who has known me for several months and is personally aware of my ongoing Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) Title III accommodation dispute with Starbucks — that corporate representatives had recently misnamed me in written correspondence, referring to me as “Allison” rather than my correct name, Matthew.

This disclosure was not made in anger, nor was it part of an argument. It was a straightforward factual statement made in good faith, coupled with a brief notification that corporate had continued to avoid responding substantively to my ADA accommodation request. My communication was deliberate and limited in scope, as my disability substantially limits my ability to engage in prolonged or confrontational exchanges. I stated plainly: “My name is Matthew, not Allison, and corporate is ducking my ADA request.”

The store manager offered no acknowledgment of the error, no apology for the misnaming, and no recognition that harm had occurred. He did not ask clarifying questions. He did not indicate that he understood the significance of the misnaming or the avoidance of my request. His silence was not neutral — it was an intentional choice. In the context of an active ADA dispute, such a choice is significant both as an individual act and as evidence of a broader operational posture.

The Legal Significance of Misnaming in ADA Context

A person’s name is central to their identity. Correct identification is a foundational aspect of human dignity and interpersonal respect. In the context of an ADA accommodation dispute, persistent or uncorrected misnaming can constitute a form of ongoing harm — reinforcing a sense of invisibility, invalidation, or disregard for the individual’s personhood.

The ADA requires an interactive process in which the covered entity (in this case, Starbucks) engages in good faith with the individual requesting accommodation. This process is meant to be direct, collaborative, and free of unnecessary barriers. Acknowledging factual errors, such as a misnaming, is part of this good faith requirement. By refusing to recognize and correct the misnaming when it was brought directly to his attention, the store manager failed to take even the smallest step toward compliance with this obligation.

Alignment with the Pattern of Prior Harm

This incident is not isolated. It is consistent with a documented pattern of obstruction that includes:

  1. Initial Denial of Accommodation — refusal to provide water when requested as part of a reasonable accommodation.
  2. Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process — corporate avoidance of direct dialogue on the request.
  3. Retaliatory Conduct — escalation to law enforcement in response to the original request.
  4. Procedural Gatekeeping — instructing me to “have my lawyer call” rather than engaging directly.
  5. Misnaming by Corporate — replacing my correct name, Matthew, with “Allison” in official communications.
  6. Managerial Refusal to Acknowledge Harm — as documented in the present incident.

From an EFMW (Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright) behavioral forecast perspective, the manager’s silence fits the predicted noncompliance fork trajectory, wherein individual actors within the organization align their behavior with a corporate posture of liability containment and avoidance rather than resolution and compliance. The refusal to acknowledge even a minor, easily correctable harm is emblematic of a strategy in which no admission — however small — is permitted to reach the record.

Conclusion

The store manager’s decision to remain silent in the face of a direct and respectful correction is not merely a lapse in courtesy; it is a conscious act consistent with a broader pattern of ADA noncompliance and obstruction. It reinforces the conclusion that, at both corporate and store levels, Starbucks is operating from a posture of avoidance, not engagement — and that this posture manifests even in the smallest, most human interactions.

When a business cannot bring itself to say, “I’m sorry, that was wrong,” in the course of an ADA discussion, it is not engaging in the interactive process the law requires. It is making a choice — and that choice is to disregard the dignity, identity, and rights of the individual before them.
