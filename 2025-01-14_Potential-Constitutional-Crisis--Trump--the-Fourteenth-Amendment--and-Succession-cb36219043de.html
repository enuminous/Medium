Potential Constitutional Crisis: Trump, the Fourteenth Amendment, and Succession

Abstract

This paper analyzes the unprecedented constitutional crisis arising from Donald Trump’s felony conviction and adjudication as an insurrectionist. Despite the Fourteenth Amendment barring insurrectionists from holding office, Trump remains the presumptive president-elect. This study evaluates the potential legal and governmental consequences if he is sworn in, the role of the Supreme Court, and possible pathways to resolve the crisis, including removal, succession, or electoral intervention.

1. Introduction

The United States faces an unprecedented situation: a president-elect with felony convictions, and a legal determination as an insurrectionist. This raises fundamental questions about constitutional enforcement, the integrity of the presidency, and the power of the Supreme Court to intervene. This paper explores the legal framework surrounding presidential eligibility and succession, along with the potential political fallout of a constitutional crisis.

2. Constitutional Requirements for the Presidency

According to Article II, Section 1 of the Constitution, a president must be:

  • A natural-born citizen
  • At least 35 years old
  • A U.S. resident for 14 years

However, the Fourteenth Amendment, Section 3, states that individuals who have engaged in insurrection are disqualified from holding office. Since a Colorado court has ruled Trump an insurrectionist, his eligibility is legally disputed.

3. The Role of the Supreme Court

If Trump is sworn in, the Supreme Court could be asked to determine his eligibility retroactively.

  • However, there is no precedent for nullifying a presidential inauguration.
  • A ruling against Trump could create a crisis where the judicial and executive branches are in direct conflict.

The Court could either:

  1. Affirm that Trump remains eligible despite the Fourteenth Amendment.
  2. Invalidate his presidency, leading to removal and succession issues.

4. Chain of Succession If Trump Is Disqualified

If Trump is disqualified before or after taking office, the Presidential Succession Act applies:

Trump is removed → Vice President assumes office

  • The elected VP would take over.

Congress refuses to certify Trump’s presidency → Speaker of the House takes over

  • If Congress deems Trump ineligible, the Speaker of the House (possibly a Republican) could assume the role of acting president.

Supreme Court rules Trump ineligible before January 20 → Biden stays in office temporarily

  • If the Court determines Trump cannot legally assume office, Biden could remain president under emergency powers.
  • This would be highly controversial and might require a special election.

Electoral College Reconsideration → New Election?

  • If the Court determines Trump should not have been on the ballot, it could invalidate Electoral College results.
  • Congress may select a new president or call a special election.

5. Potential Constitutional Crisis Scenarios

Scenario 1: Trump is sworn in, then removed

  • If Trump is sworn in and later ruled ineligible, this would create a constitutional vacuum.
  • His Vice President would take office, but legitimacy could be questioned.

Scenario 2: Congress blocks his presidency

  • If Congress refuses to certify Trump’s presidency, legal challenges could delay governance indefinitely.

Scenario 3: The military or executive branch refuses to recognize Trump

  • The Joint Chiefs of Staff and intelligence agencies could refuse to follow Trump’s orders.
  • This could lead to a government paralysis or a power struggle.

6. Precedents and Historical Comparisons

Although no U.S. president has faced this scenario, some historical cases provide insight:

Post-Civil War Reconstruction (1868)

  • The Fourteenth Amendment was used to bar former Confederates from holding office.
  • Legal challenges and pardons ultimately weakened enforcement.

The 2000 Election Crisis

  • The Supreme Court intervened to halt Florida’s recount, effectively deciding the election.
  • Judicial intervention set a precedent for resolving disputed presidential elections.

Watergate & Nixon’s Resignation (1974)

  • A president stepped down to avoid impeachment, setting a precedent for legitimacy crises.

7. Resolution Pathways

Pathway 1: Supreme Court ruling

  • The Court could rule on Trump’s eligibility either before or after inauguration.
  • A retroactive disqualification would create a legal and political crisis.

Pathway 2: Congressional action

  • Congress could impeach Trump immediately based on his disqualification under the Fourteenth Amendment.
  • However, a Republican-controlled House and Senate might refuse to act.

Pathway 3: Political compromise

  • Trump could step down voluntarily in exchange for legal protections or a pardon.
  • A negotiated transition of power might be the least disruptive option.

8. Conclusion

The legal and political ramifications of Trump’s presidency are profound.

  • If the Supreme Court does not intervene, the legitimacy of the presidency could be permanently weakened.
  • If the Court removes Trump, it could set a dangerous precedent for judicial overreach.

The resolution of this crisis will shape the future of American governance and constitutional law.
