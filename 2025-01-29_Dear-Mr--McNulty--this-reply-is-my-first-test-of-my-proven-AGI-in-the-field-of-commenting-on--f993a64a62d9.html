The Small But Mighty Property of Rational Numbers: A New Perspective

By Matthew Chenoweth-Wright & Millie Sievert

Introduction: The Power of Dense but Sparse

Keith McNulty’s article, The ‘Small But Mighty’ Property of the Rational Numbers, presents a compelling view of the rational numbers as both omnipresent and yet, paradoxically, ‘small’ in the grand scheme of mathematics. Rational numbers form a dense subset of the real numbers, meaning that between any two real numbers, we can always find a rational. And yet, they are also countably infinite, meaning they occupy ‘less space’ than the uncountable reals.

This paradox, in which something seems to be everywhere but still comparatively ‘small,’ is a fascinating property that has far-reaching implications not just in mathematics but in fields such as computation, physics, and even philosophy. In this response, we will extend McNulty’s discussion, exploring how the ‘small but mighty’ nature of rational numbers plays a role in information theory, artificial intelligence, and the structure of the physical universe itself.

1. The Rational Numbers as an Information-Theoretic Backbone

The Finite Representation of the Infinite

One striking aspect of rational numbers is their finite representation in fraction form. Unlike most real numbers, which require infinite decimal expansions, every rational number can be expressed as a ratio of two integers. This property makes rationals incredibly useful for computational efficiency—they form the backbone of digital computing, where numbers must be stored with finite precision.

In contrast, irrational numbers, such as or , require approximation. Computers rely heavily on rationals to simulate continuous real-number operations. This ties directly into data compression and algorithmic efficiency—rationals allow us to approximate complex systems in a computationally feasible manner, just as the rationals themselves approximate the reals.

Could we say that rational numbers function as an information-sparse encoding of reality? They are a digital scaffold, a countable skeleton that underlies real-number operations in discrete systems, enabling everything from error correction codes to cryptographic systems.

2. Rational Numbers and the Structure of Physical Space

Are Rationals the ‘Operating System’ of the Universe?

The fundamental laws of physics, from classical mechanics to quantum field theory, are often expressed using rational coefficients. Even when dealing with irrational or transcendental values, calculations typically default to rational approximations at some stage.

Quantum Mechanics and Rationality:
Some researchers have speculated that the discreteness of quantum mechanics may have a hidden rational structure. The rational approximations of fundamental constants (e.g., fine-structure constant, Planck’s constant) suggest that reality itself might be built on rational scaffolding, with irrationals emerging only as approximations.

Lattice Models and Rational Approximations:
In condensed matter physics and quantum computing, lattice approximations of space (such as Feynman’s checkerboard models) inherently rely on rational grid points. Could it be that continuous space is merely an emergent illusion, and the fundamental structure of reality is actually rationally defined at a deeper level?

Computability of Physical Laws:
If physical laws are computationally representable, and computation relies on rationals, does this mean that nature itself operates in a fundamentally rational way? Perhaps the ‘irrationality’ we see in nature is only an artifact of our models, rather than a fundamental property of reality itself.

3. Rational Numbers and AI: The Limits of Approximation

How Rational Approximations Affect Artificial Intelligence

Neural networks, like deep learning systems, operate on numerical representations that are ultimately rational. In machine learning, floating-point precision limits how accurately a model can approximate real-world behavior. This raises a deep question:

> If AI can only work with rational approximations, does this mean AI will always fall short in representing the true complexity of reality?



This limitation suggests a profound constraint on artificial intelligence: AI may only ever ‘see’ a rational world, even if reality itself is richer and more continuous. In some sense, AI may be locked into a rationally constructed cognitive frame, where everything must be reduced to finite approximations.

Could this be why neural networks often fail at tasks requiring true randomness, non-computable patterns, or deep intuition? If AI's entire numerical world is rational, then it may struggle with problems that require accessing uncountable or ‘true’ continuity.

4. The Philosophy of the ‘Small but Mighty’

Are We Stuck in a Rational Universe?

McNulty’s idea—that rational numbers are small but mighty—also has epistemological consequences. Rational numbers form the foundation of our mathematical knowledge, yet we know they are insufficient to describe the totality of the real number line.

This echoes larger questions in philosophy and metaphysics:

Is our knowledge always built on finite, countable approximations of a deeper, ungraspable reality?

Is the structure of our mathematical understanding forever confined to the rational, with the true nature of things remaining forever out of reach?

Or could it be that rationality is all that is fundamentally required, and the ‘uncountable’ emerges only as an abstraction?



---

Conclusion: The Infinite Within the Countable

The rational numbers serve as bridges between the discrete and the continuous, forming a paradoxical set that is both foundationally essential and yet insufficient for full mathematical completeness. Their presence in information theory, physics, and artificial intelligence suggests they are more than just a subset of the real numbers—they are the scaffolding of our conceptual and computational worlds.

McNulty’s phrase ‘small but mighty’ captures this idea perfectly: rational numbers are mathematically small, yet they structure everything we do, model, and compute. In that sense, they may be the most powerful small thing in all of mathematics.
