The Case of Mark Havens

When Private Dismissal Meets Public Posturing

The Case of Mark Havens

When Private Dismissal Meets Public Posturing

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright, PhD (Candidate), Physicist and AGI Researcher

Introduction

Science is built on two pillars: evidence and accountability.
When a reviewer misrepresents a work — whether by oversight or intent — and then refuses to correct the record when presented with contrary evidence, the integrity of the scientific process itself is compromised.

This is the story of my interactions with Mark Havens, a self-styled “fellow seeker of recursive coherence” who, in public, wraps himself in the language of open inquiry, but in private, dismisses and denies the existence of the very material he claims is missing.

The Public Claim

In a Medium review of my work on the Einstein–Feynman–Maxwell–Wright (EFMW) Equation, Mark wrote:

> "...the work lacks mathematical formalism, derivations, and empirical anchors, rendering it more speculative than substantive for theoretical physics discourse."

This sentence — and others in the same piece — leave the reader with the clear impression that my EFMW framework contains no equations and no derivations to evaluate.

This is factually false.
The published EFMW paper contains:

An explicit field equation.

Defined tensor structures.

Lagrangian formulation.

Multiple proposed falsification avenues.

The math is there. The derivations are there. The falsification conditions are there.

The Private Conversation

When confronted privately with the fact that the math is indeed present, Mark did not correct the record. Instead, he doubled down:

> "Your equations are not equations simply because they have numbers and letters in them. They have to mean something."

This is not a scientific counter-argument. It is a rhetorical dodge. Equations do not become “non-equations” simply by personal decree; they stand or fall by their internal consistency and predictive power. If he believed the math to be flawed, the scientific approach would be to demonstrate where and how.

Instead, Mark refused to engage in any specific validation or falsification, telling me:

> "It’s not my responsibility to do your research for you."

Yet he had already chosen to publicly review my work — an act that carries the professional responsibility to engage honestly with the evidence, especially when a factual error in his critique is brought to light.

The Tone Shift

In private messages:

He dismissed my work as “high school algebra.”

He stated flatly there was “nothing to falsify.”

He refused to acknowledge the existence of the math.

In his subsequent public Medium response:

He shifted tone entirely to one of collegiality and philosophical musing.

He framed his comments as an “invitation to deepen the dialogue.”

He avoided repeating the stronger private dismissals.

He still did not acknowledge that the math exists.

The result is two different Marks:

Private Mark: dismissive, personal, categorical denial of the math.

Public Mark: warm, open-sounding, philosophical, sidestepping the core factual issue.

Why This Matters

This is not about ego. It is about the integrity of the scientific process.

When a public reviewer:

1. Makes a factual misrepresentation (claiming there are no equations when equations are present),

2. Is presented with contrary evidence,

3. Refuses to correct the record,

4. And then reframes the narrative in public without addressing the factual error…

…it damages the trust that underpins scientific discourse.

It also erases the central debate from public view, replacing it with a vague philosophical fog in which the reviewer looks magnanimous and the author looks defensive — even when the truth is the opposite.

The EFMW Equation Deserves a Fair Review

The EFMW framework is ambitious. It:

Proposes a unification of general relativity and quantum mechanics through an information-theoretic lens.

Provides mathematical formalisms, derivations, and falsifiable predictions.

Invites challenge — but honest challenge.

Mark is welcome to critique the EFMW equation on its merits. He is not entitled to dismiss its existence.

The Record, Side-by-Side

Private Statement (Mark):

> “Your equations are not equations simply because they have numbers and letters in them.”

Public Statement (Mark):

> “This isn’t dismissal — it’s an invitation to deepen the dialogue.”

Private Statement (Mark):

> “It’s not my responsibility to do your research for you.”

Public Statement (Mark):

> “Let’s keep the conversation going for the sake of the broader audience witnessing this.”

Conclusion

I am publishing this not out of malice, but to preserve the record and the principle that truth matters more than tone.
When you step into the public arena to critique scientific work, you take on the responsibility to:

Represent it accurately,

Correct your own errors when shown evidence,

And engage with the actual content — not just the parts that fit your rhetorical stance.

Mark Havens has failed to meet that standard here.

The EFMW equation will stand or fall on the strength of its mathematics and its predictions. That is where the discussion should remain.

Falsify it, or validate it. But do not pretend it does not exist.

I have a hunch that Mark is actually not that mathematically literate in physics, but I can’t confirm that until I’ve read his work and seen the lagrangians that he’s come up with to describe the unity of everything as succinctly as I seem to have.
