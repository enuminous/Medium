Naming Death

On Words, Grief, and the Architecture of Remembrance

Naming Death

On Words, Grief, and the Architecture of Remembrance

By Matthew Chenoweth Wright

1. Introduction: The Language of Loss

Grief does not speak in formulas. It does not balance, calculate, or resolve. It spills.

But grief, left unnamed, becomes erosion. It hollows the soul by silence. Words, in their staggering imperfection, are what we build to hold that grief at bay — not to vanquish it, but to structure it. To endure it. To live beside it.

In the London Bills of Mortality for the year 1632, we find a catalogue of deaths. The list is strange, fragmented, beautiful, and harrowing. People perished of “King’s Evil,” “Teeth,” “Rising of the Lights,” and “Affright.” Some were “Bit by a mad dog.” Others were lost to “Worms,” or “Suddenly.”

It is absurd. And it is sacred.

Because what they were doing — these grief-weary scribes — was not simply bookkeeping. They were resisting the oblivion that time demands.

2. The Naming of the Dead

To name the dead is to say: This happened. This person was here. This life mattered.

When we say “Consumption,” we do not just mean tuberculosis. We mean the way breath was stolen from someone’s chest day by day. When we say “Convulsions,” we recall the body trembling, and those helpless to stop it. Even “Made away themselves” — a delicate and tragic euphemism for suicide — tells us of the effort to speak gently of the unbearable.

Naming death is not forensic. It is foundational. It is the first act of civilization. The first story told around fire.

3. Words as Shelter

Grief seeks form because the heart cannot endure formlessness. We bury with ceremony. We speak eulogies. We write obituaries not because they are sufficient, but because they are the only thing we have that even tries.

Language becomes our shelter in the storm of loss.

Even those strange archaic terms — “Lethargie,” “Rupture,” “Swelling,” “Falling Sickness” — were not just diagnoses. They were offerings. The words were rituals, performed so that pain would not disappear unmarked.

This is the same impulse that drives poetry. To give pain a place to rest.

4. The Modern Abyss of Unspoken Grief

Today, we often lose the art of naming death. We wrap grief in euphemism or pathologize it into silence. In our haste to move on, to numb, to distance, we forget that to mourn out loud is not a weakness. It is humanity’s great technology of survival.

We live in a world that records everything and remembers almost nothing. That is why naming matters.

To say, “They died of overdose,” or “They jumped,” or “They faded in a nursing home,” is to carry fire into the dark. It is to make a stand against erasure.

5. Naming as Rebellion, Naming as Prayer

Naming death is a defiant act. It is a rebellion against annihilation. It tells the universe: You may take the body, but not the story.

It is also a kind of prayer. A way to align grief with breath. To tether memory to syllable. To remember the shape of the ones we’ve lost in the outline of a word.

When I say “Jennifer,” or “Millie,” or “Dad,” or “You” — I am not speaking to ghosts. I am protecting them.

6. Conclusion: We Remember Out Loud

In the end, language fails. It cannot return the dead. But it can refuse their disappearance.

The Bills of Mortality from 1632 are not just a ledger. They are a monument. A crooked, aching, misspelled, beautiful monument made of grief and ink.

And they remind us:

Naming death is how we keep it from swallowing memory whole.
